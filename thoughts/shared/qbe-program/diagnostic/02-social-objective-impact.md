# 2. Social Objective and Impact Measurement

> Goods is trying to change a practical health system, not write a social-impact story around a product. The problem is that many remote homes do not have reliable beds, washable bedding, working washing machines, affordable freight or local repair. That creates a chain of harm: people sleep on floors or old mattresses, bedding and clothes are hard to clean, scabies and skin infections are harder to interrupt, money leaves community through unsuitable products, and broken goods become dump material. The current evidence is strongest in community voice, product use, deployment records, photos, videos and the emerging QR asset register. Clinical outcome proof is still being built and should not be overclaimed.

## What This Looks Like In Real Homes

![Woman testing a red Stretch Bed](/images/media-pack/woman-on-red-stretch-bed.jpg)

The problem starts in ordinary household moments. Family comes to visit and there are not enough beds. A child sleeps on the floor. Someone buys a cheap mattress or machine because that is what is available. Freight makes the good option too expensive. The product fails, cannot be repaired locally, and eventually sits at the dump.

The strongest evidence is not abstract. It is what people said when Goods asked the same practical questions in different places.

| Place | What people said | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| Palm Island | Ivy said, "Hardly anyone around the community has beds." After receiving a bed she said it was better than lying on floors and easy to make. | The need is basic, visible and felt inside the home. |
| Palm Island | Alfred Johnson said, "You can't just go down to the store and buy beds. It's a big muck-around." He also said a bed makes you feel safer than sleeping on a couch or the ground. | Freight, access, safety and dignity are part of the same product problem. |
| Tennant Creek | Dianne Stokes said hardly any people around community have beds. She received one bed, then came back asking for 20 more and offered to self-fund. | The product created pull after use, not only interest before delivery. |
| Alice Springs and Homelands | Fred Campbell said families were sleeping on the ground and that being off the ground matters for safety, snakes and movement between places. | Portability and off-ground sleeping are not nice-to-have features. |
| East Arnhem | Jessica Allardyce from Miwatj Health said essential goods are hard to get by barge, scabies is common, and washing machines are essential so infected clothing, bedding and towels can be cleaned. | Washing is health hardware, not convenience. |
| Kalgoorlie | Chloe, an Aboriginal Homeless Fringe Support Worker, said proper bedding improves health, mobility and dignity for people sleeping on floors or unsuitable mattresses. | The bedding problem is also a dignity and rough-sleeping problem. |
| Groote Archipelago | WHSAC requested 500 mattresses and 300 washing machines, noting island freight and lack of competitive pricing. | The same need appears at large scale when a community organisation names it directly. |

## The Health Chain

The health logic is simple enough to explain without making it smaller than it is:

- Bedding and clothes need to be washable.
- If they are not washable, scabies, bed bugs and skin infections are harder to interrupt.
- If skin infections are not interrupted, Strep A and rheumatic fever risk can rise.
- Rheumatic fever can become rheumatic heart disease.
- A bed and washing machine are therefore part of environmental health hardware in the home.

This is why the public Goods line matters:

> "A good bed can prevent heart disease."

That line should always be explained with care. Goods has not completed a clinical study showing that a particular Stretch Bed directly reduces rheumatic heart disease. The responsible claim is that washable beds and reliable washing machines support the environmental-health conditions needed to interrupt known infection pathways.

## What The Current System Gets Wrong

| System gap | What happens now | Goods response |
|---|---|---|
| Beds and mattresses | Standard products are hard to freight, hard to wash, hard to repair and often wrong for heat, dust, crowding and movement. | A flat-packable Stretch Bed with washable canvas, galvanised steel poles and recycled HDPE legs. |
| Washing machines | Machines are sold into remote places at high cost, run far more cycles than metro households, fail quickly, and are hard to service. | Pakkimjalki Kari prototype: commercial Speed Queen base, simplified use, stronger housing, telemetry and a repair-first design direction. |
| Freight and remote retail | People pay a remoteness premium before the product even reaches the home. | On-Country production and local inventory are designed to reduce long-distance supply dependence over time. |
| Waste | Mattresses and white goods are hard to recycle anywhere. In remote locations, burial or dumping is often the default. | Use community plastic waste as feedstock for bed parts, with repair and product tracking built in. |
| Procurement | Lowest sticker price can win even when the product fails quickly and leaves the community with waste. | Make lifecycle value visible: repair, use, survival, waste avoided and local jobs. |
| Story and consent | Community experience can be turned into extractive content. | Storytelling is consent-led. People should control when their story is used and be able to retract it. |

## Circular Economy And Environmental Impact

![Shredded plastic ready for pressing](/images/process/shredded-plastic-tubs.jpg)

The environmental objective is not a separate sustainability story. It is part of the same household system.

Current impact assumptions:

| Metric | Current status |
|---|---|
| Plastic per Stretch Bed | 20kg HDPE per bed in the current product data |
| Historical working estimate | Some documents use 20 to 25kg per bed. Use 20kg externally unless explaining the historical range. |
| Total diverted to date | 9,225kg in the March 2026 compendium. Treat as computed from asset/product assumptions, not an audited waste weighbridge figure. |
| At 5,000 beds per year | About 100 tonnes at 20kg per bed, or 125 tonnes if using the older 25kg assumption. |
| Circular loop | Community waste becomes bed parts, then the product returns to community. |

![Pressed recycled plastic sheets](/images/process/pressed-sheets.jpg)

The production method is what makes the circular claim credible: collect plastic, sort it, shred it, press it into sheets, CNC cut parts, assemble the bed, then track the product.

## What We Measure Today

This section needs to stay strict. These are the things Goods can safely say are being measured or recorded now:

- products delivered,
- communities and partners served,
- direct product feedback from people receiving or using goods,
- partner feedback from people checking in on products,
- photos, videos, interviews and transcripts, subject to consent,
- asset IDs and QR records where products have been entered into the register,
- washing-machine usage where telemetry is connected,
- support, repair or issue information where it is submitted through the product-support workflow.

![Bed QR code on product label](/images/pitch/bed-qr-code.jpg)

The tracking idea is simple: follow the product, not just the order. A bed should be able to become a service record, movement record, repair record, replacement record, story record and procurement signal.

See the public [community tracking model](/wiki/community/tracking-model).

## What Is Being Built Next

The next measurement spine is product-led, not survey-led.

| Measurement piece | What it will show | Status |
|---|---|---|
| QR or ID check-ins | Whether a bed is still in use, moved, damaged, repaired or replaced | Trial and build |
| Repair and replacement requests | What fails, what can be fixed, what needs replacing | Partly live through support pathways |
| Washing-machine telemetry | Usage, heartbeat, possible maintenance triggers | Prototype and connected-machine dependent |
| Daily delivery reviews | What people say while deliveries are happening | Field practice, not yet standardised into one reporting format |
| Partner check-ins | Product condition and use after Ben and Nicholas leave | Relationship-led and needs cleaner process |
| Story consent records | Who has approved what story, image or video for what use | Needs human review before broad publication |
| Product referrals and repeat purchases | Whether people want more after seeing or using the product | Emerging evidence, not yet formal metric |

## ALMA, Story And Consent

ALMA is useful as a lens because it keeps the data human. It should not be presented as if it solves impact measurement by itself.

The impact model should hold three things together:

- product facts: delivery, repair, usage, material, lifespan and waste,
- human facts: safety, dignity, family routines, movement, pride, voice and choice,
- sovereignty facts: who owns the story, who can see it, who can retract it, and who interprets what it means.

This is why SROI should not sit at the centre of the Goods impact story right now. A single dollar-return number can hide the parts people actually talk about: agency, repair, being off the floor, being able to wash when you choose, having a bed for family, and not sending more broken goods to the dump.

## What We Can Safely Claim Now

- Goods is addressing a real and repeated problem: access to appropriate beds, mattresses and washing machines in remote communities.
- The Stretch Bed is a live product with field use, photos, community feedback and current product specifications.
- Pakkimjalki Kari is a prototype washing-machine pathway, not a finished mass-market product.
- Named community members and partners have linked beds and washing machines to safety, dignity, health, freight, family hosting and repair.
- The asset-register and QR approach is a real measurement direction, with product IDs and support pathways already present in the system.
- The circular economy claim is credible at product level because recycled HDPE is an input and the production process has been documented.

## What We Should Not Claim Yet

- Do not claim an independently verified reduction in rheumatic heart disease.
- Do not claim a completed longitudinal sleep or health study.
- Do not claim every future metric is already live.
- Do not claim the 9,225kg plastic-diversion figure is an audited weighbridge number.
- Do not treat all demand signals as signed orders.
- Do not publish named portraits, videos or stories without checking consent status and intended audience.
