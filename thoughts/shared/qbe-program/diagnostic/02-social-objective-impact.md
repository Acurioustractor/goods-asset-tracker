# 2. Social Objective and Impact Measurement

> Can the organisation articulate the social problem being addressed and the gaps in the current system?

## The problem

In remote Aboriginal communities, families sleep on floors. Beds last weeks, not years. Appliances corrode, break, and go unreplaced. Standard consumer goods are not engineered for extreme heat and UV, high-density occupancy, dust and limited sealed flooring, or the absence of repair infrastructure and spare parts.

The downstream harms are documented and measurable:
- **Child exhaustion** feeding into school attendance and outcomes.
- **Chronic health** impacts (sleep, respiratory, infection) compounded by bed bugs and unwashable bedding.
- **Wealth extraction.** Millions of dollars annually flow out of community budgets into replacement furniture that ends up in dumps within months.

## The gap in the current system

1. **Supply side failure.** Mainstream manufacturers do not engineer for remote conditions. Their warranty economics assume return-to-store, which is impossible 2,000km from a retailer.
2. **Procurement failure.** Housing providers buy on lowest unit price, not total cost of ownership. A $200 bed replaced six times in three years costs more than a $600 bed that lasts ten years, but the procurement system does not see it.
3. **Ownership failure.** Communities are treated as recipients of donated goods rather than markets and manufacturers. Profit flows out, not in.
4. **Circular economy failure.** Plastic waste in communities accumulates because the collection-and-reuse loop does not exist locally.

## Our answer

A product-and-ownership model designed as a single system:
- **Product** that survives the conditions (Stretch Bed: 10+ year design life, 200kg capacity, hose-washable, 5-minute assembly, every part replaceable).
- **Local input loop** (HDPE collected in community, shredded and moulded locally into bed components).
- **Ownership transfer** (40% profit share; production plants designed for community purchase; PICC signalled intent to buy).
- **Storytelling as sovereignty** (Empathy Ledger captures consented community voice, giving communities control of their own narrative about what works).

## Impact measurement: the ALMA framework

We use ACT's ALMA (Adaptive Learning for Meaningful Accountability) framework. Rather than forcing every insight into a single metric, ALMA asks six signal questions of every data point:

| Signal | Question |
|---|---|
| Authority | Who holds decision-making power over this data? |
| Evidence | What type of evidence is this? |
| Harm Risk | What is the risk of sharing it? |
| Capability | How ready is the system to act on it? |
| Option Value | Does this open future possibilities? |
| Community Value Return | Does value flow back to the community? |

This is a departure from SROI-style single-number approaches. It is designed to respect Indigenous data sovereignty (see OCAP principles) rather than override it.

## Metrics we track

**Environmental**
- Plastic diverted from landfill (kg/bed, total)
- Mattresses/beds kept out of dumps
- Carbon footprint per bed (under development)

**Health and housing**
- Sleep quality, self-reported via story
- Bed bug incidents reduced
- Healthy Homes checklist items addressed

**Economic and social**
- Jobs created On-Country (collection, shredding, assembly)
- Revenue retained locally (% and absolute)
- Skills training hours delivered
- Community ownership percentage (path to 100%)

**Story-based**
- Empathy Ledger storytellers (currently 33 Goods-connected)
- Consented photo and voice records
- Bed journey tracking (each bed has an ID and a story)

## Live numbers (early 2026)

- 389 products deployed
- 8 communities served
- 1,000+ lives directly impacted
- 9,225 kg plastic waste diverted
- $537K cumulative revenue (ACT-GD cost code, Xero)
- 33 community storytellers on Empathy Ledger

## Theory of change (one paragraph)

IF we build products that actually survive remote conditions AND we run the production loop On-Country AND we transfer ownership to community over time AND we capture impact evidence with consent, THEN families get durable furniture at fair prices, waste stays out of dumps, jobs and wealth stay local, and communities control the narrative of what the work means. The alternative (charity-framed donations of cheap goods) is the status quo we are displacing.

## Gaps we are honest about

- **No independent outcomes evaluation yet.** Self-report and story are our current evidence base. An external evaluation is planned for 2026-27, likely through University of Melbourne (existing design research partnership) or similar.
- **No longitudinal sleep-quality data.** We have testimony, not clinical evidence.
- **Impact measurement capacity is under-resourced** internally.
