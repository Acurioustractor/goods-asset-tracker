The spine

"Goods turns community knowledge into health hardware, local work, and production that communities can own." Ben's model line, verbatim, 9 Jul workshop

496 beds and 16 washing machines across 9 communities, paid for by real buyers, at a $750 bed price with break-even falling to 338 beds a year. Every claim traces to the public register; every voice carries a consent tier in the Empathy Ledger.

Six acts, all sourced

Act 1 · The why

A bed off the ground, washable bedding and a working washer interrupt the scabies-to-rheumatic-heart-disease pathway. Always the why, never a claimed clinical outcome until evaluated. External evidence (Barunga laundry 60% scabies drop) cited strictly as AIG/KPMG's, not ours.

Act 2 · Designed in community

Warumungu Elder Dianne Stokes designed both products and named the washing machine Pakkimjalki Kari. The Bloomfield family built the five current machines with Nic in late January 2026. Never "co-design". Never "helping".

Act 3 · Real demand

Snow $110,000 and $16,600. Julalikari $19,800. Our Community Shed $6,765. Centrecorp $85,712. All paid. Utopia took 60 beds in October 2025 and asked for 160 more five weeks later. PICC authorised a 40-bed order and wants the production facility itself.

Act 4 · Unit economics must be real

Bed price $750. Marginal cost falls 685 to 426 to 421 as production scales. Break-even falls from 1,679 to 338 beds a year. "Target is hope. Number is fact." The delivered-cost-per-bed extract is the trust asset QBE has asked for, and it is currently blocked.

Act 5 · Handover is the test

Community ownership is binary, dated and signed: four month-6 checkpoints (factory control, named lead on payroll, community entity invoices the buyer, majority community-controlled production). Today it is honestly "a pathway moving closer to community ownership, never claimed as complete" (Ben's language lock, 10 Jul). Charity home: The Butterfly Movement Ltd, Kristy Bloomfield founding director.

Act 6 · The ask

AU$400,000 of signed, match-eligible catalytic capital by 31 August 2026 (SEFA $300K repayable anchor + Snow $100K + Centrecorp $75K), unlocking a Stage 2 application in September, outcome November. 32 consent-cleared community voices verify every claim through the Empathy Ledger.

Style verdict from the research

  • Market-first, not wound-first. Open on First Nations business as a growth economy that mid-market demand cannot reach. Deficit-first framing is the penalised register post-referendum. First Australians Capital, the closest funded analogue, wins with "60,000+ years of enterprise".
  • Leverage is the funder's own grammar. Catalysing Impact's proudest KPI is the 3.7x stack. Build the deck spine as a capital-stack table and mirror the KPI back. Careful: 3.7x is stack over grants; the external-only multiple is 2.7x.
  • First Nations businesses are the grammatical subject of every impact sentence. ACT appears only as plumbing: removes barriers, de-risks, routes demand. Purge "we empower", "beneficiaries", "on behalf of".
  • Answer black cladding before it's asked. Indigenous decision rights with real veto, transparent capped intermediary margin, benefit-sharing percentages, dated handover architecture. The cohort includes Indigenous-led Ngardara; ACT will be read side by side.
  • One braided story. The program officer needs a two-minute retellable story plus board-safe numbers. One consented, named thread (Dianne Stokes is the strongest cleared voice) woven through the numbers, with Empathy Ledger's auditable consent named as the provenance layer. No competitor shows funders a live consent chain inside the pitch.
  • Mint one sticky ratio. Funders repeat one number verbatim for years (Supply Nation's $4.41 is still quoted by PRF). Candidate: dollars of buyer demand routed per $1 of philanthropy, with a provenance note. Never publish a social-return number you can't source.

Five corrections before any external use
1. "3.7x leverage" is total-stack-over-grants per SIH's wording; external-only leverage is 2.7x. Don't misframe.
2. Closing the Gap Priority Reform 3 is "Transforming Government Organisations", not "mainstream organisations". PR2 (community-controlled sector) and PR4 (shared access to data) are the two Goods enacts.
3. The AIATSIS "including commercialisation" agreements clause is Responsibility 1.8a, not 1.9 (1.9 is the group-FPIC requirement).
4. "Philanthropy, Our Way": the correct wording is "donor agendas are prioritised over Indigenous self-determination", and "from giving to us, to investing with us" is Dr Kelvin Kong's line. Quote exactly or not at all.
5. The ~0.2% Indigenous-philanthropy figure is not on the Productivity Commission report page. Re-source it or drop it. Also: the Generation One brand was rolled into broader Minderoo; verify naming before it appears in any Minderoo material.
