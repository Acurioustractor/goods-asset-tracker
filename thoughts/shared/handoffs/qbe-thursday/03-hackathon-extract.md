## Part 1 — How the Hotel Etico challenge was framed

This is the reference brief verbatim in structure. Every point below is drawn from the source doc; nothing is invented.

**The social enterprise (context).** Hotel Etico is Australia's first not-for-profit social enterprise hotel. It employs and trains young people with intellectual disabilities in hospitality and independent living. It is a boutique 15-room hotel in Mount Victoria in the Blue Mountains, running a live-in training program called the Academy of Independence. Trainees get on-the-job experience, are paid full award wages from day one, and graduate into long-term employment with industry partners. Since 2020 it has supported 44 graduates with an 82% post-program employment rate. It has won the Australian Human Rights Award.

**The audience / stakeholder.** Two stakeholders sit at the centre of the challenge: Hotel Etico itself (which needs midweek revenue to fund its training model) and the corporate client it wants to attract (businesses looking for purposeful offsites and a way to meet their CSR / ESG goals).

**The situation.** Occupancy is low Monday to Thursday, which hurts the financial sustainability of the Mount Victoria site. Weekends are strong (over 80% occupancy in April 2025, better than regional averages), but midweek bookings stay low. Other revenue streams (restaurant/bar, NDIS funding) are capped by scale, pricing rules, and fixed costs. The hotel needs new commercial segments during the week to improve cashflow and fund its training and inclusion work.

**The problem statement (the framed question).** "How might we attract corporate clients to stay at Hotel Etico from Monday to Thursday, and what offerings, services, or business models could support this shift?" The headline challenge statement was phrased as: "How might Hotel Etico increase midweek occupancy by attracting corporate clients to its Mount Victoria site?"

**The value (why it matters).** Better midweek occupancy lifts revenue, covers operating costs, and grows the employment model. For corporates, Hotel Etico offers a socially conscious alternative to a generic offsite venue: a way to support disability inclusion, lift staff engagement, and meet ESG commitments through travel and event choices. A working model could also inform Hotel Etico's national growth (new sites planned in Canberra and Leura).

**Past approaches (so teams don't repeat them).** The hotel has focused on leisure and tourism. It has done local community groups and weddings, but never a tailored corporate package, and it has lacked the resources to drive that side of the business. Its award-winning model has had media attention but has not yet been used as a CSR hook for business travel or offsites.

**The constraints.** As stated in the doc:
- Single site in Mount Victoria with 15 rooms, which limits scale.
- No full-time event coordinator or corporate sales function in place.
- Weekday packages must fit trainee schedules and protect the training focus of the hotel.
- New offerings must be cost-neutral or revenue-generating. (The source doc ends mid-sentence on this last constraint.)

**The deliverable teams produced.** The brief is written as a "How might we" design challenge for teams to answer with offerings, services, or business models. So the implied deliverable is a pitched proposal: a corporate midweek offer (what it is, who it targets, how it is packaged and sold) that respects the constraints above. Note: the source doc does not spell out a formal deliverable format (slides, pitch length, judging rubric), so this is inferred from the challenge wording, not stated.

## Part 2 — Why it worked for a one-day process

- **Accessible without deep sector knowledge.** Everyone in a corporate room already understands hotels, offsites, midweek vs weekend, and CSR. No one needs hospitality expertise to have a useful idea. The mission (jobs for young people with disability) is easy to grasp and emotionally clear.
- **One clear audience and one clear problem.** It is a single question with a single target customer (the corporate client) and a single measurable goal (midweek occupancy, Monday to Thursday). Teams never have to guess what "good" looks like.
- **Broad enough for creative thinking.** "What offerings, services, or business models" invites packages, partnerships, pricing, marketing, and storytelling. There are many right answers, so teams can diverge.
- **Specific enough to land in one day.** The 15-room, single-site, midweek frame is concrete. Ideas stay grounded and testable rather than drifting into strategy abstractions.
- **Likely to produce practical recommendations.** The hard constraints (cost-neutral, fits trainee schedules, no sales team yet) force teams toward realistic, do-able ideas, not blue-sky concepts the enterprise could never run.
- **A real, current pain with a clear stakeholder benefit.** The enterprise actually needs this, and the value to both sides (hotel revenue, corporate ESG) is spelled out, so teams feel the work matters.

The takeaway for Goods: pick ONE stakeholder and ONE clearly framed problem, make it human and easy to picture, keep it broad in the "how" but tight in the "who/what", and add two or three honest constraints so the ideas stay buildable.

## Part 3 — Three draft Goods on Country hackathon challenges (PROPOSALS)

These are clearly-labelled proposals for the program lead to choose from, built against the stated criteria: (a) accessible without deep sector knowledge, (b) clear audience or problem, (c) broad enough for creative thinking, (d) specific enough for tangible one-day ideas, (e) likely to lead to practical recommendations. All three deliberately avoid specialist asks (no sales-ops playbook, no LOI templates) and lean human-centred and tangible. Pick one; do not run all three.

About Goods on Country (shared context for whichever is chosen): a social enterprise delivering washable, repairable, recycled-plastic beds and washing machines to remote Indigenous communities. The endgame is community-owned, on-Country production: communities collecting plastic, making the goods, and owning the plant. Design-thinking day flow: understand the challenge, explore stakeholder needs, define the problem, generate ideas, pitch a practical solution.

### Draft A — The first night with a new bed
**Framing.** A family in a remote community is about to receive a Goods bed for the first time. Picture the whole moment, from the truck arriving, to opening the flat-pack, to putting it together, to the first night's sleep, to keeping it clean and working for years. Right now the unboxing, set-up, and care experience is not designed with that family at the centre. How might we make receiving, setting up, and owning a Goods bed feel simple, welcoming, and built to last, for someone who has never seen one before?
**Audience / stakeholder.** A remote community family receiving a bed for the first time (the everyday end user, including kids and elders).
**Deliverable teams pitch.** A reimagined "first experience" for a new bed: what is in the box, the set-up moment, the welcome or instructions, and the care-and-repair touchpoints over the first year, presented as a simple journey with one or two standout ideas.
**Why it fits a one-day non-specialist group.** Everyone has unpacked and set up something at home, so anyone can contribute. The problem is human, visual, and easy to roleplay, and it generates concrete, low-cost ideas fast.

### Draft B — Help a community tell its own impact story
**Framing.** Goods works because of what happens in community: beds that get used and washed, machines that keep running, plastic that gets turned into something useful, jobs that stay local. But that story is usually told for the community by outsiders, in funder reports. How might we help a community capture and share its own impact, in its own voice, in a way that is true, easy to keep doing, and respects who owns the story?
**Audience / stakeholder.** Community members who live the impact and want to tell their own story (and the partners and funders who need to hear it, secondhand).
**Deliverable teams pitch.** A simple, repeatable way for a community to gather and share its own impact, such as a lightweight format, prompt set, or sharing channel, with attention to consent and ownership, pitched as something a community could actually run with low resources.
**Why it fits a one-day non-specialist group.** Everyone understands storytelling and "show, don't tell." It needs empathy and creativity, not sector data, and it lands naturally on tangible, human ideas.

### Draft C — Make "community-owned" make sense to an outsider
**Framing.** Goods' endgame is community ownership: communities collecting the plastic, making the beds and machines, and owning the production plant on Country. To a corporate partner, funder, or new customer, "community-owned production" can sound abstract. How might we make that idea instantly clear and compelling to someone who has never been to a remote community, so they get why it matters and what their support actually builds?
**Audience / stakeholder.** An outsider who needs to understand the model: a corporate partner, funder, or first-time customer.
**Deliverable teams pitch.** A way to explain community ownership that an outsider gets in under a minute: an analogy, a visual, a short experience, or a simple framing device, pitched with the one moment that makes it click.
**Why it fits a one-day non-specialist group.** The QBE room IS the outsider audience, so they are the perfect test users. Explaining a concept clearly is a universal skill, and it produces sharp, presentable ideas in a day.

---

## Recommendation note
Draft A is the closest match to why Hotel Etico worked: one stakeholder, one vivid moment, broad in the "how" but tight in the "who/what," and it produces buildable ideas. Draft C is the strongest if the goal is to also sharpen how Goods pitches itself, since the QBE participants double as the real audience. Draft B is the most open-ended and the best fit if the room skews creative or communications-minded. The program lead should pick one and add two or three honest Goods constraints (for example: low cost, works with patchy connectivity or remote logistics, must respect community consent and ownership) to keep ideas buildable, the same move that made Hotel Etico land.
