## (c) Recommendation — TWO drawings, with a conditional third

**Carry 2 drawings. Beat 05 (the plastic loop) and beat 11 (handover).**

- Beat 05 → `model-diagrams/02-loop-on-country.png` (fallback `process-anchors/01-plastic-loop-v2.png` only if the hands are removed).
- Beat 11 → `model-diagrams/04-handover-gates.png`.

**Why two.** Both are the only beats depicting something that cannot be photographed: a cycle, and a future state. Every other beat either has a real photo already shot (02, 03, 06, 07, 12 — the `design/deck-photos/` set is locked and staged) or is made of numbers, and numbers are barred from generated art by rule 1, which pushes beats 04, 08, 09 and 10 to native charts by construction. There is no third beat where a drawing beats the alternative on merit.

The failure mode of a drawing per beat is not ugliness, it is flatness: twelve illustrations in one hand read as a brand deck rather than a record of work that actually happened. Goods' strongest evidence is photographic — 540 beds exist, the press ran at Maningrida, people built these. Every drawing that displaces a photo trades proof for decoration. Two drawings also read as deliberate: the eye registers them as "this one is a concept, not a place", which is exactly the signal beats 05 and 11 need.

**The conditional third: beat 01.** If the opener needs the handover thesis stated visually rather than in a line of type, `model-diagrams/05-taper-scaffold.png` (propped up → fewer props → stands alone) is the cleanest one-look statement of the whole thesis and carries no figures. Take it only if it *replaces* the beat-10 chart's metaphor duty rather than doubling it — running scaffold at 01 and the taper chart at 10 is coherent (metaphor opens, data closes), running both at 10 is redundant. Ceiling either way: **3**.

**Do not carry** `01-five-moves` or `03-three-doors` (both restate the spine other slides already carry) or the ownership D/E stages (parked, and beat 11's gates diagram does the job better).
