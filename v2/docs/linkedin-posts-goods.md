### 1. Stretch Bed Origin Story (Nick Cave post)

Nick Cave talks about how making something - a song, a letter, a line - is itself the act of thinking. You don't figure it out first and then build. You build, and the understanding comes through the work.

That's been the Stretch Bed.

We didn't start with a product. We started with conversations around fires in Tennant Creek. Elder Dianne Stokes received one of the first basket beds told us clearly.

"That's comfortable, that's what I have been looking for".

Ivy on Palm Island told us: "Hardly anyone around the community has beds. When family comes to visit, people sleep on the floor."

Not by choice. By absence.

Then communities pushed us further. "We want it high off the ground." "We don't want the foam." "We want to move it around." "We want a MAD bed."

So we built. Recycled HDPE plastic legs pressed from waste. Galvanised steel poles. Heavy-duty canvas. 26kg, flat-packs, no tools, 5 minutes to assemble. Every bed diverts 20kg of plastic from landfill.

Each version got closer because the people using them were designing them. Not recipients. Co-designers.

The philosophy came through the making:

Our job is to become unnecessary.

Build a containerised factory that turns community plastic into bed components. Train local people to run it. Transfer ownership. Step back.

We're not there yet.

400+ beds across 8 communities. A production plant doing ~30 beds a week. Prototypes being tested in conditions no lab can replicate - 40°C heat, red dust, bore water.

We are sharing this because I want to find people who feel it too. The ones who cut through the noise towards something real. Community connection. Innovation that actually serves. Understanding that goes deeper than a slide deck.

If that's you...we'd love to know where you are in your journey.

### 6. Tennant Creek / Patricia Frank / Wilya Janta / Mud Brick

🪨 "This is the heaviest seat I've ever sat on," I said, settling onto a mud brick in Tennant Creek.

What happened next challenged everything I thought I knew about social innovation.

The design principle that changes everything: "If we were focused on empowering the community to solve these challenges themselves, what reframing would we do?"

Patricia Frank, a Warumungu Elder, wasn't asking for housing solutions. She was building a movement.

The Reframe in Action:
❌ Old Frame: "What makes your housing solution different?"
✅ New Frame: "How can your wisdom help other communities design their own solutions?"

Patricia's response floored me:
"We want to help other language groups, other cultures... how THEY want to design their house using THEIR culture, THEIR language, THEIR climate."

5 powerful reframes I witnessed:
1. From Beneficiary → to Knowledge Holder Patricia: "We've done cultural awareness already, so we know what direction we're going."
2. From Competition → to Collaboration Not "fund us instead of them" but "we'll teach other mobs how to do this for themselves"
3. From Housing → to Holistic Solutions One conversation covered shelter, washing machines, cultural tourism, bush medicine, and economic development
4. From Rigid Programs → to Adaptive Innovation "I wanna make a couch out of it instead of a bed" - communities reshape solutions to fit their actual needs
5. From Charity → to Partnership "You're here to help us, not use us up" - the difference between extraction and collaboration

The Snow Foundation gets it:
They didn't arrive with solutions. They arrived with questions:
- "How does housing connect to health outcomes?"
- "Who else is part of this ecosystem?"
- "What have you learned that others should know?"

The Result?

Not just better houses.

A model that the Wilya Janta team of Simon Quilty, Jimmy Frank Jupurrurla, Norman Frank Jupurrurla, Patricia and many more of the Tennant Creek community is leading:

Every community designs for their own climate and culture
- Knowledge flows between communities
- Solutions multiply rather than compete
- Local expertise drives innovation

The Mud Brick Lesson:
That uncomfortable seat taught me comfort isn't the goal - transformation is. When we reframe from "solving problems FOR communities" to "empowering communities to solve their own challenges," everything shifts.

Patricia's vision isn't just about Wilya Janta. It's about First Nations people across the NT becoming architects of their own futures.

"Someone needed to start somewhere and we're starting somewhere."

Witnessed during field work with A Curious Tractor and the Snow Foundation in Tennant Creek, NT. Patricia Frank's wisdom shared with permission and deep respect.

Listen to Patricia and other community voices from Tennant Creek at - https://lnkd.in/gB4xYRG5

### 7. Norman Frank Jupurrurla / Wilya Janta

"Without your culture, you feel lost"
 - Norman Frank Jupurrurla

Today I want to honour a Warumungu Elder whose vision is transforming lives in Tennant Creek and beyond.

Twenty years ago, Norman lived in a tin shed with one power point and one tap.

His friend Simon Quilty lived across the road in the doctor's house. When Simon promised to help build him a proper house one day, Norman laughed: "In your dreams, mate."

But dreams grounded in culture and community have power. Today, Norman chairs Wilya Janta - "standing strong as one" - creating culturally designed housing that honours how Aboriginal families actually live, move, and care for each other.

What strikes me most about Norman's approach:

🏠 Culture First: Every design element considers kinship systems, ceremony, and climate - "We work with how we sleep, how we move around the house with our people"

🔄 Generational Thinking: "I'm just opening the gateway for them" - building not just houses but pathways for his children and grandchildren to lead

🤝 True Partnership: Two decades of friendship and collaboration, respecting different knowledge systems while working as one

The Wilya Janta logo - ancient snakes from Norman's Country that adorned shields for thousands of years - now appears on letterheads and shirts. Ancient wisdom meeting modern solutions.

Norman's vision extends beyond Tennant Creek: "Not only for us here today... it's for everybody, for all different tribes."

This is what self-determination looks like: Communities designing their own futures, honouring culture while embracing innovation, transforming "backwards" living into dignified homes.

Thank you Norman, for showing us that standing strong together means never forgetting where you come from while building where you're going. 🙏

I am excited to help warlukun (start a fire), were we all blow together to make sure that these ideas flame to life. 🔥

### 8. Oonchiumpa / Xavier / CONTAINED Room 3

Last week I asked a 16-year-old in Alice Springs why young people get in trouble.

She didn't pause.

"Oppression."

She's been to Darwin youth detention. 1,500km from her family. Twelve-minute phone calls. Two-hour waits between calls.

She didn't say "boredom." She didn't say "bad choices." She said the word that every inquiry has circled around for decades without landing on.

She's 16.

After my last post about Diagrama in Spain, a lot of people asked: does anything like this exist here?

There is a version of that answer told through numbers. 1,076 community-led alternatives documented.

But numbers are a translation. Something always gets lost in the translation.

Let me tell you the fuller version.

In Alice Springs, an organisation called Oonchiumpa has been doing this work for years. 100% Aboriginal community-controlled. Founded by Kristy Bloomfield a Central Arrernte woman whose grandfather was a stolen generation man taken to the Bungalow in Alice Springs.

Tanya Turner, co-founder, graduated from UWA law school, worked as an Associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria...and came home. Because these are her kids. Her Country. Her responsibility.

Together they built something the system has never managed to build: trust.

Fred Campbell, one of their case workers, told me about a young man named Xavier. Other services didn't share his disability information. When the relationship got hard, they "distanced."

Oonchiumpa stayed. For years.

"He trusts us. We earned that trust."

When Xavier got the chance to build a bed from recycled plastic (a Stretch Bed designed for and with remote communities) he led the work. Despite reports about his "ability to understand," he knew exactly what he was doing.

He got paid. Bought shoes. Couldn't stop smiling.

J is 14. Lives with his grandfather. Been to detention. When I asked what he'd tell politicians, he said one word:

"Programs."

When I asked what would stop him getting in trouble:

"Looking after my family."

Oonchiumpa's numbers:

→ 95% diversion success
→ 72% of disengaged youth back in education
→ 97.6% cheaper than detention
→ Operation Luna: 21 high-risk young people on the NT Police list. By December, one remained.

But the number that matters most is the one you can't measure. The years Fred spent earning Xavier's trust. The midnight phone calls Kristy answers. The rides to school that nobody sees.

This is what Room 3 of CONTAINED is for.

Room 3 changes at every stop because the alternative isn't one model. It's hundreds of organisations in every community already doing the work.

Oonchiumpa will fill Room 3 when CONTAINED reaches Alice Springs. In Mount Druitt, it's Mounty Aboriginal Youth & Community Services. In Townsville, it's Palm Island Community Company.

In each city, the local community tells its own story.

Room 3 is theirs.

Find Oonchiumpa's full story here: https://lnkd.in/d9YV3KnJ

### 9. Fury vs Future / Tennant Creek Beds

Thanks to Nicholas Marchesi OAM (Him/He) for encouraging me to post about this.

Two posts I shared last week - one about vision and sovereignty, another about rage and systems that devour children - revealed something profound about how we metabolise change.

The anger post ignited like dry grass. Hundreds of responses. Fierce engagement. Comment threads that pulsed with righteous fury. But anger, while necessary for awakening, can become its own territory - a landscape where we exhaust ourselves arguing about problems rather than nurturing solutions.

The sovereignty post - Shaun Fisher vision of oyster restoration as cultural reclamation - received gentler attention. Thoughtful nods. But not the volcanic engagement that accompanies outrage.

Here's what I'm learning from communities who've mastered transformation: fury and future aren't opposing forces, they're sequential territories in the same journey.

Uncle George in Mount Isa knows this. His decades of youth work began with rage at a system that criminalised his people's children. But he learned to channel that fire into daily presence - showing up, creating circles where young people find their strength through connection rather than confrontation.

On Palm Island, PICC's sovereignty didn't emerge from anger alone but from the patient, methodical work of building alternative infrastructure. 197 local jobs. Community-controlled everything. Rage transformed into regeneration.

In Tennant Creek, housing solutions aren't born from debating policy failures but from designing dignity - beds built with community hands, spaces that honour both need and culture.

These communities have mastered the alchemy: converting outrage into outcome.

So here's my commitment: I want to share more stories from the territories beyond anger. The landscapes where solutions breathe freely. Where community wisdom shapes tomorrow rather than argues about yesterday.

Not because fury isn't necessary - it is the lightning that illuminates what needs changing. But because lightning without thunder, without the sustained rumble of constructive action, remains only spectacle.

The same energy that drives us to share devastating statistics can fuel revolutionary support for communities already building answers.

What if we loved solutions as fiercely as we hate problems?

The communities I'm privileged to learn from don't need our anger - they need our attention, our resources, our willingness to follow their lead toward futures we can barely imagine.

This is my invitation to myself, and to anyone still listening: let's practice loving the future as intensely as we despise the present.

The transformation we seek lives in that tender territory between rage and restoration.

### 10. Palm Island Elders

I've been travelling with the Palm Island Elders who make up the Palm Island Community Company Elders Advisory Group - out to homelands, into the ordinary hours, the long stretches where truth has room to breathe.

We sat and we yarned. Sometimes in the heat. Sometimes in the bus, between stops. Sometimes in the quiet after the stories have landed and nobody rushes to fill the space. And in that space, something keeps happening to me: my own identity gets rearranged. Not in a dramatic way. More like a steady hand on the shoulder - turning me back toward what matters.

Palm Island holds some of the hardest history I've ever heard, and some of the deepest dignity I've ever witnessed. The two sit side by side. There's no performance in it. No need to polish the truth. Just people carrying Country, family, memory, and responsibility - still standing, still guiding, still laughing when laughter is deserved.

I don't have the words for what it means to be welcomed into that circle, except gratitude - and a kind of reverence that makes you speak more carefully, listen more slowly, and feel the weight of what you've been trusted with.

To every Elder who has shared time, stories, and presence: thank you. To everyone who makes these journeys possible: thank you. I feel honoured to be included, and I feel the responsibility that comes with it.

I'm quietly excited to share more about these remarkable people because the world needs to learn from them. Not as a "lesson" packaged up neatly, but as a living invitation: to understand history, to deepen connection, and to walk forward with pride, humility, and respect.

### 11. Farm My School / Colac (Goods adjacent — ACT)

What happens when 600 people pick up shovels instead of waiting for permission?

This weekend in Colac, Nicholas Marchesi OAM (Him/He) and I got the chance to plan, drive a tractor and dig. We didn't need through planning documents or strategy sessions, just dirt under our fingernails and shared sweat. James McLennan, the Farm My School team and the Colac community turned an underused school ground into productive land in 24 hours – but that's not the real story.

The real story is watching a community realise they don't need anyone's permission to feed themselves. Watching kids who've never held a seedling suddenly understand where food comes from. Watching volunteers who arrived as strangers leave as co-owners of something tangible.

The Farm my school team and legend of all legends Costa Georgiadis brought their wisdom, but they weren't the teachers – the soil was. Every raised bed we built was a question: What if schools grew food? What if communities owned their food systems? What if the solution was this simple all along?

At A Curious Tractor we talk about handing over the keys rather than driving. Farm My School has taken this further – they're showing communities they already had the keys. They just needed someone to point out the ignition.

The Kubota Tractor Corporation tractor was beautiful (yes, Nic drove it), but the real power take-off happened when 600 people connected their energy to transform a space. No extractive systems. No dependency creation. Just communities building what they need with their own hands.

This model is designed to replicate and obsolete itself. Train the school, build the infrastructure, then step back. Let the community own it fully. That's the kind of innovation that makes existing models nervous.

See more and support Farm my School at www.farmmyschool.com

### 7. RHD / Washing & Health (Orange Sky overlap with remote communities)

My Grandmother had RHD, Former Prime Minister Kevin Rudd too.

It was a common sight to see across the country's health system during the 1940s and 1950s, but improved access to health care has nearly eradicated the condition for non-Indigenous Australians.

Acute rheumatic fever is caused by untreated group A streptococcal infections, and if left untreated can lead to RHD, which could cause heart failure and stroke.

The bacteria are easily spread by a lack of proper washing facilities and overcrowded housing, increasing the risk.

We can all play a small part in changing these environmental living conditions, so proud to see the work of North West Hospital and Health Service and Orange Sky Australia here

https://lnkd.in/g9YGM33P

### 8. Fiji Fellowship — Health Hardware

For many of us, Fiji is the location for a relaxing getaway or of fond family memories; however, many people across Fiji struggle with accessing facilities to wash clothing and bedding.

As part of an Orange Sky Fellowship opportunity to focus on deep learning, I recently visited to learn more about "partnerships for health". I had the chance to meet with incredible people from a range of organisations and sectors including the Fiji Ministry of Health, ADRA, International Red Cross, Save the Children, Rotary, Habitat for Humanity, National RHD Programs and Unicef.

My key learnings were:
- Access to health hardware is a challenge for many people, including people who sleep rough, people who live in informal settlements or remote villages
- Poor access to health hardware can affect wellbeing
- While many see this as a priority, access to clean drinking water is an even greater priority
- Community led, evidence based and less technological solutions are important
- There are many local, national and international agencies creating impactful change so partnerships and working alongside Ministry Departments is critical

While I saw obvious differences, there were also similarities with the experiences of people in remote communities across Australia. This opportunity and learnings from new regions can help Orange Sky tailor impact in the areas we currently operate and direct longer term action, growth and strategy in an impactful and sustainable way.

Thank you so much to all who helped with learnings and connections as well as to those who I met, I'm so grateful for your time and wisdom - you are all doing impactful work creating positive change.
