03 / What is Goods

We make beds, washing machines, and refrigerators with First Nations communities. Then we transfer the manufacturing to community ownership.

  • A social enterprise, not a charity.
  • Co-designed with Elders, made On-Country.
  • Our job is to become unnecessary.

05 / Pakkimjalki Kari

Pakkimjalki Kari.

Named in Warumungu language by Elder Dianne Stokes. Commercial-grade Speed Queen base. Recycled HDPE plastic enclosure. One-button operation. In prototype with several communities.

"They truly wanna a washing machine to wash their blanket, to wash their clothes, and it's right there at home."
Patricia Frank, Tennant Creek
06 / How it's made

Six steps. Designed to move On-Country.

  • 1. Source. Collect plastic from communities.
  • 2. Process. Shred, melt, press into sheets.
  • 3. Cut. CNC router shapes the bed legs.
  • 4. Build. Hydraulic press forms the legs.
  • 5. Assemble. Poles thread through canvas, legs click on.
  • 6. Deliver. Family assembles in five minutes.

Two shipping containers. ~$100K invested already (TFN and ACT). ~30 beds per week when deployed for two months. The plant is designed to move to community ownership.

09 / The model

Enterprise transferred to community ownership.

  • We sell the Stretch Bed. Revenue funds the work.
  • The plant is designed to move to community ownership.
  • Communities co-design, name, manufacture, sell.
  • We become unnecessary.
Pathways
1. Sponsor beds (philanthropy)
2. Buy beds (procurement)
3. Host or own a plant (enterprise transfer)
4. Catalytic capital (blended finance)
