# Theory of Change

> If Goods can make products people actually want, make them survive remote conditions, make them washable and repairable, and move more of the production loop On-Country, then families get better household goods, communities keep more value, and less product ends up buried in dumps. The evidence has to come from use, feedback, repair, repeat demand and consented story rather than our own claims.

## Plain version

People in remote communities are already buying or receiving beds, mattresses, washing machines and appliances. Too many of those products are unsuitable, overpriced, unrepairable and short-lived.

Goods changes the chain:

1. Listen to community about what is needed and what fails.
2. Design or adapt the product for those conditions.
3. Use materials that can be washed, repaired and reused.
4. Move production and repair closer to community.
5. Build local work and, over time, local ownership.
6. Track what happens through feedback, product records and consented stories.

## Three shifts

**Material:** unsuitable products are replaced by durable, washable and repairable goods.

**Economic:** more money and capability stays local through collection, production, repair and ownership.

**Story:** community members control how their experience is recorded, shared and interpreted.

## What we can claim now

- Products are in communities.
- Feedback has already changed product design.
- People and organisations are asking for more.
- The Stretch Bed is the current commercial product.
- Washing machines are still prototype and too expensive for the end goal.

## What we are still proving

- Long-term product life in many communities.
- Resident purchase at the right price.
- Local plant economics.
- Health outcomes beyond plausible pathways and lived feedback.
- A repeatable governance model for community-owned production.
