Goods on Country · Investor brief · 2026

We press recycled-plastic beds On Country, and build the factory so community owns it.

A good bed is health hardware, not furniture. Goods turns local plastic waste into durable, washable beds — and hands the means of making them to the communities that need them.

Raising AU$400,000 in catalytic capital that converts into community ownership.

The problem

Thousands of people in remote Australia sleep on the floor tonight.

The freight tax

“You can’t just go down to the store and buy beds. It’s a big muck-around. You have to bring them on the barge, pay for freight, and still, not everyone gets one.”

Alfred Johnson, Palm Island

Beds that fail in months

Furniture freighted into community is built for suburbs. It breaks, it can’t be washed, and the real remote cost is buying it again and again.

The pattern across communities

Sleep you can’t clean

Off-the-ground, washable sleep supports the conditions needed to interrupt the scabies→rheumatic-heart-disease pathway. This is the why — not a claimed outcome.

Health rationale

How it’s solved today — badly

The current answer is charity drops and freighted goods that end up in the dump.

One Alice Springs supplier sells ~AU$3M of washing machines into remote communities a year — most reach the dump within months.
Bed drops & hardship grants move product once, leave no jobs, no ownership, and no way to fix or replace it locally.
Do nothing means the floor, a couch, or a mattress on the ground — the workaround most people are living with right now.

The demand is already proven — it’s just being met by things that don’t last and money that leaves the community.

The Stretch Bed

Collect the plastic. Press the parts. Build a bed that lasts a decade.

1 · Recycled HDPE legsTwo crossed-plank X-trestles pressed On Country from 20kg of diverted plastic.
2 · Two steel polesGalvanised poles thread through the leg holes and the canvas sleeves.
3 · Structural canvasTensioning the canvas braces the frame — no tools, ~5 minutes, it won’t stand without it.
200kg capacity 26kg, flat-packable 10+ year life 20kg plastic / bed

The magic isn’t the bed. It’s that the making belongs On Country — the plant can move to community ownership.

Why now

Three shifts make this buildable now — and not before.

Technology

Shred-melt-press micro-manufacturing is now small, cheap and mobile enough to run On Country — the plant can sit where the waste and the need are.

Policy & procurement

Closing the Gap and First Nations procurement targets are moving institutional buyers from grant-funded hand-outs toward buying from Indigenous enterprise.

Economics

Recycled HDPE feedstock plus brutal freight costs make a bed built in community cheaper over its life than one trucked in to break.

The model

Sell beds to buyers who already have a duty to house people — then bring the plastic in-house.

The path off grants has two levers: in-source the plastic (8.6× markup verified on bought-in feedstock) and move institutional buyers from grant-funded to procurement-funded.

Go-to-market — the first three wedges

2 · Community co-invest

Communities buying their own beds — and their own plant — on their terms.

Proof: Dianne Stokes offered to self-fund; PICC offered to buy a plant.

Online Stretch Bed sales and match-eligible catalytic capital that de-risks the scale-up.

Why we get stronger as we grow

The advantage compounds where a competitor can’t follow: in community.
