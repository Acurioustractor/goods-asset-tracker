## 0. The TL;DR

You are selling **a manufactured good** into a system that is currently buying **disposable, unreliable goods** (3-12 month bed lifespan, $3M/yr Alice Springs washing machines that "end up in dumps within months"). Your unfair advantages are: (a) recycled-content + on-country production, (b) repairable design, (c) Indigenous co-design and (eventually) ownership, (d) a 5-year warranty no metro retailer will give a remote household.

The ceiling on demand is **not real demand** — it is **procurement plumbing**. Eight pipes carry the cash today; if you connect to even three of them well, you sell out the next 5,000 beds and 500 washing machines.

The eight pipes:

1. **NT Remote Housing Investment Package** — $4B over 10 years, 270 new homes/year. **Furniture fitout is currently unspecified** in public reporting → opportunity to *write yourselves into the standard fitout package.*
2. **Aboriginal Hostels Limited** — 40+ hostels, ~17,000 beds in service. Replacement cycle = bulk purchase opportunity.
3. **Outback Stores + ALPA retail network** — ~80 stores in remote NT/WA/QLD. Stretch Bed becomes a stocked item.
4. **NACCHO + 144 ACCHOs** — clinic and staff-housing fitouts. One peak-body endorsement opens the channel.
5. **NIAA Community Laundries — $11.4M / 12 laundries** (already won by Heart Foundation/AIG). Don't compete; **partner or differentiate to per-household Pakkimjalki Kari**.
6. **NSW Aboriginal Housing Office + state housing equivalents** (QLD, WA, SA, NT) — 222+ houses delivered in NT 2024-25 alone.
7. **Mining-region foundations (BHP, Rio Tinto, Glencore, Newmont)** — 9-figure giving, geographic overlap with deployment communities.
8. **NDIS SDA + community housing fitouts** — under-mapped but a scaling pool, particularly for adaptive (washable, no-foam) bedding.

**Pre-condition for 4 of these 8 pipes:** A 51%+ First Nations-owned operating entity (the IPP 2026 eligibility threshold tightens 1 July 2026). Without it, the door to federal IPP-eligible contracts closes — and federal procurement is rising from 3% to 4% Indigenous spend by 2029-30, ~$1B+ annually in absolute terms.

## 2. The eight channels — concrete plays

### Channel 1: NT Remote Housing Package fitout standard
- **Spend pool:** $4B over 10 years, 270 homes/year
- **Decision-makers:** Aboriginal Housing NT (AHNT), four NT Land Councils, NT Dept Housing, Local Government and Community Development (DHLGCD)
- **Why this is achievable:** The reporting on the program does NOT specify fitout standards. Furniture, beds, and whitegoods are the gap between "house built" and "house occupied".
- **Action:** Co-write a "Goods Standard Fitout Pack" with AHNT and one Land Council. Target the Murray River North / Thamarrurr Development Corp / Arnhem Land Progress contractor cluster (already winning $5-13M housing contracts) — they need a fitout subcontractor they can trust.
- **First-meeting target:** AHNT CEO + DHLGCD Director Remote Housing. May 2026.

### Channel 2: Aboriginal Hostels Limited bulk replacement
- **Spend pool:** Implicit ~2,400 beds/yr replacement cycle for 17,000 in-service beds
- **Decision-makers:** AHL CEO (federal statutory body, reports to NIAA Minister)
- **Why this is achievable:** AHL is a single Commonwealth entity. They publish an annual procurement plan. Snow Foundation contacts (and Nic's Orange Sky / OAM relationships) can introduce.
- **Action:** Get on AHL's standing offer panel for "essential furniture and household goods". Pilot one hostel (e.g., Tennant Creek AHL hostel) with 20 Stretch Beds and document outcomes.
- **First-meeting target:** AHL Procurement Director. June 2026.

### Channel 3: Outback Stores + ALPA retail
- **Spend pool:** Replace the Alice Springs $3M/yr washer supplier; capture direct-to-community bed sales
- **Decision-makers:** Outback Stores Pty Ltd CEO; ALPA General Manager
- **Why this is achievable:** Both organisations are explicitly mandated to improve community wellbeing AND prefer Indigenous-aligned suppliers. ALPA already won $14M in NT housing contracts → they're already in the construction supply chain.
- **Action:** Two parallel meetings: (a) ALPA — "stock Stretch Bed in 30 East Arnhem stores"; (b) Outback Stores — "exclusive Pakkimjalki Kari washing machine line". Both can be pitched as Indigenous-procurement compliance for their corporate customers (mining, government).
- **First-meeting target:** ALPA Darwin office, May 2026; Outback Stores Alice Springs, May 2026.

### Channel 4: NACCHO + ACCHOs
- **Spend pool:** 144 ACCHOs nationally, each spending on staff housing and clinic accommodation
- **Decision-makers:** NACCHO Health Procurement; AHCWA, VACCHO, QAIHC for state-level
- **Why this is achievable:** The clinical case (scabies → RHD, KPMG $1:$6 ROI) is rigorously evidence-based and already lands inside NACCHO's published priorities.
- **Action:** Write a NACCHO-endorsed "Health-First Bed and Laundry Procurement Specification". Pilot with 3 ACCHOs (one each in QLD, NT, WA). Use Centre Corp / Snow funding to subsidise the pilots.
- **First-meeting target:** NACCHO Public Health Director, June 2026.

### Channel 5: NIAA Community Laundries — partner or pivot
- **Reality:** The $11.4M / 12 laundries pool was won by AIG + Heart Foundation. Direct competition is futile.
- **Two alternative plays:**
  - **5a. Subcontract:** Approach AIG to be the *manufacturer/installer* of the per-laundry equipment for the next 12 builds. AIG has Kimberly-Clark for consumables but no announced equipment manufacturer.
  - **5b. Pivot to per-household:** Reframe Pakkimjalki Kari as "12 community laundries for 1 community vs 1 washing machine for every overcrowded household". This is the wedge — the AIG model serves 12 communities; per-household serves 270 NEW houses every year via Channel 1.
- **First-meeting target:** AIG CEO Steve Watson, June 2026. Explore subcontract OR explicit complementary positioning.

### Channel 6: State Aboriginal Housing offices
- **Spend pool:** NSW AHO ($47M federal-recorded), Homes NSW ($9M), Department of Communities & Justice NSW ($11.5M), plus equivalents in QLD/WA/SA
- **Decision-makers:** State AHO CEOs, Aboriginal Housing Office Boards
- **Why this is achievable:** They already buy in volume from non-Indigenous suppliers (Australian Bedding Co, Master Building Group). An IPP-style state procurement preference for First Nations suppliers exists in NSW.
- **Action:** NSW AHO is the highest-leverage entry — closest to Sydney decision-makers, biggest documented spend, public Indigenous-supplier mandate. Meet first.
- **First-meeting target:** NSW AHO CEO, July 2026.

### Channel 7: Mining-region foundations
- **Spend pool:**
  - BHP Foundation: $195M annual giving (overlaps Pilbara, QLD coal regions)
  - Rio Tinto Foundation: $153M annual (Pilbara, Weipa)
  - Glencore Coal Communities Fund (~$5-10M/yr)
  - Newmont Tanami Community Investment (~$2-5M/yr — Yuendumu region)
  - Inpex Indigenous Engagement (~$3M/yr — Darwin, Tiwi Islands)
- **Decision-makers:** Foundation CEOs / Community Relations leads
- **Why this is achievable:** Mining companies have public Indigenous procurement targets and need credible programs. A bed/washer in a remote community is photogenic, measurable, and aligns with company "social licence" reporting.
- **Action:** Build a single "Mining-Sector Pack" that says: "We deliver X beds and Y washing machines to communities within 100km of your mine sites for $Z, with 40% community profit share". Aim for 1 anchor mining-foundation deal in 2026.
- **First-meeting target:** BHP Foundation Director, August 2026.

### Channel 8: NDIS SDA + community housing fitouts
- **Spend pool:** NDIS SDA market is ~$700M/yr nationally. "Adaptive" SDA properties need washable, no-foam bedding for hygiene compliance. Community housing providers (Yumba-Meta, Compass, Hume) all bulk-buy beds.
- **Decision-makers:** SDA developers; community housing CEOs
- **Why this is achievable:** Stretch Bed's washable design is *uniquely* suited to disability-supported accommodation hygiene standards.
- **Action:** Get one NDIS-registered occupational therapist (OT) to specify Stretch Bed for one SDA property. Use that as the case study for sector outreach. Lower priority than 1-7 but high margin.
- **First-meeting target:** Summer Foundation (SDA peak), Q4 2026.

## 4. The structural pre-requisite: 51% First Nations-owned operating entity

**This is the make-or-break.** From 1 July 2026, IPP eligibility requires 51% First Nations ownership. Without it:
- NIAA cannot directly contract with you.
- Channels 1, 2, 4, 5, 6 are gated or de-prioritised.
- Mining-sector deals (Channel 7) require IPP-aligned suppliers for their own targets.

**Recommended structure (4-6 month build):**
- **Goods Co-op Ltd** (or equivalent) — new operating company, 51% Indigenous-owned via:
  - 26% Oonchiumpa Bloomfield Family Trust
  - 15% Aboriginal Investment NT
  - 10% individual Indigenous co-founders/elders' nominees (Diane Stokes, Fred Campbell, etc.)
  - 49% A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd
- **Profit share:** 40% returned to artisan communities (matches existing public commitment)
- **Governance:** Board majority (3 of 5) Indigenous; Goods/ACT keeps founders' veto on product spec only
- **Asset transfer:** A Curious Tractor licenses the Stretch Bed and Pakkimjalki Kari IP to Goods Co-op for nominal fee; production facility owned by Goods Co-op
- **Supply Nation registration:** Apply Q3 2026; expect 6-month registration timeline

**Critical sub-task:** Engage a First Nations governance lawyer (Arnold Bloch Leibler Indigenous practice, or Ashurst's First Nations team) to structure properly and avoid "black cladding" perception.

## 5. Production capacity scaling

To hit 6,000 beds + 975 washers in 5 years from 369 beds + 20 washers today, production must scale ~16x.

| Year | Beds/yr | Washers/yr | Production facilities | Communities producing |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 2026 (baseline) | 100 | 20 | 1 (Alice Springs prototype) | Tennant Creek, Alice Homelands |
| 2027 | 600 | 100 | 2 (add Palm Island OR Maningrida) | + Palm Island OR Maningrida |
| 2028 | 1,200 | 200 | 3 | + 1 of Kalgoorlie/Groote/Maningrida |
| 2029 | 1,800 | 250 | 4 | + 1 |
| 2030 | 2,500 | 400 | 5-6 | + 2 |

Each containerised facility = ~$300k capital + ~$120k/yr operating. 5 facilities = $1.5M capex + $600k opex = ~$2.1M/yr at peak.

**Revenue at peak (2030 wholesale-blended):** 2,500 × $700 + 400 × $1,300 = $1.75M + $520k = **$2.27M/yr**. Breaks even with operating costs at ~3,000 beds/yr; **profitability requires the ALPA/Outback retail premium and mining-foundation pre-purchase margins**.

## 8. Where this becomes a platform, not a product

The Goods Asset Register tracking system (389 assets, QR codes, real-time check-ins) is ALSO a saleable asset. Once the Goods Co-op is delivering thousands of beds, the *register* becomes:

- **A specification standard** for any remote-housing fitout (every bed has a QR, every washer has a maintenance log)
- **A measurement-and-evaluation product** for funders (Tim Fairfax, BHP, Snow can see real-time impact dashboards)
- **A platform other Indigenous social enterprises can use** (Orange Sky, AIG, ALPA could plug their assets in for $)
- **A government-grade asset management offer** (state housing departments could license the platform for their entire remote portfolio)

The platform sale is a 2027-2028 conversation — but design every channel-1-to-8 deal with the option to upsell tracking infrastructure later.
