# Pakkimjalki Kari Washing Machine

> Pakkimjalki Kari is the washing machine prototype, named in Warumungu language by Elder Dianne Stokes. It is not for direct sale yet. The current machines prove the repairability direction, but the price still has to come down before resident ownership is realistic.

## Why it matters

Washing machines are health hardware. They let people wash clothes, bedding and towels when they choose, rather than only when a service is available.

That choice matters for school, work, self-esteem, scabies prevention and household dignity.

## Current prototype

The current prototype is based on a commercial-grade Speed Queen.

Nicholas described the approach as taking a strong washing machine and adding the equivalent of bull bars and bash plates for remote conditions. It works as a prototype, but it is expensive. Current units cost roughly $4,500 to $5,000, while the target resident-accessible price is closer to $1,000 to $2,000.

About 15 machines have been built to date. Most have been purchased or placed through councils or organisations, not directly bought by residents.

## Telemetry

Some machines can connect to the internet for usage and maintenance data. That is useful for fleet learning, but it should not be overdescribed as a complete live fleet system yet.

The useful questions are:

- Is the machine being used?
- Has usage stopped unexpectedly?
- Is it using unusual power or water?
- Does it need maintenance?
- What design change would make it easier to own, use or repair?

## What is still being solved

- resident price point
- repair model in remote communities
- ownership model versus community laundry model
- telemetry consent and data use
- product supply chain
- whether rebates, councils or avoided landfill costs can reduce the real price
