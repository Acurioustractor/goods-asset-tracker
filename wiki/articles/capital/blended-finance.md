# Blended Finance

> A strategy that combines the right mix of capital from multiple stakeholders with different risk-return requirements but common impact objectives. Grants and guarantees "catalyse" private capital by taking the risky bottom of the stack.

## Definition

From GIIN, Big Society Capital, and the World Bank Independent Evaluation Group:

> A strategy that combines the right mix of capital from multiple stakeholders who have different risk-return requirements on their capital but common impact objectives.

Often framed as the strategic use of philanthropic funding to catalyse private capital into impact investments where the unaided economics would not meet commercial risk-return targets.

## How it works

Philanthropic funding acts as the "de-risking" layer. Private capital sits on top at market rates. The blended structure finances the social enterprise.

```
Private capital   ── market-rate ─┐
                                   ├─→ Blended finance structure ─→ Investee
Philanthropic    ── concessional ─┘                 │
funding            "de-risking"                      ▼
       ▲                                         Repayment
       └──────── "catalyses" private capital
```

## Capital stack (blended vs regular)

**Regular finance stack** (most senior on bottom):
1. Standard Debt
2. Subordinated Debt
3. Common Equity

**Blended finance stack** adds protection layers above:
1. Standard Debt (most senior, most protection)
2. Subordinated Debt
3. Common Equity
4. Junior Equity Position
5. Grants
6. Guarantees (least senior, absorbs first losses)

Grants and guarantees protect senior layers so commercial investors can participate at their required risk-return profile. If grant value is not absorbed by losses, it generally accrues as equity on the balance sheet.

## Four types of blended finance

| Type | Description | Fit to Goods |
|---|---|---|
| **Fund-level** | Concessional + commercial in one fund | Low fit, too early stage |
| **Project-level** | Partial funding or guarantees for large infrastructure that contributes to SDGs | **High fit** for containerised manufacturing plant |
| **Company-level** | Credit enhancement (guarantees, subsidised concessional loans) on below-market terms | High fit for SEFA + IBA blend |
| **Outcome-based** | Corporate bonds or loans tied to SDG impact targets | Future relevance (e.g. NT Housing outcome contract) |

## Three benefits

1. **Increases availability of capital.** Conventional investors can participate in impact deals that would not otherwise meet their risk/return thresholds.
2. **Meets the needs of social enterprises.** More flexible products (unsecured, affordable rates, longer terms).
3. **Improves investment economics.** Concessional capital can subsidise fund operating costs.

## For Goods

The Goods stack is effectively a **project-level + company-level** blend anchored on the containerised production plant. See [[capital-stack]] for the current proposed structure. Grants and guarantees sit on top (QBE, Snow, PFI recoverable), commercial debt at the base (IBA, SEFA). Mindaroo and PFI recoverable capital is the catalytic layer that makes the commercial debt feasible.
