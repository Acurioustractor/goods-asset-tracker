# 9. Legal Structure

> The legal structure is not finished, and the pack should say that plainly. Goods has moved from Nicholas Marchesi OAM's sole-trader history toward A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd as the current trading home. A Kind Tractor Ltd exists in the wider ACT world, but it is not the active Goods vehicle. The legal work now is to design a structure that can sell products, receive the right capital, protect purpose, carry risk, and make community-owned production real.

## The human version

The legal structure has to serve the work, not the other way around.

Goods needs enough structure for funders, lenders, customers and product liability. It also needs enough openness for Aboriginal-controlled or locally owned entities to run collection, production, repair, sales and maintenance in their own geographies. If the legal model centralises everything in ACT because that is easiest for contracts, it will work against the long-term purpose.

## What the structure must enable

- Direct product sales, including resident purchase pathways over time.
- Institutional sales to councils, health providers, funders, corporates and community organisations.
- Grants and philanthropic funding where appropriate.
- Patient or concessional debt only when repayment capacity is clear.
- Product liability, warranty, safety, insurance and quality management.
- On-Country production, repair and maintenance with local or Aboriginal-controlled entities.
- Clear ownership of IP, designs, machinery, brand, data, training materials and source documents.
- Shared services from ACT without trapping all value in the centre.
- Data and story governance consistent with consent and Indigenous data sovereignty.
- Mission protection strong enough that capital cannot pull Goods away from community ownership and repairable products.

## Community ownership question

This is the core legal issue, not a side note.

The medium-term vision is not one large Goods organisation manufacturing everything centrally. It is multiple On-Country production plants, run by Aboriginal-controlled or locally owned enterprises where possible, with shared support for design, machinery, funding, quality and learning.

The legal model therefore needs to answer:

- Who owns the plant and equipment?
- Who owns the plastic, product stock and finished beds?
- Who signs product warranties and safety obligations?
- Who owns customer and product data?
- Who can use the Goods brand and designs?
- How does ACT recover shared service and R&D costs?
- How do local entities build equity rather than only provide labour?
- What happens if a production partner changes direction or wants to leave the system?

## Charity trade-off

A charity structure could help with philanthropic funding, gifts and specific community benefit work.

The risk is strategic drift. If Goods becomes mainly a charity distributor, the product may be judged by whether funders like it rather than whether residents, local producers and communities choose it. The current purpose is sharper than that: desirable, repairable, washable products that people actually want and that can move production and value On-Country.

The structure may need both charitable and commercial functions, but the distinction must be intentional.

## Mission protection

There is no confirmed Goods-specific mechanism yet such as:

- B Corp certification
- purpose-lock constitution
- community benefit company model
- Aboriginal-controlled governance body
- special shareholder or veto right
- asset lock
- formal community-ownership deed

The current mission protection is cultural and relational: founders, advisory input, community relationships and the repeated intent to learn from Indigenous governance rather than forcing everything into Western templates.

That is strong enough to guide early work. It is not enough to carry larger capital without legal form.

## What legal advice should decide

- Whether Goods needs its own entity before taking repayable capital.
- Whether debt should sit with ACT, Goods, a subsidiary, a project vehicle or local production entity.
- How to separate product liability from community production participation.
- How IP, machinery, brand and training should be licensed or shared.
- Whether A Kind Tractor Ltd should be used, kept separate or ignored for Goods.
- What mission lock is credible and practical.
- What structure best supports Aboriginal-controlled or locally owned production.
- What data and story ownership clauses need to sit in contracts, consent forms and partner agreements.

## Human review questions

- What has already been registered, and what documents prove it?
- Which existing contracts, grants or invoices still name Nicholas personally or a previous structure?
- What should A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd own centrally for now?
- What should a local production partner be able to own or control from day one?
- What legal promise are we prepared to make about community ownership?
- What capital types are impossible or risky until structure is settled?
