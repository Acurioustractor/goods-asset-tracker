# Impact measurement method

> **Why this page exists.** Goods works at the join between household hardware and health. That makes it tempting to claim a health outcome. We will not. This page shows exactly where we draw the line: what is **independently checkable today**, what is **modelled from stated assumptions**, and what is a **future indicator we have not yet collected**. A reviewer should be able to take any number we publish and trace it to the asset register, to `products.ts`, or to a labelled assumption — and should be able to see, just as clearly, the things we do not yet measure. The honesty *is* the method.

## The theory of change in five steps

If Goods makes products people actually want, makes them survive remote conditions, makes them washable and repairable, and moves more of the production loop on-country, then families get better household goods, communities keep more value, and less product ends up buried in dumps. The evidence has to come **from use, feedback, repair, repeat demand and consented story** — not from our own claims.

1. **Listen** — to community about what is needed and what fails.
2. **Design** — adapt the product for actual remote conditions.
3. **Build washable / repairable** — materials that can be cleaned, mended and reused, not landfilled.
4. **Move production closer to community** — local making, repair and, over time, local ownership.
5. **Track via consented feedback** — product records, return visits, and stories the community controls.

**The three shifts this is meant to produce:**

- **Material** — durable, washable, repairable goods replace failing ones.
- **Economic** — more money and capability stays local through collection, production, repair and ownership.
- **Story** — community members control how their experience is recorded, shared and interpreted.

## The health rationale, stated responsibly

The reason this work matters runs along a known infection pathway:

> **unwashable bedding → scabies / skin infection → Strep A → rheumatic fever → rheumatic heart disease.**

This chain is the **WHY** — the rationale for treating a bed and a washing machine as **environmental-health hardware**, not just furniture. It is *not* a measured Goods outcome.

**The claim ceiling — the most we will say:** *washable beds and reliable washing machines support the environmental-health conditions needed to interrupt known infection pathways.* We do **not** claim a verified reduction in rheumatic heart disease, and we do **not** claim a completed longitudinal study. Both would require evidence we do not hold.

**The route to validated outcomes is a partnership, framed as future.** Clinical and primary-health partners — Miwatj Health and equivalent ACCHOs — are the only legitimate source of a validated health-outcome measure. A partner-validated outcomes method, co-designed with those partners, is a named future build (see below), not a present claim. Until it exists and is run with partner evidence, the health pathway stays in the **[FUTURE]** column and in the rationale, never in the results.

## Consent as method

Consent is not a compliance step bolted on at the end — it is part of how we measure, in the OCAP tradition (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession).

- **Only consent-cleared voices are used externally.** Today that is exactly three: **Ivy Johnson, Dianne Stokes, and Ray Nelson.** Everyone else is consent-pending and is not named, quoted, or pictured in any external material.
- **Consent travels with the data.** A story's permission status is attached to the story — when it moves to the wiki, a newsletter, an Empathy Ledger syndication, a deck, or the website, its consent status moves with it and is re-checked at the point of use.
- **The human anchor we can use:** Ray Nelson's bed (consent-cleared) — *"Since receiving their new beds, they are no longer experiencing back pains."* This is a lived-experience account, presented as such, not as a clinical result.
- **The short consent-cleared list is itself a current gap.** Three cleared voices is too few to evidence impact at the scale the work is reaching. Closing that list — confirming consent with more storytellers, properly — is a measurement-method task, not a marketing one.

## What's next to make this fundable

The measurement method has two honest gaps. Naming them as the next builds is what makes the page credible rather than thin.

1. **A partner-validated outcomes method.** Co-design a health-outcomes measure with Miwatj / clinical partners — the legitimate route to ever moving a health indicator out of the FUTURE column. Until it runs, the claim ceiling holds.
2. **A consent-cleared story list.** Expand beyond the current three (Ivy Johnson, Dianne Stokes, Ray Nelson) with confirmed, documented consent, so impact can be evidenced through community voice without breaching OCAP.
3. **Turn the bed into something easy to follow** (the near-term measurement build that feeds the FUTURE column): QR / ID-based bed check-ins, repair and replacement requests, simple "still in use" checks, and a record of whether a bed has moved, been damaged, been repaired or been thrown out. This is how survival-at-12/24/36-months and reuse metrics eventually become **[VERIFIED]** rather than **[FUTURE]**.

> The discipline underneath all three: a metric earns its way leftward — FUTURE → MODELLED → VERIFIED — only as real evidence arrives. We would rather show a funder a gap they can see than a number that hides one.
