# Goods on Country
## Vision and ambition

*Written by the founders. The diagnostic told us our vision is strong when we talk about it and thinner when we write it down. This is us writing it down properly. It is also where we set out, plainly, how Goods on Country relates to A Curious Tractor above it and to the community-owned production we are building toward — because that map has lived in our heads and conversations more than on the page, and a reader deserves to see it.*

---

## The vision

Goods on Country started from one uncomfortable observation. In remote Australia, the household goods that people depend on for their health and their dignity are often the worst-designed things in the house. The bed is hard to freight, impossible to wash, quick to break, and has no recycling pathway when it fails. The washing machine is sold at a metro price, asked to run far more cycles than a city machine ever would, and built for conditions it was never built for. When these things break, and they break fast, they go to the community dump, because there is nowhere else for them to go.

That is not only a waste problem. It is a health problem, and the chain is short and well understood. Bedding that cannot be washed feeds scabies and skin infections. Skin infections feed Strep A. Strep A feeds rheumatic fever, and rheumatic fever becomes rheumatic heart disease — a condition almost unheard of in wealthy Australia and endemic in remote communities. We say it the way it is true: a good bed can prevent heart disease. We are not making charity furniture or convenience appliances. We are making environmental-health hardware.

The solution is to design that hardware *with* communities, build it from recycled plastic for the conditions it will actually live in, track every unit so it can be repaired and supported rather than dumped, and make it on country. The flagship is the Stretch Bed: a flat-pack, washable bed of recycled HDPE plastic legs, two galvanised steel poles, and heavy-duty Australian canvas. It weighs 26 kg, carries 200 kg, assembles in about five minutes with no tools, and is built for a ten-year-plus life. Each bed diverts 20 kg of plastic from landfill. It is not a clip-together kit — the poles thread through the canvas sleeves and the crossed-plank legs, and tensioning the whole assembly makes the canvas itself structural. It stands because of how it is built. We have deployed 496 beds across 9 communities, every one of them tracked by QR code in a register a stranger can scan, and the demand that matters most arrived after people used the product, not before.

The north star sits behind all of this, and we want it stated first rather than buried at the end. **Our job is to become unnecessary.** Success is not Goods on Country growing into a large central supplier of beds. Success is the communities we work with owning the means to collect, make, repair, and sell these goods themselves — so that the central organisation can step back. We are building toward our own redundancy on purpose. Everything below is in service of that.

## The three shifts

The work changes three things at once, and we hold all three or none.

**Material.** Unsuitable goods are replaced by goods that are durable, washable, and repairable. This is the visible shift — the bed you can hose down, the machine built to be fixed rather than thrown away. It is the easiest to see and the easiest to underrate.

**Economic.** More value stays local. Today most of the money that flows into remote essential goods flows straight back out — to metro suppliers, to freight, to replacement after replacement. The production model is designed to keep collection, manufacture, repair, and eventually ownership on country, so the dollars and the capability stay where the need is.

**Story.** Communities control how their own experience is recorded, shared, and interpreted. We track every product and we hold every story under consent, and the authority over both moves toward community rather than away from it. This shift is the one that keeps the other two honest.

## The structure, mapped

The diagnostic flagged that the relationship between Goods on Country, our parent A Curious Tractor, and the future community-owned production entities was not explicitly mapped or communicated. It was clear in our heads and our conversations, less clear on the page. Here it is on the page. The honest framing throughout is that the structure is mid-migration this financial year, so we separate what is settled, what is moving, and what is being designed — the same discipline the entity-wording block holds.

```
       ------------------------------------------------------------

   Status: NOT YET ESTABLISHED. Legal form being designed with
           MinterEllison. This is the pathway, not the current state.
```

Reading the map from the top:

**A Curious Tractor is the parent, and it carries the things a single community venture cannot carry alone.** It holds the intellectual property and the product designs, the Goods on Country brand, the shared services that no small operating entity should have to build from scratch — finance, operations, story and consent infrastructure, research and development — and the relationships that give access to capital, including the catalytic raise now underway. A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd (ACN 697 347 676) is the trading company, and it is the confirmed go-forward operating company for Goods.

**Goods on Country is the venture that sits beneath it and trades as it.** This is the work itself: the co-design with communities, the production system, the asset register, the deployments. Today Goods trades through Nicholas Marchesi as a sole trader, and all of that trading activity is migrating into A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd this financial year. Tax-deductible giving is handled separately, through The Butterfly Movement Ltd, which becomes the charitable home for this work from FY2026-27. That charitable pathway is not live today — donors cannot tax-receipt this work yet, and we are careful never to imply otherwise.

**The community-controlled production entities are the spokes, and they do not exist yet.** This is the part we most want a reader to understand correctly. The long-term shape is hub-and-spoke: A Curious Tractor as the hub holding design, quality systems, R&D, and shared back office, and Aboriginal-controlled or locally owned entities as the spokes that collect plastic, press sheets, cut and assemble beds, repair what is in the field, and hold the revenue and the employment on country. The legal form for those entities is still being designed with our lawyers at MinterEllison. We will not describe any transfer of ownership as complete, because it is not. Community ownership is the pathway we are building, not a state we have reached. Saying so plainly is part of doing no harm — we will not centralise value in A Curious Tractor while talking about community ownership, and we will not let the language run ahead of the legal reality.

So: the hub is current, the venture is mid-migration, and the spokes are future. That is the whole structure, and that is the order it matures in.

## Ambition over time

We hold the ambition in three time horizons, and we are deliberate about what belongs in each. The further out the horizon, the more we describe direction rather than commitment.

**Short — the next 12 months: prove the economics and put the first community operator on the payroll.** The immediate task is to turn our central economic claim from modelled to measured. Today the Stretch Bed sells for AU$750 against a marginal cost near AU$684.79, and breakeven on those numbers is out of reach. Our containerised production model projects that in-sourcing the plastic drops the unit cost substantially, but we have made 40 beds in-house at the farm already, so the process is proven; the per-bed cost at volume is the number that still needs a measured run. The first thing this period must deliver is a roughly 50-bed in-source production run that confirms or re-bases that cost. Alongside it, the first concrete step toward the spokes: a community-employed operator in a production role, so that the shift from "built by us" to "built on country" begins as a real job, not a slide.

**Medium — by end of FY2026-27: scope the first Aboriginal-controlled operating entity.** Within the next financial year we intend to have the first community-controlled production entity scoped — its legal form chosen with MinterEllison, its ownership structure capable of being 51%-plus First Nations-owned, and the related-party boundaries with A Curious Tractor and The Butterfly Movement Ltd clean. Scoped is the honest word. We are not promising a fully operating Aboriginal-owned plant by then; we are committing to having designed the vehicle that makes one real, and to having the governance and the legal form settled enough to stand it up.

**Long — distributed, community-owned production on country.** The endpoint is a distributed network of community-owned production, recycling, and maintenance on country: local entities collecting waste, making the goods, repairing them through the asset register, and holding the revenue and the work, with A Curious Tractor providing design, quality, R&D, funding support, and shared back office only where that genuinely helps. The exact number of spokes and the precise legal model are not settled, and we will not pretend a timeline we cannot yet evidence. The direction is settled. The plant we are building was designed from the start to be able to move to community ownership, and that design choice is the long horizon expressed in steel and plastic rather than in words.

## A closing line

We would rather grow the role of our community partners than grow the size of the central organisation. If in five years A Curious Tractor is smaller in this work and the communities are larger in it — owning the plants, holding the revenue, employing their own people, and needing us less each year — then the vision will have done its job. The measure of success here is not how big we become. It is how unnecessary we manage to make ourselves.
