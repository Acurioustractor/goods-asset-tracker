# Outreach note: SEFA (Social Enterprise Finance Australia)

> DRAFT for founder review. Placeholders are in `[square brackets]`. This is a lender conversation, not a grant appeal — honest about debt, honest about what is and isn't in place. The actual send is Ben's to make.

---

Dear [confirm contact — pipeline says Hannah Ebeling],

We met SEFA through the QBE Catalysing Impact cohort, where you set out a line that has stayed with us: all debt does is bring cash flow from the future into the now. We are writing because Goods on Country is reaching the point where that is the right tool, and we would rather open the conversation early and honestly than arrive with a polished ask and gaps underneath it.

Goods designs and delivers essential household goods for remote Indigenous communities. The flagship is the Stretch Bed, a washable, flat-packable bed built from recycled plastic, galvanised steel, and Australian canvas. As of 29 May 2026 we have 496 beds deployed across 9 communities, each one tagged and trackable in a register you can scan. Across funders we have received AU$649,710.79, at an 88.8% collection rate. That figure is from our Xero workpaper and is unaudited; we will say so every time.

Here is the honest commercial position. Goods is about 89% grant-funded today, by design. We have proven communities want the bed and that funders will pay for deployments. We have not yet proven we can make it cheaply enough to stand on its own. The lever is making the recycled-plastic legs ourselves rather than buying them in at a verified 8.6 times markup. We have modelled the in-source cost at AU$425.74 against AU$684.79 today, but we have built zero beds in-house so far. A 50-bed production run, funded from the philanthropic layer, is designed to turn that claim from modelled to measured. We mention this because it is exactly the kind of clear-eyed risk a lender should weigh, not something to talk around.

What we would bring to SEFA is the senior, concessional base of a blended raise: a working-capital line of around AU$300,000, sitting beneath philanthropic and catalytic layers that absorb first risk. The use is specific. Our sharpest operational risk is that costs land before payments arrive — material and freight go out the door weeks or months before a grant, customer, or partner pays. The line would bridge that timing gap and fund inventory against confirmed orders, not speculative stock. WINYA Furniture, an Indigenous-owned manufacturer serving the same remote market on 36 federal contracts, is proof the path from social enterprise to a serviceable procurement-backed business exists. We see a comparable pipeline ahead of us, and we present it as direction, not as booked revenue.

We also know your gating conditions, and we are not pretending they are met. A SEFA facility needs a completed Goods-only financial model, an independent-majority fiduciary board rather than the advisory group we have today, and a revenue covenant we can actually service. None of those three is in place yet. What we are asking for now is not money. It is a first conversation, and your read on whether the path and timeline we are working to would make Goods financeable on terms that fit how SEFA lends.

Could we find half an hour in the next few weeks? We will bring the one-pager and the model as it stands, and answer anything directly.

With thanks,

Ben Knight & Nicholas Marchesi
Goods on Country
