# Goods on Country
## Business model on a page

*The diagnostic was right to flag this. We can describe our impact and our product clearly; we have been less disciplined about saying, on one page, exactly how the money works, who pays, what each customer is worth, and how we get off grants. This document does that. It does not dress up the commercial position — about 89% of our income is grant-funded today, by design, and we treat that as the starting point we are working away from, not a thing to hide. Read it alongside the strategy memo (§3 holds the same honest numbers) and the alignment map (the source of truth for every figure).*

## How we create, deliver, and capture value

**We create value** by designing essential household goods *with* remote communities rather than for them, so the product survives the conditions it actually lives in. The Stretch Bed is the flagship: recycled HDPE plastic X-trestle legs, two galvanised steel poles, and heavy-duty Australian canvas, built for a ten-year-plus life with a five-year warranty, washable, repairable, and flat-packable for freight. Co-design is not a courtesy — it is why the demand that matters most arrives *after* people use the product, not before.

**We deliver value** through on-country manufacture and QR-tracked deployment. The production model collects waste plastic, shreds it, presses and cuts it into bed components, and assembles on country. Every unit deployed carries a QR code in a live asset register that a stranger can scan, which turns each bed from a one-off handout into a trackable asset that can be repaired and supported in the field rather than dumped when it fails.

**We capture value** today almost entirely through product sales — but the buyer is usually an institution or funder paying on a community's behalf, not the resident. That is the current reality and the thing this page is honest about. The intended second value-capture line, not yet live, is the **plastic-collection / waste-offset revenue line**: turning the plastic input from a bought cost into a paid collection stream, where councils and waste partners pay Goods (or a community operator) to divert plastic that would otherwise go to the community dump. That flips a cost into revenue and is one of the few levers that improves unit economics without raising the price to the buyer. It is a design intention with a clear mechanism, not booked income.

The deeper model is **transfer, not just sales.** The plant is designed from the start to move to community ownership — local people collecting, making, repairing, and eventually selling, with A Curious Tractor providing design, quality systems, R&D, and shared back office only where that genuinely helps. The business model's job is to become unnecessary. That north star sits behind every segment below.

## Customer segments

Four segments carry the model. They are listed in order of how much revenue they actually produce today — which is the honest order, not the aspirational one. The first segment is essentially all of current income; the fourth is AU$90. We keep them separate on purpose, because each one proves something different and blending them hides exactly the gap a catalytic investor is being asked to close.

> Across all four, the **Stretch Bed is the only product for direct sale.** The Pakkimjalki Kari washing machine is a register-interest prototype — deployed and gathering feedback, not yet purchasable — so it appears below as pipeline and payment-innovation, never as booked product revenue.

### Segment 1 — Institutional / community-controlled buyers and councils funding deployments
*The current bulk of revenue.*

- **Who:** Aboriginal community-controlled organisations, homelands and council bodies, health services, and the philanthropic funders who pay for deployments through them. Centrecorp (via the Utopia pathway), PICC on Palm Island, Tennant Creek partners, Oonchiumpa.
- **What they buy:** Stretch Bed batches for community deployment; in future, plant partnerships.
- **How they pay:** Grant funds, foundation money, or invoice payment against an approved batch. This is where the AU$649,710.79 received [WORKPAPER — Xero mirror, 29 May 2026, unaudited] has come from, against AU$732,210.79 billed at an 88.8% collection rate.
- **Unit economics note:** Beds in this segment transact around the AU$750 list price [VERIFIED] or at a grant-subsidised effective rate. At today's marginal cost of AU$684.79 [WORKPAPER] the contribution per bed is thin (~AU$65); the segment's value today is *proof and volume*, not margin. The cost-down (below) is what turns this segment from break-even-ish delivery into a contributing line.
- **Sales effort:** Relationship-led and high-touch. Long co-design and trust-building cycles, founder-carried today. Each deal is bespoke; there is no repeatable funnel yet. This is the #1 operational risk (founder bottleneck) and the reason a Business Development hire is in the use-of-funds.
- **Capacity-to-pay (honest read):** Real but indirect and lumpy. These buyers do not pay from a household budget — they pay from grant rounds and program funds, so capacity rises and falls with funding cycles outside our control. The capacity is genuine (107 beds approved and INV-0291 paid through Centrecorp proves it) but it is *grant-derived capacity*, which is exactly why this segment cannot, on its own, get us off grants. It moves us off grants only when these same buyers shift from grant-funded to procurement-funded purchasing (Segment 3).

### Segment 2 — Businesses purchasing for staff housing
*Small today, clearer commercial logic.*

- **Who:** Employers running remote staff accommodation — mining-region operations, pastoral and agricultural employers, remote workforce and ranger programs, and corporates with place-based or RAP commitments.
- **What they buy:** Stretch Beds for staff and worker housing, where the washable, freight-friendly, durable design beats the true remote cost of beds that fail in months.
- **How they pay:** Direct commercial purchase or sponsored batch, typically at or above list price. Mining-region foundations may pay a premium (modelled ~1.2–1.5× for branded social-licence reporting) [MODELLED].
- **Unit economics note:** This segment can sustain full price or better, so contribution per bed is stronger than Segment 1 and improves materially after the cost-down. Volume today is negligible.
- **Sales effort:** Medium. Warmer than government procurement, slower than retail. Requires a credible B2B offer (quote, warranty, delivery schedule, references) that does not yet exist in packaged form.
- **Capacity-to-pay (honest read):** Genuine and budget-backed — these are buyers with operating budgets and a real problem (beds that don't survive remote staff housing). The risk is not their capacity but our framing: sold carelessly, staff-housing sales can drift into charity optics or distract from the remote-community mission. Governed well, this is one of the few near-term segments that pays a commercial margin.

### Segment 3 — Procurement / housing programs
*Future. Gated on 51% First Nations ownership.*

- **Who:** Government and program procurement — NT Remote Housing fitouts, Aboriginal Hostels Limited (~17,000 beds in service), state Aboriginal Housing offices, NACCHO/ACCHO staff and clinic housing, Outback Stores / ALPA. (These are the procurement "pipes" detailed in the go-to-market draft — see the caveat below.)
- **What they buy:** Stretch Beds at volume as a standard fitout or replacement item; later, washing-machine pathways.
- **How they pay:** Wholesale procurement contracts and standing-offer panels, at negotiated volume pricing. This is the channel that, at scale, replaces grant income with contracted revenue.
- **Unit economics note:** Wholesale pricing sits below list (the go-to-market draft models ~AU$650/bed to government buyers) [MODELLED], so this segment only contributes margin *after* the in-source cost-down brings marginal cost below that wholesale price. Volume is the trade-off for price — and the binding constraint here is procurement plumbing, not demand.
- **Sales effort:** Very high and slow, with a hard structural pre-condition. Long procurement cycles, panel applications, and specification-writing. Critically, **four of the eight procurement pipes require a 51%+ First Nations-owned operating entity** (the IPP threshold tightens 1 July 2026). Until the legal structure is settled — the keystone gap across this whole pack — this segment cannot be sold into at all.
- **Capacity-to-pay (honest read):** The strongest capacity of any segment — these are multi-billion-dollar pools (NT Remote Housing $4B/10yr; federal Indigenous procurement rising toward ~$1B+/yr) [MODELLED, from public sources]. But capacity is meaningless to us while the door is shut. The honest read is: *enormous capacity, currently inaccessible*, gated on (a) the 51% structure and (b) at least one signed procurement/offtake commitment, of which we hold zero today.

### Segment 4 — Direct retail + NDIS / aged-care
*Smallest today — AU$90 of shop revenue.*

- **Who:** Individual customers, allies, campers, and metro buyers via the website; and NDIS SDA, aged-care, and occupational-therapy pathways where a washable, no-foam, low-height bed meets a funded support need.
- **What they buy:** A single Stretch Bed at list price (AU$750) through the shop; or a clinically-specified bed under a participant's funding.
- **How they pay:** Card checkout via Stripe (the only direct-sale pipe live today); or NDIS/aged-care funding against an OT specification.
- **Unit economics note:** Full retail price, so the best contribution per unit of any segment — but the volume is three orders totalling **AU$90** [WORKPAPER]. NDIS/SDA can command a premium (modelled AU$1,200–1,500 for adaptive/clinical-grade) [MODELLED] but requires clinical documentation we do not yet produce.
- **Sales effort:** Low per-transaction for retail (the storefront exists and runs itself) but currently near-zero marketing behind it. NDIS requires high admin effort per unit (clinical spec, OT relationships).
- **Capacity-to-pay (honest read):** This is the most direct test of willingness-to-pay and the smallest. The AU$90 is not embarrassing — it is the point: it proves the *pipe* works (a stranger can buy a bed online and it ships) but not yet the *market*. Direct remote-resident capacity-to-pay at full price is the open question the whole business is built around; we are explicit that most remote households cannot today buy direct at AU$750 without the kind of predatory credit we exist to avoid. NDIS/aged-care is the one direct pathway with funded capacity behind it, and it is under-developed.

## Payment innovations

Two mechanisms are designed to break the affordability constraint without simply lowering price. Both are intentions with clear logic, not live revenue.

- **Lease-to-own washing machines.** The Pakkimjalki Kari prototype is still too expensive for resident ownership at its current build cost (several thousand dollars). A lease or service model spreads that cost over time and bundles the repair pathway, so a household gets a reliable, supported machine without a single large upfront payment or a predatory payment plan. This keeps the durability while making the price reachable — and it suits the prototype's register-interest status, letting us deploy and learn before committing to a sale price.

- **Council waste-management cost offsets paying for community plastic collection.** Councils already carry the cost of plastic and broken goods accumulating in community dumps. If a council pays a community operator to collect and divert that plastic, the same plastic that is currently a *bought input* to the bed becomes a *paid collection stream*. This is the lever that turns the plastic input from a cost line into a revenue line — improving unit economics from the input side rather than the price side, and putting paid local work into the production model. It depends on place-by-place arrangements with councils and waste partners and is not yet contracted anywhere.

## The transition off grants

This is the heart of what the diagnostic asked for, and we will be plain about it.

**Where we are.** About **89% of income is grant-funded today, by design** [WORKPAPER]. Direct commercial revenue is AU$90. This is a de-risking phase, not a growth phase — we have proven communities want the product and that funders will pay for deployments; we have *not* yet proven the product can be made at a price that lets the business stand on its own. We are not going to pretend the 89% is anything other than what it is.

**Why it cannot stay there.** At today's numbers the Stretch Bed sells for AU$750 [VERIFIED] against a marginal cost of AU$684.79 [WORKPAPER] and a fixed-cost block near AU$109,500/yr [MODELLED]. On those numbers, breakeven sits around **1,679 beds a year** [MODELLED] while we currently deploy about 130. That is out of reach, and grant income is what bridges the gap. The model only becomes self-supporting if the unit cost falls.

**The path has two moves, and they compound:**

1. **In-source the plastic to bring the cost down.** We have verified an 8.6× markup on the HDPE legs we currently buy from an external supplier [VERIFIED]. The containerised production model projects unit cost falling from **AU$684.79 → AU$425.74** (factory configuration) and **→ AU$270.74** (community configuration) [all MODELLED]. At the factory target, breakeven drops from ~1,679 to **333–338 beds a year** [MODELLED] — within sight of demand. The critical caveat, stated everywhere in this pack: **zero beds have been assembled in-house so far.** This is a hypothesis, not a result.

2. **Move institutional buyers from grant-funded to procurement-funded.** Segment 1's capacity is real but grant-derived. The transition is the same buyers (and the Segment 3 procurement pools behind them) purchasing through contracted procurement rather than grant rounds — which requires the 51% structure and the first signed procurement/offtake commitment.

**The proof point that gates everything: the 50-bed in-source run.** The first and most important use of funds is small and specific — **AU$60–80K to run a 50-bed in-source production run** [TARGET use of funds]. That experiment turns the central economic claim from modelled to measured.
- **Pass** = AU$425.74 confirmed → scale the raise on proven economics, breakeven becomes credibly reachable, and the procurement segment becomes worth the long sales cycle.
- **Miss** = re-base honestly at AU$684.79 and adjust the plan.

A catalytic investor is being asked to fund this experiment, not a finished economic engine. We say so plainly. The transition off grants is not a forecast we are asserting — it is a hypothesis with a AU$60–80K test in front of it.
