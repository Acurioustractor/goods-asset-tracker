## 1. The strategic spine (the one-sentence chain)

**A good bed can prevent heart disease.** In remote Australia the household goods people depend on for health and dignity — beds, washing machines — are often the worst-designed things in the house: hard to freight, impossible to wash, quick to break, and unrecyclable when they fail. Unwashable bedding feeds scabies and skin infections → Strep A → rheumatic fever → rheumatic heart disease. Goods designs essential household goods *with* communities, builds them for actual remote conditions from recycled plastic, tracks every unit, and moves production toward community ownership. **The endgame is to become unnecessary** — local people making, owning, and repairing the goods themselves.

The three shifts: **material** (durable, washable, repairable goods replace failing ones), **economic** (more value stays local), **story** (communities control how their experience is recorded). [Source: theory-of-change.md, enterprise/01–02]

# Goods on Country
## Vision and ambition

*Written by the founders. The diagnostic told us our vision is strong when we talk about it and thinner when we write it down. This is us writing it down properly. It is also where we set out, plainly, how Goods on Country relates to A Curious Tractor above it and to the community-owned production we are building toward — because that map has lived in our heads and conversations more than on the page, and a reader deserves to see it.*

## The vision

Goods on Country started from one uncomfortable observation. In remote Australia, the household goods that people depend on for their health and their dignity are often the worst-designed things in the house. The bed is hard to freight, impossible to wash, quick to break, and has no recycling pathway when it fails. The washing machine is sold at a metro price, asked to run far more cycles than a city machine ever would, and built for conditions it was never built for. When these things break, and they break fast, they go to the community dump, because there is nowhere else for them to go.

That is not only a waste problem. It is a health problem, and the chain is short and well understood. Bedding that cannot be washed feeds scabies and skin infections. Skin infections feed Strep A. Strep A feeds rheumatic fever, and rheumatic fever becomes rheumatic heart disease — a condition almost unheard of in wealthy Australia and endemic in remote communities. We say it the way it is true: a good bed can prevent heart disease. We are not making charity furniture or convenience appliances. We are making environmental-health hardware.

The solution is to design that hardware *with* communities, build it from recycled plastic for the conditions it will actually live in, track every unit so it can be repaired and supported rather than dumped, and make it on country. The flagship is the Stretch Bed: a flat-pack, washable bed of recycled HDPE plastic legs, two galvanised steel poles, and heavy-duty Australian canvas. It weighs 26 kg, carries 200 kg, assembles in about five minutes with no tools, and is built for a ten-year-plus life. Each bed diverts 20 kg of plastic from landfill. It is not a clip-together kit — the poles thread through the canvas sleeves and the crossed-plank legs, and tensioning the whole assembly makes the canvas itself structural. It stands because of how it is built. We have deployed 496 beds across 9 communities, every one of them tracked by QR code in a register a stranger can scan, and the demand that matters most arrived after people used the product, not before.

The north star sits behind all of this, and we want it stated first rather than buried at the end. **Our job is to become unnecessary.** Success is not Goods on Country growing into a large central supplier of beds. Success is the communities we work with owning the means to collect, make, repair, and sell these goods themselves — so that the central organisation can step back. We are building toward our own redundancy on purpose. Everything below is in service of that.

## The three shifts

The work changes three things at once, and we hold all three or none.

**Material.** Unsuitable goods are replaced by goods that are durable, washable, and repairable. This is the visible shift — the bed you can hose down, the machine built to be fixed rather than thrown away. It is the easiest to see and the easiest to underrate.

**Economic.** More value stays local. Today most of the money that flows into remote essential goods flows straight back out — to metro suppliers, to freight, to replacement after replacement. The production model is designed to keep collection, manufacture, repair, and eventually ownership on country, so the dollars and the capability stay where the need is.

**Story.** Communities control how their own experience is recorded, shared, and interpreted. We track every product and we hold every story under consent, and the authority over both moves toward community rather than away from it. This shift is the one that keeps the other two honest.

## The theory of change in five steps

If Goods makes products people actually want, makes them survive remote conditions, makes them washable and repairable, and moves more of the production loop on-country, then families get better household goods, communities keep more value, and less product ends up buried in dumps. The evidence has to come **from use, feedback, repair, repeat demand and consented story** — not from our own claims.

1. **Listen** — to community about what is needed and what fails.
2. **Design** — adapt the product for actual remote conditions.
3. **Build washable / repairable** — materials that can be cleaned, mended and reused, not landfilled.
4. **Move production closer to community** — local making, repair and, over time, local ownership.
5. **Track via consented feedback** — product records, return visits, and stories the community controls.

**The three shifts this is meant to produce:**

- **Material** — durable, washable, repairable goods replace failing ones.
- **Economic** — more money and capability stays local through collection, production, repair and ownership.
- **Story** — community members control how their experience is recorded, shared and interpreted.

***

## The health rationale, stated responsibly

The reason this work matters runs along a known infection pathway:

> **unwashable bedding → scabies / skin infection → Strep A → rheumatic fever → rheumatic heart disease.**

This chain is the **WHY** — the rationale for treating a bed and a washing machine as **environmental-health hardware**, not just furniture. It is *not* a measured Goods outcome.

**The claim ceiling — the most we will say:** *washable beds and reliable washing machines support the environmental-health conditions needed to interrupt known infection pathways.* We do **not** claim a verified reduction in rheumatic heart disease, and we do **not** claim a completed longitudinal study. Both would require evidence we do not hold.

**The route to validated outcomes is a partnership, framed as future.** Clinical and primary-health partners — Miwatj Health and equivalent ACCHOs — are the only legitimate source of a validated health-outcome measure. A partner-validated outcomes method, co-designed with those partners, is a named future build (see below), not a present claim. Until it exists and is run with partner evidence, the health pathway stays in the **[FUTURE]** column and in the rationale, never in the results.

***

## Consent as method

Consent is not a compliance step bolted on at the end — it is part of how we measure, in the OCAP tradition (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession).

- **Only consent-cleared voices are used externally.** Today that is exactly three: **Ivy Johnson, Dianne Stokes, and Ray Nelson.** Everyone else is consent-pending and is not named, quoted, or pictured in any external material.
- **Consent travels with the data.** A story's permission status is attached to the story — when it moves to the wiki, a newsletter, an Empathy Ledger syndication, a deck, or the website, its consent status moves with it and is re-checked at the point of use.
- **The human anchor we can use:** Ray Nelson's bed (consent-cleared) — *"Since receiving their new beds, they are no longer experiencing back pains."* This is a lived-experience account, presented as such, not as a clinical result.
- **The short consent-cleared list is itself a current gap.** Three cleared voices is too few to evidence impact at the scale the work is reaching. Closing that list — confirming consent with more storytellers, properly — is a measurement-method task, not a marketing one.

***

## What's next to make this fundable

The measurement method has two honest gaps. Naming them as the next builds is what makes the page credible rather than thin.

1. **A partner-validated outcomes method.** Co-design a health-outcomes measure with Miwatj / clinical partners — the legitimate route to ever moving a health indicator out of the FUTURE column. Until it runs, the claim ceiling holds.
2. **A consent-cleared story list.** Expand beyond the current three (Ivy Johnson, Dianne Stokes, Ray Nelson) with confirmed, documented consent, so impact can be evidenced through community voice without breaching OCAP.
3. **Turn the bed into something easy to follow** (the near-term measurement build that feeds the FUTURE column): QR / ID-based bed check-ins, repair and replacement requests, simple "still in use" checks, and a record of whether a bed has moved, been damaged, been repaired or been thrown out. This is how survival-at-12/24/36-months and reuse metrics eventually become **[VERIFIED]** rather than **[FUTURE]**.

## How we create, deliver, and capture value

**We create value** by designing essential household goods *with* remote communities rather than for them, so the product survives the conditions it actually lives in. The Stretch Bed is the flagship: recycled HDPE plastic X-trestle legs, two galvanised steel poles, and heavy-duty Australian canvas, built for a ten-year-plus life with a five-year warranty, washable, repairable, and flat-packable for freight. Co-design is not a courtesy — it is why the demand that matters most arrives *after* people use the product, not before.

**We deliver value** through on-country manufacture and QR-tracked deployment. The production model collects waste plastic, shreds it, presses and cuts it into bed components, and assembles on country. Every unit deployed carries a QR code in a live asset register that a stranger can scan, which turns each bed from a one-off handout into a trackable asset that can be repaired and supported in the field rather than dumped when it fails.

**We capture value** today almost entirely through product sales — but the buyer is usually an institution or funder paying on a community's behalf, not the resident. That is the current reality and the thing this page is honest about. The intended second value-capture line, not yet live, is the **plastic-collection / waste-offset revenue line**: turning the plastic input from a bought cost into a paid collection stream, where councils and waste partners pay Goods (or a community operator) to divert plastic that would otherwise go to the community dump. That flips a cost into revenue and is one of the few levers that improves unit economics without raising the price to the buyer. It is a design intention with a clear mechanism, not booked income.

The deeper model is **transfer, not just sales.** The plant is designed from the start to move to community ownership — local people collecting, making, repairing, and eventually selling, with A Curious Tractor providing design, quality systems, R&D, and shared back office only where that genuinely helps. The business model's job is to become unnecessary. That north star sits behind every segment below.

## Payment innovations

Two mechanisms are designed to break the affordability constraint without simply lowering price. Both are intentions with clear logic, not live revenue.

- **Lease-to-own washing machines.** The Pakkimjalki Kari prototype is still too expensive for resident ownership at its current build cost (several thousand dollars). A lease or service model spreads that cost over time and bundles the repair pathway, so a household gets a reliable, supported machine without a single large upfront payment or a predatory payment plan. This keeps the durability while making the price reachable — and it suits the prototype's register-interest status, letting us deploy and learn before committing to a sale price.

- **Council waste-management cost offsets paying for community plastic collection.** Councils already carry the cost of plastic and broken goods accumulating in community dumps. If a council pays a community operator to collect and divert that plastic, the same plastic that is currently a *bought input* to the bed becomes a *paid collection stream*. This is the lever that turns the plastic input from a cost line into a revenue line — improving unit economics from the input side rather than the price side, and putting paid local work into the production model. It depends on place-by-place arrangements with councils and waste partners and is not yet contracted anywhere.

This is the heart of what the diagnostic asked for, and we will be plain about it.

**Where we are.** About **89% of income is grant-funded today, by design** [WORKPAPER]. Direct commercial revenue is AU$90. **Why it cannot stay there.** At today's numbers the Stretch Bed sells for AU$750 [VERIFIED] against a marginal cost of AU$684.79 [WORKPAPER] and a fixed-cost block near AU$109,500/yr [MODELLED]. - **Miss** = re-base honestly at AU$684.79 and adjust the plan.

A catalytic investor is being asked to fund this experiment, not a finished economic engine. We say so plainly. The transition off grants is not a forecast we are asserting — it is a hypothesis with a AU$60–80K test in front of it.

## The 3-year strategy

Phase 3 is a direction we build toward, not a date we have set; the entity form and the ownership mechanism (the profit-share principle is settled, the mechanism is not) are resolved in Phase 1 before any of this is built.

## How to read this — the scoring key

Each risk is scored on two 1–5 scales. The score is **Likelihood × Consequence** (range 1–25), bucketed into four bands.

**Likelihood (L) — how probable in the next 12 months**

| L | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Rare — would be a surprise |
| 2 | Unlikely — possible but not expected |
| 3 | Possible — even odds over the period |
| 4 | Likely — expected unless something changes |
| 5 | Almost certain / already happening |

**Consequence (C) — severity if it occurs**

| C | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Minor — absorbed in normal operations |
| 2 | Moderate — a setback, recoverable in weeks |
| 3 | Serious — sets the plan back a quarter or damages a relationship |
| 4 | Major — threatens the raise, a community relationship, or safety |
| 5 | Severe — physical harm, loss of community trust at scale, or organisation-ending |

**Score bands**

| Band | Score (L×C) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | 20–25 | Act now; carry to every founder and governance review |
| **High** | 12–19 | Active mitigation with a named owner and a next action |
| **Medium** | 6–11 | Managed; review monthly |
| **Low** | 1–5 | Monitor; review quarterly |

## 4. Production SOP — the document to build

This is the operating document Goods does **not** yet have in a transferable form, and the one the diagnostic most pointed at. A facility guide and an operations handbook exist in draft, but they are not yet a clean, teachable SOP that a community operator could run from without a founder present. Below are the headings that SOP needs. The reason to build it is direct: **a written SOP is what lets the team grow without the founders being the single point of failure** — it is the concrete answer to the #1 risk in the register.

**Production SOP — required sections**

1. **Intake.** What plastic is accepted; sort HDPE/PP; exclude unsafe and contaminated material; weigh and log input. (Feeds the per-bed material claim — today 20 kg HDPE diverted per bed [VERIFIED from products.ts]; current actual input per bed needs confirming against the plant.)
2. **Shred and press parameters.** Shred to flake; weigh flakes; fill tray; heat and press temperature/time; cool the sheet. These parameters are the heart of the transferable knowledge — they are what turns "the founders know how" into "the plant can be taught."
3. **Cut and assembly QA.** CNC-cut parts; edge finish; assemble with the two galvanised steel poles and canvas (X-trestle tension design — poles thread through the canvas sleeves and the HDPE X-leg holes; the canvas is structural). Define the assembly QA check: tension correct, frame stable, no sharp edges, weight/dimension within spec (26 kg, 188 × 92 × 25 cm, 200 kg capacity).
4. **Defect handling.** What counts as a defect; what gets reworked vs scrapped; how scrapped HDPE re-enters intake (so the process does not "accidentally add to the waste problem" — a named environmental risk); who signs off a unit as pass.
5. **Dispatch.** QR-tag and register each unit before it leaves; record the destination; trigger the delivery and the post-delivery feedback path. Dispatch is the handoff from manufacture (founder-dependent today) to the asset register (systematised).

**A note that belongs in the SOP itself:** documenting these steps is not bureaucracy — it is the mechanism by which Goods reduces founder dependency as it grows and as the first community-employed operator comes on. The SOP is also the artifact that makes the on-country plant genuinely transferable to community ownership, which is the whole point of the model. Until the 50-bed in-source run happens, the shred/press parameters are partly modelled; the run is what turns them into measured, documented settings.

## 5. The human-in-loop rule

State it as policy, in one line, and hold it:

> **AI is a repository and a drafting aid. It is never the author of record. No document leaves Goods for any external party — SIH, mentors, funders, partners, board — unless a named core team member has audited it, understands every claim in it, has edited it where needed, and can defend it in detailed live Q&A.**

This rule has two halves, and both matter.

**Distinguish AI-as-repository from AI-as-authored-content.** The systems above use AI and automation well for what automation is good at: summarising source documents, finding contradictions, building review queues, drafting first-pass pages, comparing funders to criteria, producing admin and reporting drafts. That is the repository and aggregation role, and it is genuinely a strength. What automation must **not** do is be the final voice on anything that carries weight — community story interpretation, external language, consent decisions, funder claims, legal and governance claims, finance numbers, and relationship judgement. A polished draft is an input to a human, not a substitute for one.

**Enforce the human gate before anything ships.** A draft is not a deliverable. Before any document goes to an external party, a named owner from §3 must be able to stand behind every line of it under questioning. This is exactly the discipline the rest of the pack already runs on:

- **The evidence-label discipline** — every external number carries VERIFIED / WORKPAPER / MODELLED / TARGET (see `00-master-alignment-map.md`). A figure no owner can defend should not be in the document.
- **The pre-send checklist** (`00-master-alignment-map.md` §11) — nothing ships until a core team member has audited each external number and can defend it in live Q&A, Xero is re-pulled fresh, only consent-cleared voices/photos are used, and the legal-structure wording is approved.
- **The OCAP / consent gate** — before publishing any storyteller or community content externally, the consent trail is checked first; an incomplete or unverified trail means do not publish, and names or dates are never fabricated to fill a gap.

The diagnostic's deepest point across every area was that materials must be founder-owned, accurate, and defensible in live Q&A — that the real readiness gap is signed money and audited numbers, not more collateral. The human-in-loop rule is how that point becomes a daily operating habit rather than a good intention: the technology buys the founders more time with people; it does not get to speak for them.

## 2. The gap & the move — from advisory to a fiduciary board

The diagnostic named two linked gaps, and they resolve into one move.

**The commercial / scale-up gap.** The committee is strong on cultural authority, manufacturing and social-enterprise mission. It is light on the capabilities a venture needs precisely at the point of taking on capital and standing up production: product scale, distribution into hard geographies, procurement contracts, working capital and inventory risk, concessional debt and impact investment, product-liability and warranty exposure, and the trade-off between growing fast and protecting the mission. This is recorded in the wiki as the "known governance gap," and it is real.

**The no-fiduciary-board gap.** All Goods activity is migrating this financial year from Nicholas Marchesi (sole trader, ABN 21 591 780 066) into **A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd (ACN 697 347 676), trading as Goods on Country.** That company has directors, but it does not yet have an independent-majority fiduciary board with the finance oversight and decision authority a lender or catalytic investor will expect. Tax-deductible giving runs separately through The Butterfly Movement Ltd (DGR from FY2026-27, not live today), and the community-production entity's legal form is still being designed — so the governance question is live across three different legal positions, not one.

**The move (a plan with a trigger, not a structure that exists):**

1. **Recruit 1–2 independent directors** with commercial / social-enterprise scale-up experience — the capability the advisory committee is missing. These are *non-founder* directors, brought in to give the company independent oversight rather than to extend the founders' reach.
2. **Stand up a minimum-viable fiduciary board** for A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd t/a Goods on Country, with an independent majority, sitting over (not replacing) the advisory committee. The committee keeps doing what it does well; the board carries the legal duties the committee cannot.
3. **The trigger is the capital, not the calendar.** The board is stood up as the raise advances, because it gates the raise: **SEFA's ~AU$300K working-capital line is gated on an independent-majority fiduciary board plus the financial model plus a revenue covenant** [TARGET — ~70% modelled, not secured]. No board, no debt layer. This is also one of the five named raise-blockers (master map §9, item 4): "advisory ≠ board; need 1–2 directors with commercial/scale-up experience; reports not audited."

**Be honest about status.** Today there are **0 independent directors recruited** and **no fiduciary board in place.** The advisory committee is the only standing governance body. The board charter, delegations-of-authority matrix and director induction pack are all "not in place" in the policies register, each triggered by independent-director recruitment. This document describes the move; it does not claim the move is done. For external use, this is worded as a planned governance step the capital funds (it is QBE-shareable on that basis — risk register R7), never as an existing board.

## 4. Reporting cadence — from per-grant to a consolidated annual cycle

**The gap the diagnostic named.** Today, external reporting is driven **per grant** — each funder's report is assembled on its own timeline, against its own template, from numbers pulled fresh each time. That is workable at five grants; it does not scale, it invites the figures to drift between reports, and it produces no single annual account of the whole organisation. It is also why the same metric can appear three ways across documents — the discipline the master alignment map exists to enforce.

**The move.** Adopt a **consolidated annual reporting cycle** that sits over the per-grant obligations rather than replacing them. Grant acquittals still happen on each funder's schedule, but they are drawn from one consolidated annual account built once a year against one reconciled set of numbers — the same evidence-labelled figures used everywhere else (VERIFIED / WORKPAPER / MODELLED / TARGET). One cycle, one source of numbers, many outputs.

**The reporting products and their audiences.** The order matters and is set deliberately — community first, per the data-sovereignty practice (§5):

| Product | Primary audience | Cadence | Source of truth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Community / partner report-back | Community members and partners | Continuous + annual summary | Consented stories, photos/video shared back, asset-register delivery records |
| Annual impact & operations report | Funders and investors (catalytic, philanthropic, lender) | Annual (consolidated) | Reconciled figures + impact measurement method (05) |
| Per-grant acquittals | Each individual funder | Per grant (their schedule) | Drawn from the same consolidated account |
| Monthly governance pack | Founders, advisory committee, future board | Monthly | Risk register, cashflow, capital pipeline, delivery, product quality, partner feedback, story/data issues |

**Tie to the impact MEL method (05).** The annual report does not invent its own metrics — it reports against the impact measurement method, which already draws the line between what is independently checkable today, what is modelled from stated assumptions, and what is a future indicator not yet collected. Concretely, the consolidated cycle reports the **[VERIFIED]** assets (496 beds tracked in the asset register; 9 communities served; 20 kg HDPE diverted per bed), the **[MODELLED]** figures honestly labelled (2,660 kg HDPE diverted, Stretch only; projected in-source unit cost), and it does **not** dress a **[FUTURE]** indicator (bed survival at 12/24/36 months, wash frequency, any health outcome) as if it were live. The reporting cadence is the delivery mechanism; the MEL method (05) is the rule for what may be claimed. The claim ceiling holds in the report exactly as it holds everywhere else: washable beds and reliable washing machines *support* the environmental-health conditions needed to interrupt known infection pathways — no claimed reduction in rheumatic heart disease without partner clinical evidence.

**Status.** The consolidated annual cycle is **not yet in place** — reporting is still per-grant today. The monthly governance pack is the nearest-term build (it is also Proof 5 in the next-stage focus and feeds the future board). A first consolidated annual report is gated on the accountant-reviewed, Goods-only finance figure, which does not exist yet (master map §9, item 3) — so this section describes the target cycle and is explicit that the single reconciled number underneath it is still being produced.

## 5. Data & consent governance

This is the area's standing strength, and it is written as a practice, not a slogan. The core commitment: people own their stories, know how they are used, and can withdraw or change consent at any time. Community stories are not marketing stock.

**OCAP principles.** Goods works in the OCAP tradition — **Ownership, Control, Access, Possession** — for First Nations data sovereignty. OCAP is used as a working reference for how data and story are handled, not as decoration. In practice it means the community owns the story, controls how it is used, can access it, and the consent status travels with the data.

**Storyteller rights (the practice that is in place).**

- **Retraction / recall.** A person can withdraw or change consent at any time, including for photos, videos, quotes, transcripts and the way a story is used in reports or pitch material. Story retraction is supported.
- **Informed of use.** People whose stories are used are checked in with — including leaders such as Dianne Stokes — so they know where and how their story appears.
- **Shared back.** Photos and videos are shared back with community members rather than held only by Goods.
- **Consent travels with the data.** A story's permission status is attached to the story; when it moves — to the wiki, a newsletter, an Empathy Ledger syndication, a deck, the website, a funder or board pack — its consent status moves with it and is re-checked at the point of use.

Empathy Ledger (project context `goods-on-country`) is the technical direction for consent, withdrawal and story governance. It is treated as internal implementation evidence, not as mentor-facing proof, and Goods does not claim it already solves all impact measurement — the lived process still has to match the technical promise, and story-shareability still needs human review.

**The consent-cleared list.** Cleared for external use today: **Ivy Johnson, Dianne Stokes, Ray Nelson** [VERIFIED — consent confirmed]. **Everyone else is consent-pending** and is not named, quoted, or pictured in any external material. The shortness of this list (three of many storytellers) is itself a recorded gap and a measurement-method task — expanding it with properly documented consent, not a marketing task. The human anchor that *is* cleared, used as a lived-experience account and not a clinical result: Ray Nelson's bed — *"Since receiving their new beds, they are no longer experiencing back pains."*

**The consent gate — who approves external use.** Before any community **story, quote, name, photo or video** is used externally — public wiki, newsletter, Empathy Ledger syndication, social, funder or board deck, website — it goes through a consent gate. **Ben Knight owns this gate** (community relationships, story and consent sit with him in the role map). The rule is blocking: if the consent trail is incomplete or unverified, it does not publish, and a gap is never filled by inventing a name or a date. Unclear consent means the item goes to the review queue and only de-identified pattern evidence is used. This is risk register R2 — story/data consent, scored Critical (likelihood 4 × consequence 5 = 20) — and the gate is its primary control.

**Public-copy accuracy discipline (the governance claim, applied to our own words).** The same honesty owed to storytellers is owed to the figures:

- **Evidence labels on every external number.** VERIFIED (independently checkable — asset register, `products.ts`), WORKPAPER (Xero mirror, unaudited — never "audited revenue"), MODELLED (planning assumption, 0 beds assembled in-house), TARGET (sought, not signed). This is risk register R14 — public-copy / overclaim — and the labels are the control. Retired figures stay retired (the 9,920 kg / 9,225 kg plastic overclaim, "600+ shipped", "$445K", "audited revenue").
- **No entity overclaim.** Goods is not described as a registered charity or DGR; the DGR pathway (Butterfly, FY2026-27) is not described as live; community ownership is described as a direction worked toward, not a completed transfer. Per 04-entity-wording-block.md.
- **Rotate plaintext passwords out of Notion.** Plaintext credentials still sit in Notion — an open item under the consent/data risk (R2). They are a data-governance exposure and are to be rotated out; this is named here so it is owned, not left implicit.

## The founder-dependency problem

This is the standing **#1 risk** on the register, and it is flagged as **"already happening,"** not as a future possibility (master map §8). Every buyer conversation, every funder, every production fault, every community relationship and every governance question currently routes back through the same two people.

The closeness is what makes Goods good. It is also the ceiling. Goods cannot become a repeatable product system across remote communities if every relationship, decision, sale, story, and repair depends on Ben or Nic being in the room.

The single-point-of-failure map — six domains, two people:

| Domain | Who holds it today | What breaks if that person is unavailable |
|---|---|---|
| Sales / buyer pathways | Ben + Nic | Warm interest stalls; no one drives LOIs to signature (the match gate stays at $0 signed) |
| Operations / procurement | Nic (+ ACT admin) | Orders, freight, inventory, supplier management lose their only owner |
| Production | Nic | On-Country plant troubleshooting has no backup; scale cannot start |
| Finance / modelling | Ben + Nic (+ ACT) | Cashflow, working capital, unit economics have no dedicated discipline — and debt needs that discipline |
| Community relationships / consent | Ben | Consent and follow-up capacity is the constraint on serving more places safely |
| Governance | Ben + Nic | Advisory ≠ board; decisions concentrate in the founders |

The encouraging signal: production knowledge is already starting to leave founder hands. Nic has moved from teaching himself shredding, pressing, CNC and assembly to lower-skilled operators running parts of the process. That is the direction for everything: turn founder intuition into teachable systems. This plan is how that happens across the other five domains, production included.

## C. Open questions for Keith Rovers (MinterEllison)

Each item below is unsettled and is phrased as a decision to confirm. The "why it matters" column is the consequence of getting it wrong.

**C.1 — Go-forward entity form beneath A Curious Tractor.**
Should Goods on Country sit as (a) a trading name of A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd, (b) a separate subsidiary company, (c) a company limited by guarantee, or (d) a hybrid?
*Why it matters:* this determines whether liability, accounts and a future fiduciary board can be cleanly separated from the wider A Curious Tractor activity, which in turn affects investor and lender readiness (the SEFA working-capital line is gated on an independent-majority fiduciary board).

**C.2 — Legal form of the community-production entity.**
What is the right vehicle for on-Country production owned by Aboriginal-controlled or locally owned entities (e.g. ORIC corporation, co-operative, Pty Ltd with community shareholders, licence/franchise to a local entity)?
*Why it matters:* this is the structure that makes "community ownership" real rather than rhetorical. It also intersects with the **Supply Nation 51% precondition** below — the chosen vehicle has to be capable of being 51%+ First Nations-owned.

**C.3 — Contracting party / applicant / seller of record during the migration.**
While trading moves from the sole trader to A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd this FY, which entity is (a) the grant applicant per grant, (b) the seller of record for Stripe and institutional buyer contracts, and (c) the party that gives product warranties and carries product liability — and on what date does each switch?
*Why it matters:* match eligibility and clean contracting. A grant or contract signed by the wrong entity, or an ambiguous seller of record, creates problems for funders' due diligence and for any later novation.

**C.4 — Supply Nation 51% First Nations-ownership precondition.**
What entity structure achieves 51%+ First Nations ownership of the operating entity, and on what timeline?
*Why it matters:* a 51%+ First Nations-owned operating entity is a precondition for Supply Nation certification and roughly four of the eight procurement channels Goods is targeting. The Indigenous Procurement Policy threshold tightens around 1 July 2026, so the timing is live.

**C.5 — Mission lock.**
What mission-protection mechanism is credible and practical (purpose-lock constitution, asset lock, special/reserved rights for a community or Aboriginal-led body, B Corp or equivalent, a community-ownership deed)?
*Why it matters:* there is currently **no formal mission lock** — protection is described by the team as "cultural and relational" only, which the team itself says is "not enough to carry larger capital." A funder or lender providing six-figure catalytic or concessional capital will reasonably ask what stops the purpose drifting; today there is no legal answer.

**C.6 — Donor tax-deductibility timing and the related-party boundary.**
From what date can a donor to this work receive a tax-deductible (DGR) receipt through The Butterfly Movement Ltd, and what related-party agreement governs Butterfly funding or buying from the trading entity?
*Why it matters:* the DGR pathway is not live today, so any "tax-deductible" claim is premature until Butterfly's FY2026-27 handover and related-party safeguards are complete. Getting the boundary wrong creates both a compliance risk for the charity and an overclaim risk in public copy.

## Strategy memo — the catalytic case for the first $400K

*Prepared for catalytic investors and philanthropic partners. Read alongside the one-pager and deck in this folder. Figures are labelled [VERIFIED] / [WORKPAPER, unaudited] / [MODELLED] / [TARGET] so you can see exactly what is proven and what we are asking you to help prove.*

### 1. The problem we exist to solve

In remote Australia, the household goods people rely on for health and dignity are often the worst-designed things in the house. Beds are expensive to freight, impossible to wash, and quick to break. Washing machines are sold at metro prices, run far more cycles than a city household, and fail under conditions they were never built for. When they break, there is no recycling pathway, so they end up in the community dump. The human cost is direct and well understood: unwashable bedding feeds scabies and skin infections, which feed Strep A, rheumatic fever, and rheumatic heart disease — a disease almost unheard of in wealthy Australia and endemic in remote communities.

We put it plainly: a good bed can prevent heart disease. We make beds and washing machines as environmental-health hardware, not as charity or convenience.

### 2. What we have proven

Goods is past the concept stage. The proof is independently checkable, which is why our investor conversations open with a link rather than a logo wall.

We have deployed **496 beds across 9 communities** [VERIFIED — asset register, 29 May 2026], every one of them tracked by QR code in a live register you can scan. Of those, 133 are the current flagship Stretch Bed and 363 are the earlier Basket Bed, now discontinued and open-sourced. The most recent operation — 87 beds delivered to Utopia Homelands over two days in May 2026 with the Oonchiumpa Consultancy and Utopia Council — diverted 1.74 tonnes of plastic and put beds under 36 households directly. One recipient, Ray Nelson on the Plenty Highway, told us he is no longer waking with back pain. That is the unit of impact we are scaling.

We have a real product with real specifications. The Stretch Bed is a flat-pack, washable bed built from recycled HDPE plastic legs, two galvanised steel poles, and heavy-duty Australian canvas. It weighs 26 kg, carries 200 kg, assembles in about five minutes with no tools, and is designed for a ten-year-plus life with a five-year warranty. Each bed diverts 20 kg of plastic from landfill. ### 3. The honest commercial reality

We are not going to hide the gap, because a catalytic investor is precisely the partner who funds it.

Goods has received **AU$649,710.79** to date [WORKPAPER — Xero mirror, 29 May 2026, unaudited], against AU$732,210.79 billed, at an 88.8% collection rate. Around 89% of that is grant-funded, by design. Direct commercial revenue through the shop is AU$90 — three orders. That figure is not embarrassing; it is the point. We have proven that communities want the product and that funders will pay for deployments. We have not yet proven that the product can be made at a price that lets the business stand on its own.

At today's cost, the Stretch Bed sells for AU$750 [VERIFIED] against a marginal cost of AU$684.79 [WORKPAPER] and a fixed cost block near AU$109,500 a year [MODELLED]. On those numbers, breakeven sits around 1,679 beds a year while we currently deploy about 130. That is out of reach, and we will not pretend otherwise.

The lever that changes the picture is in-sourcing the plastic. We have verified an 8.6× markup on the HDPE legs we currently buy from an external supplier. Our containerised production model — collect, shred, press, cut, and assemble on country — projects a unit cost falling from AU$684.79 to AU$425.74 in a factory configuration and AU$270.74 in a community configuration [all MODELLED]. At those costs, breakeven drops to 333–338 beds a year, which is within sight of demand. The critical caveat: we have assembled zero beds in-house so far. This number is a hypothesis, not a result.

### 4. What we are asking catalytic capital to do

We are raising **AU$900K–$1M in blended, non-equity capital**, and the immediate task is to close the first **~AU$400K of signed, match-eligible commitments by 31 August 2026**.

That deadline is set by the QBE Catalysing Impact program, which offers up to AU$400K from a $1M pool, on a basis of at least 1:1 matching to the external investment we secure — the more we raise, the more the match unlocks, to the $400K ceiling. The program prioritises repayable finance over grants, and the matched capital must be legally binding (signed letters of commitment, loan or investment agreements) and is verified by the program at the September 2026 Stage 2 application. The QBE match is an output of money raised, not an input. As of today we hold zero signed commitments — and we are clear that this is a conversation problem, not a discovery problem. Our warmest funders are watching each other; the first signed dollar unlocks the rest.

The stack is designed junior-to-senior so that philanthropic and catalytic capital de-risk the commercial debt that follows. Grants form the junior layer (~AU$500K target — Snow R4/R5, Centrecorp's next round, VFFF, with Butterfly/DGR upside once that vehicle is live in FY2026-27). Every figure except the QBE invitation is a target, not a commitment.

Critically, we are not raising equity. The end-state of this business is community-owned production. Taking external equity that needs an exit would contradict the thing we are building. Equity becomes interesting only inside a future model where communities themselves are the owners.

The first and most important use of funds is small and specific: **AU$60–80K to run a 50-bed in-source production run.** That experiment turns our central economic claim from modelled to measured. If the run confirms AU$425.74, we scale the raise on proven economics. If it misses, we re-base the raise honestly at AU$684.79 and adjust. The rest of the use of funds — plant capex (~$300–400K), a working-capital buffer (~$150–200K), and two senior hires (~$200K) — follows that result.

### 5. Why this is a catalytic investment, not a growth one

Goods sits at the end of "proven product, proven delivery" and the very start of "proven repeatable commercial engine." We are pre-commercial-revenue, we do not yet have a fiduciary board, and our core economic lever is unproven. A conventional impact investor cannot yet underwrite us. That is the exact gap catalytic capital exists to cross — funding the de-risking experiment, funding the governance and evaluation capacity around the edges, and accepting concessional terms so that commercial debt can follow. One catalytic dollar here is structured to unlock several commercial dollars behind it.

The structure that fits is a project-level plus company-level blend, anchored on the containerised production plant, with grants and guarantees on top and concessional debt at the base. An Indigenous-owned blueprint already exists — WINYA Furniture holds 36 federal contracts as a Supply Nation supplier — so the path from social enterprise to credible remote-market manufacturer is not theoretical.

### 6. What we still have to build (and are not hiding)
