# The voices are the evidence

> Flagship narrative for blog, stories page and deck use. Every quote below is
> registry-verbatim and cleared (Ben pass, 2026-07-20). Claims-status: delivery figures are
> register-verified; everything else is a person speaking for themselves. No em dashes, no
> banned words. Source of truth: the Voice Impact Model (`wiki/investor/12-voice-impact-model.md`).

---

Margaret Lloyd has lived on her homeland in the Utopia region for about thirty years. When the
truck arrived, she was sleeping in a tent beside a house that no longer kept the weather out.

"I sleep in the tent but look, there's my house or the rubbish house. No mattress. Just for
chucking us blanket, that's all I was sleeping."

The bed went together in front of her. Steel poles through canvas, recycled plastic legs, five
minutes, no tools. Her whole review was seven words.

"Our bed is right. That's good. Good."

That recording is 292 words long. We think it is one of the most important documents we hold.

## Numbers prove scale. Voices prove meaning.

Goods on Country has delivered 540 beds across 11 communities, with 22 washing machines working
in community and 3,540kg of waste plastic turned into bed frames. We track every one of those
numbers in a register, and we label every figure honestly: delivered, measured or modelled,
never blurred.

But a register cannot tell you why a bed matters. Only Margaret can.

"Because I was sleeping on floor, turn around, turn around. No comfortable nothing."

So alongside the register we keep a second spine: every interview a storyteller has recorded
with us, held in the Empathy Ledger, analysed line by line, and never used without that
person's clearance. Twenty-nine voices so far. Around seventy-eight thousand words. Every quote
we publish traces back to a transcript, a timestamp and a consent record.

We call it the Voice Impact Model, and its first rule is simple: no number without a voice, and
no voice reduced to a number.

## What the voices agree on

When you read all twenty-nine transcripts together, patterns appear that no survey would find,
because nobody was asked to find them. Twenty-three separate voices, in different communities,
describe the same broken supply chain: freight that doubles the price, products that fail in
months, the long drive to town to replace them. The problem statement in our funder documents
was not written by us. It was said to us, over and over.

Dianne Stokes, the Warumungu Elder who designed and named our washing machine, Pakkimjalki
Kari, put the alternative in one sentence when the machine reached her homeland.

"I will know where to come. If I need to wash my blanket, I don't have to go do it in town.
Laundry, do it here."

And she told us, on the record, how any organisation should arrive in a community.

"If you have an idea what place you'd go to, first go and talk to the elders. Sit down and talk
to them." Then: "They need to hear from the ground, who wants it, who's gonna do it, who's out
there, who needs help?"

That is not a quote we decorate reports with. It is the operating manual.

## The direction of the flow

Shayne Bloomfield drove the biggest delivery we have made, forty beds and a laundromat refit in
Maningrida, built with young people from the aged care service. Weeks later his family were
still sending messages.

"Today my mum still gets text messages from her family saying, I can't believe how good this
bed's going. I don't want to get outta bed in the morning 'cause it's just so comfortable."

But the line of his we keep coming back to is about direction.

"A lot of people say community is community and they need what we've got. When you flip it, we
need what they've got."

That flip is the whole model. The design authority came from community. The problem statement
came from community. The proof comes from community. The destination is that the making, and
the story of the making, belong to community too. Ownership is a pathway we are on, not a claim
we make. The same test we apply to a factory applies to this archive: if the transcripts stay
but the authority to interpret them leaves with us, nothing really changed.

## Why we work this way

Most impact measurement asks communities to produce data for someone else's report. The people
who live the outcomes become rows in it. We think the evidence should work the other way: the
storyteller owns the recording, consents line by line to what is shared, can withdraw it, and
speaks in their own words or not at all. The numbers keep us honest about scale. The voices
keep us honest about everything else.

Dianne, unprompted, told us what a fair exchange feels like.

"I didn't get money, but those two young fellows that are doing something, they offered us and
shared their blessings with us. So I shared my blessings to them."

That is the standard. Not impact reported. Blessings shared, both ways, on the record, on
Country.
