# Health evidence appendix — Goods on Country Theory of Change

**Status:** v0.1, 2026-05-29. Produced by a fan-out research workflow (one researcher + one independent fact-checker per claim) against primary sources: peer-reviewed literature, national clinical guidelines, and government agency reports. Companion to `2026-05-29-goods-theory-of-change-and-mel.md`. Founders to confirm before external use.

Each claim carries a verdict, the citation, the defensible figure, and a one-line caveat.

## 1. Scabies prevalence in remote First Nations children
**Claim: about one in three remote Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children have scabies.**
- **Verdict: Substantiated, with a wording fix.**
- Citation: Davidson L, Knight J, Bowen AC (2020), "Skin infections in Australian Aboriginal children: a narrative review", *Medical Journal of Australia* 212(5):231-237. Corroborated by Gramp & Gramp (2021), *PLOS Neglected Tropical Diseases*.
- Defensible figure: **as high as one-third (up to about 33 to 35%)**. Community studies span 16.1% to 35%; some sit as low as 12 to 14%.
- Caveat: say "as high as one-third (up to about 33 to 35%)", not "one in three children have scabies". The 33% is the top of a range, not an average.

## 2. The skin-infection to rheumatic-heart-disease pathway
**Claim: skin infection (scabies, impetigo, skin sores) → Group A Streptococcus → acute rheumatic fever → rheumatic heart disease is an established causal pathway.**
- **Verdict: Partial.** The throat arm is well evidenced; the skin arm is recognised but still emerging.
- Citation: RHDAustralia (2025), *Australian Guideline for the Prevention, Diagnosis and Management of Acute Rheumatic Fever and Rheumatic Heart Disease* (Edition 3.3), Chapter 5: Primary Prevention.
- Defensible figure: median Strep A skin-sore prevalence among First Nations children in remote settings is **44.5%** (IQR 34.0 to 49.2%), described as "almost one in two affected at any one time". (This measures impetigo, not scabies.)
- Caveat: present as a recognised, biologically plausible, still-emerging link, not settled trial proof. The guideline added skin-infection treatment to primary prevention but grades the human evidence as "emerging".

## 3. RHD burden and cost
**Claim: RHD disproportionately affects remote First Nations communities; an episode or surgery costs ~AUD $250,000.**
- **Verdict: Partial.** Disparity strongly substantiated; the **$250,000 cost figure is unsubstantiated**.
- Citation: Cannon J, Katzenellenbogen JM, Wyber R, et al. (2018), *The cost of inaction on rheumatic heart disease: technical report*, END RHD CRE, Telethon Kids Institute. Disparity confirmed via AIHW ARF/RHD report (2024).
- Defensible figures: **94% of new acute rheumatic fever cases in Australia occur among Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander people**. A surgical RHD inpatient admission costs about **AUD $67,000 to $70,000** (2015 prices). No primary source supports $250,000 per episode or surgery.
- Caveat: do not cite "$250,000 per surgery" (it conflates per-episode cost with the report's aggregate projections, e.g. $317M total medical care for all projected cases 2016-2031). Use "~$70,000 per surgical admission" per-event, or the $317M aggregate at system scale.

## 4. Working washing machines in remote homes
**Claim: about 59% of remote First Nations homes lack a working washing machine.**
- **Verdict: Partial.** The figure is quoted verbatim from an NGO source, is itself uncited upstream, and is narrower than the claim.
- Citation: FRRR (2022), "Remote laundries building strong remote communities" (figure originates with the Remote Laundries Project; no upstream source). Primary-survey anchor: Healthabitat, *Housing for Health*.
- Defensible figure: FRRR states "59% [of remote community homes] do not have washing machines" (NT context). Healthabitat survey data: laundry services working rose from ~29 to 60% (definition-dependent) before intervention to 74 to 92% after.
- Caveat: FRRR says homes "do not have" a machine, not "lack a working" one, and the context is NT remote communities, not all remote First Nations nationally.

## 5. Health hardware and health
**Claim: functioning washing and laundry facilities (health hardware) reduce skin infection and improve health.**
- **Verdict: Substantiated.**
- Citation: NSW Department of Health (2010), *Closing the Gap: 10 Years of Housing for Health in NSW*. Peer-reviewed corroboration: Pholeros et al. (2013), *International Journal of Circumpolar Health*.
- Defensible figure: a **40% lower rate of hospital separations for infectious diseases** for Housing for Health residents versus a rural NSW Aboriginal control population (71 communities, 2,230 houses), plus an "over 2-fold improvement in the ability to wash people and to wash clothes and bedding".
- Caveat: the 40% is the infectious-disease aggregate. Skin infections specifically fell 19% and per-disease categories did not reach statistical significance. It is an observational before/after study with a control group, not a randomised trial.

## 6. Crowding, bedding and infection
**Claim: inadequate bedding and household crowding contribute to skin and respiratory infection.**
- **Verdict: Substantiated for crowding and inadequate housing; the bedding-specific limb is an inference.**
- Citation: AIHW and NIAA (2026), "2.01 Housing — Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Health Performance Framework". Peer-reviewed: Ali, Foster & Hall (2018).
- Defensible figure: AIHW: "overcrowding and inadequate infrastructure contribute to elevated rates of infectious, respiratory, and cardiovascular diseases, as well as skin infections, otitis media, trachoma and acute rheumatic fever." Severe overcrowding (2021 Census): 5.6% of First Nations households in Very Remote areas. Ali et al.: skin disease associated with crowding (crude OR 2.71 to 2.92).
- Caveat: sources attribute risk to crowding and inadequate infrastructure, not to "bedding" specifically. Frame the bedding angle as Goods' own inference.

## How to use

- **Safe in front of funders as-is:** the Housing for Health 40% infectious-disease reduction (claim 5); 94% of new ARF cases being First Nations (claim 3); crowding to infection (claim 6).
- **Use only with the corrected figure:** scabies "up to about one-third (16 to 35%)" (claim 1); "~$70,000 per surgical RHD admission" (claim 3), never $250,000.
- **Present as recognised but emerging / qualified:** the skin-infection to RHD pathway (claim 2); the 59% washing-machine figure as an FRRR / Remote Laundries Project figure for NT (claim 4); bedding-specific harm as Goods' inference (claim 6).
- **Drop entirely:** "$250,000 per RHD surgery."
