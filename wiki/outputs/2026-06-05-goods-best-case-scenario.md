# The Goods Best Case: what the data says about the model, the people, and the compounding

**Built:** 2026-06-05 · Companion to the Cost Lab (`/sites/cost-lab`, "Seed fleet of 3" scenario button) and the Cost Lab Playbook.
**Status discipline:** VERIFIED = invoices or Xero. MODELLED = the locked 2026-05-29 engine, pending vendor quotes. Everything in the compounding section is modelled.

## 1. The engine is one number: the markup gap

A finished HDPE leg kit costs $344.05 (verified invoices) while the shredded plastic inside it costs about $40. That 8.6x gap is where the business lives. Owning the press captures $194.05/bed of it, dropping marginal cost from $684.79 (Buy-Kit) to $425.74 (factory) and lifting contribution from about $65 to about $324 per bed at the $750 price. The containers, the loans and the compounding are all machinery for capturing that gap in community hands.

## 2. The most important finding: dignity is economically neutral

Community production paying a $130/bed fair wage lands at $420.74 marginal, about $5/bed BELOW the factory path. Fair wages do not cost the model anything. There is no trade-off between viable economics and paying people properly. This is the anchor line for every impact-investor conversation.

**What one container does for people, per year at 500 beds (modelled):**

| Measure | Value | Note |

## 3. Why repayable finance fits

A container costs about $125K (modelled mid-range, quotes pending). At capacity it generates about $140K/yr of surplus, repaying its own build cost in under a year. At half capacity (250 beds/yr) it repays in about 2 years. A 3 to 5 year repayable loan is comfortable against that. QBE's stated preference for repayable finance over grants is therefore in our favour: the asset pays for itself, the loan just brings it forward. The right structures are recoverable grants and patient debt with a community-ownership transfer trigger.

## 4. The best case (modelled end to end)

Seed the fleet with the QBE stack: $400K signed external capital + $400K QBE match = $800K, deployed as roughly 3 containers ($375K) + 2 years of central fixed block ($219K) + inventory and site setup (about $200K).

- 3 containers = 1,500 beds/yr. Contribution about $494K, minus $72K site overheads, minus the $109.5K central block = about $312K/yr surplus.
- That surplus self-funds roughly 2.5 new containers per year.
- By year 5: 10 to 12 containers, 5,000+ beds/yr, 100+ tonnes of plastic/yr, $650K+/yr in community wages, about $1M/yr of surplus compounding.
- Then transfers begin. Each container handed to a CATSI corp takes its self-feeding loop with it. What compounds nationally is not Goods revenue, it is community-owned productive capacity.

Run it live: the Cost Lab "Seed fleet of 3" scenario sets every dial to this case.

## 5. The real constraint is demand, not cost

Break-even is about 338 beds/yr on the factory path and 496 beds are already delivered (verified). The binding constraint in the best case is signed demand: 500 beds/yr per container needs real buyers. The pipeline supports it (NPY 200 to 350, WHSAC 500+, Centrecorp repeat, Miwatj, Homeland Schools) but none of it is signed yet. The same signatures that unlock the QBE match are what make the compounding math real. One activity, both payoffs. This is why the LOI close plan is the highest-leverage work in the company.

## 6. Honesty rails

- Container costs are modelled pending three vendor quotes (shredder, hot press, CNC). The Vendor Quote Truth Test play exists to shrink that range first.
- The compounding table assumes every bed made is sold and a smooth run. A slow year stretches the doubling.
- QBE is up to $400,000, at least matched by signed capital, not secured until awarded (Nov 2026).
- Contribution is not profit. The fixed block is funded first, always.
