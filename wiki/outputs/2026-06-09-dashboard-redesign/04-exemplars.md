# World-Class Impact & Transparency Dashboards — Exemplars for the Goods Partner Page Redesign

Nine real, named examples plus the studios/people behind them. Each entry gives the single most transferable move for a small, community-first social enterprise. Verification note: these are freshly web-verified (June 2026) except where flagged. Design-team attributions are noted only where the search surfaced them.

---

## 1. charity: water — "Proof" + The Spring
- **URL:** charitywater.org · case study: germanetcheverry.design/charity-water
- **What they do well:** Every donation links to the *exact* funded project with GPS coordinates, dated photos, and remote sensor status (Flowing / Needs Maintenance / Inactive / Unknown). They built lithium-powered well sensors that text status back, so "is it still working?" is answered with live field data, not a one-time completion photo. The 100% model (separate operating fund) means donor money provably reaches the field.
- **Most transferable technique:** **The in-service status flag.** Don't just report "X beds delivered" — report each asset's current state (in service / needs maintenance / unknown). This is exactly your washer-ping data and bed register. A single honest status word per asset is more credible than a big cumulative number. The "Unknown" category is the trust-builder: they publish what they *can't* confirm.

## 2. GiveDirectly — GDLive + live performance chart
- **URL:** givedirectly.org · live.givedirectly.org · design by Brooklyn Digital Foundry
- **What they do well:** The performance chart on the homepage *mirrors the internal dashboard the team uses in the field* — donors see the same numbers, updating in real time, that staff see. GDLive is an unfiltered newsfeed where recipients report their own experience (first name + country only). They also publish an annual fraud/harm/safety report.
- **Most transferable technique:** **"Same dashboard we use internally."** Frame your live metrics as *the operational truth*, not a marketing cut. This is your strongest forward-looking move: a partner sees the real asset register and roadmap, not a polished funder-facing veneer. Also note their consent-minimal identity model (first name + country) — it maps cleanly to your non-extractive rule.

## 3. Watsi — radical transparency open ledger
- **URL:** watsi.org · 2025 year-in-review: watsi.org/2025
- **What they do well:** A public "Transparency Document" (literally a shared spreadsheet) logs *every* financial transaction with screenshots of fund transfers, per patient. Post-treatment photos and outcomes close the loop on each case. 100% model, credit-card fees absorbed.
- **Most transferable technique:** **One link to the raw ledger.** For a small enterprise, you can't out-design a foundation — but you can out-*open* them. Offer a "see the underlying data" link (your Xero-reconciled summary, the asset register) so the dashboard is the readable layer over a verifiable source. Radical openness substitutes for scale.

## 4. GiveWell — radical reasoning + the "Our Mistakes" page
- **URL:** givewell.org/about/our-mistakes · givewell.org/how-we-work/transparency
- **What they do well:** They publish their full reasoning, key assumptions, and *largest sources of uncertainty* on every grant page — and a standing "Our Mistakes" page logging errors and lessons. They pioneered the public mistakes page (now copied widely).
- **Most transferable technique:** **Name your largest source of uncertainty on every metric.** This is the operational form of your COUNTED / MODELLED / NOT-YET-MEASURED grading. Don't just grade the number — add one sentence: "what would change our mind / what we can't yet confirm." A "What we're still learning" or "where we might be wrong" block builds more trust than any vanity stat, and it's cheap for a small team.

## 5. Our World in Data / Gapminder (Hans Rosling) — fewer numbers, more story
- **URL:** ourworldindata.org · gapminder.org
- **What they do well:** Rosling told 200 countries × 200 years through one moving bubble chart — five variables, one narrative, four minutes. Gapminder's whole mission is "fact-based worldviews everyone can understand": lead with the human story, let one well-chosen visual carry the weight, kill the rest.
- **Most transferable technique:** **One hero visual that answers the human question, not a metric wall.** Your hero should answer "what changed for people" with a single well-framed graphic (e.g. the funding→goods→still-in-service→outcome pathway), and push the detailed figures below. Resist the dashboard-clutter instinct. Context beats density.

## 6. Acumen / 60 Decibels — Lean Data (the voice of the recipient as the metric)
- **URL:** acumen.org/lean-data · 60decibels.com
- **People:** Sasha Dichter & Tom Adams (built it inside Acumen, spun out as 60 Decibels 2019)
- **What they do well:** Lean Data puts the *customer/recipient voice at the centre of impact measurement* — fast, mobile, direct from the people served, rather than slow third-party evaluations. The output is benchmarked, comparable, and outcome-focused ("did their life actually change?"), not output-counting.
- **Most transferable technique:** **A community-comparison / "in their words" panel beside every metric.** This is the methodological backbone for your "every section gets a community comparison + an Indigenous voice." Pair each output number (beds, washers) with a consented outcome quote and, where possible, a simple proportion ("X of Y families reported Z"). Output-vs-outcome made visible.

## 7. Stripe Climate / Frontier — corporate impact disclosure done cleanly
- **URL:** stripe.com/climate · climate.stripe.com (per-business impact pages)
- **What they do well:** Each participating business gets its own clean impact page; commitments are stated as advance market commitments ($1B+ by 2030) with *tonnes contracted vs tonnes actually removed* kept distinct. The disclosure separates "money committed" from "outcome delivered" — never conflating a pledge with a result.
- **Most transferable technique:** **Separate "committed / targeted" from "delivered."** This is the spine of Ben's forward reframe. State funding *targets* (the QBE up-to-$400K matched ask) in a visibly different register from delivered results, so ambition reads as ambition and never as an overclaim. The per-partner page model also validates your reusable-template approach.

## 8. Maiam nayri Wingara + CARE Principles — Indigenous data sovereignty (the governance exemplar)
- **URL:** maiamnayriwingara.org/mnw-principles · CARE: datascience.codata.org/articles/10.5334/dsj-2020-043
- **What they do well (the standard, not a dashboard):** The Australian Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander data sovereignty principles + the global CARE principles (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) establish that data must be *contextual and disaggregated*, must empower self-determination, and must be accountable to community. This is the framework your non-extractive rule already cites.
- **Most transferable technique:** **A visible "data sovereignty / how we handle community data" note as a first-class section, not fine print.** State who controls the data, what's aggregated vs withheld, and that community holds authority. For a First Nations-facing enterprise this is a *credibility asset*, not a compliance chore — make it a designed panel (it pairs naturally with your per-section "equity note + confidence note"). Cite CARE + AIATSIS explicitly.

## 9. Tides Foundation report microsite (studio: Acton Circle) + the scrollytelling craft layer
- **URL:** actoncircle.co (studio) · constructive.co 2025 nonprofit annual-report roundup
- **Studios named:** Acton Circle (social-impact design studio, did the Tides interactive report), Constructive, New Media Campaigns
- **What they do well:** Long-scroll microsite with scroll-triggered animation, *progressive disclosure*, and strictly linear navigation reinforcing "one story, one goal" — color-blocking and a bold modern grid for visual hierarchy. This is the craft reference for the exact build Ben wants: one long page, sticky scrollspy nav, no tabs.
- **Most transferable technique:** **"One story, one goal" linear scroll with progressive disclosure + sticky scrollspy.** Exactly Ben's brief: sections reveal as you scroll, headline metric first, detail on demand, a persistent nav rail that tracks position. This is the structural pattern to build on — it directly replaces the rejected tabbed layout.

## What to AVOID (anti-pattern list for the build)

1. **Vanity / cumulative-only metrics.** A giant "10,000 lives touched" with no denominator, no time bound, no in-service status. Always pair with "of how many" and "still working?" (charity: water's status flag is the antidote.)
2. **Conflating committed with delivered.** Never let a funding *target* (QBE up-to-$400K, *matched, not yet secured*) sit in the same visual register as a delivered result. Stripe/Frontier keep these strictly separate.
3. **Extractive imagery & identity.** No identifiable faces, GPS, household names, contact details, or raw issue text without documented consent. GiveDirectly's first-name-+-country minimum is the ceiling, not the floor, for you.
4. **Dashboard clutter / metric walls.** Resist the "show everything" reflex. Rosling's lesson: one hero visual, detail on demand. Tabs and 20-tile grids fail the "what changed for people" test.
5. **Transparency as a buzzword (no substance).** GiveDirectly's explicit warning: don't *say* transparent, *be* it — link the raw source (Watsi's open ledger, your Xero-reconciled data).
6. **Hiding uncertainty.** Omitting "what we can't yet confirm" reads as either naive or evasive. GiveWell's mistakes page and "largest source of uncertainty" turn admitted limits into trust.
7. **Dark patterns / forced donation framing.** No guilt-trip CTAs, no pre-checked recurring gifts, no manufactured scarcity. The forward-looking "where we're going / how to back the growth" frame should invite, not pressure.
8. **Asset-first instead of community-first.** Leading with the bed/washer as a product rather than with the family and the on-country ownership pathway. Every section needs the community comparison + voice + equity note.

## The through-line for your redesign
The forward reframe Ben wants maps cleanly onto these exemplars: **lead with the human question (Rosling), state targets-not-thanks in a register distinct from delivered results (Stripe/Frontier), prove ongoing community-owned assets with live in-service status (charity: water) over the same data the team uses internally (GiveDirectly), grade every number for confidence and name its biggest uncertainty (GiveWell), centre the recipient voice beside each metric (Acumen Lean Data), make data sovereignty a designed first-class panel (CARE / Maiam nayri Wingara), keep one link to the raw ledger (Watsi), and build it as one linear scrollytelling page with sticky scrollspy and progressive disclosure (Acton Circle / Tides).**
