## The spine

**Working title:** *Sit down and ask us*

**Protagonist:** the relationship — the slow transfer of power from two founders to the communities who were never asked. Ben and Nic appear only as the ones being taught.

**The real message, in one line:** *"We know what we need. Sit down and ask us, make it with us, and leave the making with us."* The bed is the evidence. The relationship is the story.

**Credit rule:** every proof point is a three-beat chain — **they said → it changed → it returned.** Community is the subject of the first and last beat; the organisation only appears in the middle, as the verb.

## Movement 3 — The Proof

**The beat:** Communities assess the bed like the experts they are, and their feedback physically changes the product. Nobody performs gratitude.

**Voices**
> "Sleep on a good mattress. For the back of legs, muscles." — **Gloria Turner, Kalgoorlie — the first person to ever sleep on the mattress**

> "I think it's a great bed. Nice bed. And it's more lower, um, more comfortable." — **Melissa Jackson, Tennant Creek**

> "You feel more safe when you sleep in a bed. It's different than sleeping on the couch, and then sleeping on the ground." — **Alfred Johnson, Palm Island**

> "…the beds were on the ground. It's a safety thing… a lot of snakes, so everyone wants to be off the ground." — **Fred Campbell, Youth Case Worker, Oonchiumpa**

**Design receipts — feedback that physically changed the product**
- Norman Frank asked for three more beds **in maroon** — his colour request became a design feature.
- Basket Bed → Stretch Bed: washable, movable, repairable, each change driven by real community feedback.
- Bed height set by elders' knees and mobility.
- Dirty beds observed in Tennant Creek May 2025, no washer access (**Dianne Stokes specifically** lacked one, not just "the community" generally, per the visit log) → pulled the washing-machine line forward.

## Movement 4 — The Reversal

**The beat:** The turn. It was never a delivery program. It is the community's project, and the record proves it. Power visibly changes hands: names, language, consent.

**Voices**

## From story to investment

**Why invest**
1. **Demand is proven by community behaviour, not surveys.** Dianne received one bed and came back for twenty, offering to pay herself.
2. **The product was de-risked by two years of design led by community, on country.** Every design change traces to a named person's request.
3. **The workforce and the ownership path already exist.** Oonchiumpa: 100% Aboriginal-owned, paid roles stood up, asking about owning a plant.
4. **The story infrastructure is investable in itself.** Empathy Ledger consent architecture means every claim is auditable back to a consenting voice.
5. **The exit is the point.** Success looks like communities owning production and Goods becoming unnecessary.

**What the money buys (canon figures only)**

| Item | Figure |
|---|---|
| Beds delivered to date | 496 across 9 communities |
| Washing machines in community | 16 |
| Plastic diverted | 2,660 kg |
| Revenue (Goods carve-out, accountant-signed) | AU$713,827 |
| Current ask | AU$400K (QBE, signed by 31 Aug 2026) on top of SEFA $300K + Snow $100K + Centrecorp $75K |

**How to invest, per funder**
- **QBE / catalytic:** funds the Stage 2 production circuit — the machinery of handover.
- **SEFA / debt:** working capital against the community-validated order pipeline.
- **Snow / philanthropic partnership:** the RHD health chain and the storytelling infrastructure, deepening the longest listening relationship.
- **Centrecorp / place-based:** Central Australia production with Oonchiumpa, beds and jobs on the same country the funding comes from.

**Closing line:** *"We know what we need. Sit down and ask us, make it with us, and leave the making with us."* The funding buys the middle of that sentence, so communities can own the end of it.
