## How to read this blueprint

- The deck marches in the order investors **price risk**: believe the problem → understand the approach → see proof → trust the team & governance → see the impact thesis → see the ask → see the future.
- **Heart and head alternate, never blend.** Story slides (2, 9, 18) carry emotion; metric slides (5, 7, 8) answer "is this real, working, moving the right way?" Keep the macro number on a different beat from the human story (the "drop-in-the-bucket" device).
- **Community is the hero. Goods is the guide. The funder is the catalyst who joins the story.** No charity / saviour framing anywhere.
- **CANON discipline (non-negotiable, every slide):** numbers come from this brief; everything else is tagged `[modelled]` / `[unverified]` / `[target]`. Xero is the source of truth, not GHL. **No surplus claim, no "230% self-reliance"** — the connected entity runs an FY26 net loss (the correct scale-up story). Public-cleared voices: **Linda Turner, Alfred Johnson, Norman Frank, Mykel, and Fred** — the Utopia-trip voices were consent-cleared by Ben (2026-06-02). **Mykel is the recurring human spine** (enters Slide 9, closes Slide 18). Full cleared roster + beat-by-beat weave in the companion **Voice Map (`05-goods-voice-map.md`)**. (Demand *quantities* are a separate gate — they stay qualitative until A10 provenance.)
- **Revenue basis (single number, one as-at date):** cite the **live Xero paid figure — $650,910.79 paid / $733,410.79 raised / $82,500 due, as at 1 Jun 2026** — NOT the older "~$588K+". Re-pull Xero and re-date before any external share.
- **Confidence-tagging the cost-down curve:** only **Buy-Kit** and **Factory** marginal costs are *engine-locked* (hard BOM in `cost-model/engine.ts`). The **Community** state is **`[modelled]`** — it rests on a $130/bed fair-wage assumption (band $100–160, marked "inferred" in the engine) and a $0 free-feedstock assumption. Never group Community under "engine-locked v6".
- **TFN is not clean invested capital yet:** the only skin-in-the-game figure to cite is the **verified press capex (~$110,046 spent)**. ### Slide 6A — Alternatives & the moat (what communities buy today)
- **One-line message:** The alternative isn't "no bed" — it's a bed that fails in remote conditions, hauled in at freight cost, and dumped within months. That failure pattern *is* the moat.
- **Content / bullets (substitutes analysis, evidence-led — not one diagnostic sentence):**
  - **What communities buy today:** mass-market flat-pack beds (not washable, not repairable, fail under remote dust/damp/overcrowding), and commercial white goods that don't survive the conditions. The named market signal: **one Alice Springs provider sells ~$3M/yr of washing machines into remote communities — most end up in dumps within months** `[CLAUDE.md / sector knowledge — to provenance-source in A11]`. That is the substitute, and it is a recurring-failure spend, not a solution.
  - **Why incumbents don't pivot:** the economics only work if you (a) engineer for repair/washability, (b) flat-pack for barge/road freight, and (c) localise the supply chain — none of which a city-based volume manufacturer will do for a remote, dispersed, low-margin market. The remoteness that makes the problem hard is exactly what keeps incumbents out.
  - **The Goods moat (three layers, not one sentence):** (1) **product** — washable, repairable, flat-pack, no-tools, 10+ yr design life; (2) **economics** — owning the press (Slide 4) makes the unit cost defensible; (3) **structural** — Supply-Nation 51% certification + IPP Mandatory Set-Aside (Slide 16) makes the community-owned plant a *preferred procurement supplier*, which a non-Indigenous incumbent structurally cannot replicate. The third layer is the durable moat.
- **Visual / data:** A 3-column compare table — *Mass-market flat-pack bed* · *Remote white-goods status quo* · *Goods Stretch Bed* — scored on washable / repairable / flat-pack-freight / survives-remote / local-supply / IPP-eligible. The "$3M/yr to the dump" stat as a callout.
- **Speaker note:** A moat claim resting on one diagnostic sentence is thin for DD — so name the actual substitutes, name the recurring-failure spend (the $3M/yr washing-machine-to-dump market), and explain *why* incumbents won't pivot. End on the structural moat (IPP set-aside), because that's the one a competitor can't buy their way around.
- **Exact canonical numbers:** ~$3M/yr washing-machine-to-dump market `[sector knowledge — provenance pending A11]`; product specs per Slide 6.
