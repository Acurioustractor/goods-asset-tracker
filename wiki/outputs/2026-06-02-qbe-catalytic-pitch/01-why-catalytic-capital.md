# Why Catalytic Capital for Goods on Country

*Catalysing Impact 2026 (QBE Foundation × Social Impact Hub) — rationale for the catalytic ask*
*Prepared 2026-06-02. All figures CANON unless flagged modelled/unverified. Cite Xero, not GHL. No surplus claim. Display-cleared community voices only (Mykel and Fred are consent-pending — excluded).*

---

## Thesis (the one paragraph)

Goods on Country sits in the exact gap catalytic capital was built to close: it is **too proven for a grant and not yet bankable for commercial money**. The proof is real and deployed — 496 beds and 28 washing machines (14 working) across 9 communities, 2,660kg of recycled HDPE diverted, a $750 product that communities are *pulling*, not being pushed. The non-obvious truth underneath it is an economic one: a recycled-plastic leg-set bought finished costs ~$344 against ~$40 of raw shredded plastic — an 8.6× markup — so **owning the press is the whole business**. It collapses marginal cost from ~$685 (Buy-Kit) to ~$426 (Factory) and break-even from ~1,679 beds/yr to ~338 against the same ~$109,500 fixed block. Commercial lenders will not yet underwrite a containerised on-Country plant for remote Indigenous communities; a grant alone leaves Goods grant-dependent and never crosses to viability. Catalytic capital — patient, risk-tolerant, concessionary, **deployed to crowd in the money that follows** — is the only instrument that bridges the distance between the buy-kit price and a *community-owned production floor*. That bridge is also the impact: the plant, the jobs and the ~$329/bed margin are designed to move into community ownership. The capital's return mechanism *is* "our goal is to become unnecessary." This is health hardware (a washable bed off the ground in the scabies → Strep A → acute rheumatic fever → rheumatic heart disease chain), employment, plastic diversion and self-determination, converging on one asset — which is why a community-led movement of this consequence deserves the one instrument that can carry it across the gap.

## Five load-bearing reasons

### 1. Goods is in the pioneer gap — the precise place catalytic capital exists to serve

Catalytic capital (MacArthur C3) "accepts disproportionate risk and/or concessionary returns relative to a conventional investment in order to generate positive impact **and enable third-party investment that otherwise would not be possible**." Its purpose is to fill the **pioneer gap / missing middle** (C3, Convergence): ventures **too proven for a grant, too unproven, sub-scale or thin-margin for commercial capital**. Its job is to de-risk and demonstrate so commercial capital can follow.

That description is Goods, almost line for line. The SIH/QBE Diagnostic V4's headline verdict is **"strong proof, weak paperwork"** — every priority gap (Business Model 4→7, Financial Management 4→7, Governance 5→8, Impact/MEL 5→8) sits in the structuring layer, *not* in the mission (Vision scored 8/10) or the product. SIH's own investment thesis: Goods "has the potential to attract early-stage impact capital" on "a clear and compelling mission, demonstrated community trust, proven product iteration, and demand from multiple customer segments," but needs structuring work over 6–12 months. On Tideline/MacArthur's three roles, Goods is a **Scaling** ask (a proven model, still sub-scale — fund the plant) with a **Sustaining** edge (serving the most remote communities at the margin, where full commercial viability is structurally out of reach). It is explicitly **not a seed** — the catalytic dollar de-risks the unit-economics shift, not the concept.

### 2. Neither grants alone nor commercial capital alone can do this job — only the blend

This is the heart of *why this instrument*. Run the two alternatives:

- **Grants alone** keep Goods grant-dependent. The Catalysing Impact program is explicit: *"Repayable finance will be prioritised over grant funding"* — it wants enterprises building toward investability, not a deeper subsidy habit. SIH flagged that orders today are "typically tied to specific funders" with "no standing inventory yet"; pure grant funding entrenches that fragility rather than curing it.
- **Commercial capital alone** will not flow. A market-rate lender cannot underwrite a first-of-kind containerised plant for remote communities at Goods' current stage, margin and documentation maturity. The CASE *Investor Alignment Tool* makes this concrete via its six **knockout** criteria — return expectations, capital type, stage of growth — any one of which a market-rate fund fails on for Goods today. Knockout-pass is necessary before any "fit" conversation even begins.

Blended finance (Catalysing Impact, after GIIN/Big Society Capital/World Bank) is "the right mix of capital from multiple stakeholders who have different risk-return requirements but common impact objectives," where concessional/philanthropic capital **de-risks** to pull in private/market-rate money. In the capital-stack model the program teaches, **grants and guarantees sit junior — they absorb first losses — protecting senior debt and equity above them.** That reordering is the only structure that makes Goods financeable *now*: a catalytic/first-loss tranche underneath, matched committed capital and revenue above. The program's flagship proof is **Jigsaw** ($3M = $2.1M debt + $0.9M grant via a SEFA syndicate) — "proof that the impact investment approach can work in a not-for-profit context." Catalytic is the deliberately concessionary *middle* of Omidyar's returns continuum — distinct from pure philanthropy (it expects to mobilise other money) and from market-rate impact (which makes no concession). Goods needs exactly that middle.

### 3. The additionality is the press — and it is arithmetic, not narrative

Catalytic funders test for **additionality**: proof the capital does something commercial money won't, rather than subsidising what markets would fund anyway. Goods' additionality has a number on it.

A recycled-plastic leg-set costs **$344 bought finished vs ~$40 of raw shredded plastic — an 8.6× markup** (~21.5× against raw polymer). Steel, canvas and hardware all sit under ~3×. **The plastic is the whole game.** Pressing legs in-house saves **~$194/bed** and walks the marginal cost down the cost-down curve:

| Stage | Marginal cost/bed | Contribution at $750 | Break-even beds/yr |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buy-Kit (buy finished legs, today) | ~$685 | ~$65 | ~1,679 |
| Factory (press our own legs) | ~$426 | ~$324 | ~338 |
| Community (free feedstock + fair-wage labour) | ~$421 | ~$329 | ~333 |

*(Cost model v6, engine-locked; fixed block ~$109,500/yr. The often-quoted "$1,780/bed loss" is a fixed-cost-absorption artefact at pilot volume — NOT a marginal cost — and is never headlined.)*

Same fixed costs; roughly **one-fifth the beds** to clear them once leg-pressing comes in-house. A commercial lender will not underwrite a first-of-kind, mobile, on-Country press line for remote communities — which is exactly why a catalytic dollar funding that plant is *additional* by definition. The path to break-even becomes **arithmetic, not story.** (Honesty note: factory capex is modelled $112K–222K gross with ~$110,046 already invested; on-Country site capex of $100K–150K/site is unverified and labelled as such.)

### 4. The cost-down → community-ownership pathway *is* the exit-of-concession funders reward

Catalytic funders look for a credible, time-bound **crowd-in / exit-of-concession path** — a line of sight to capital that no longer needs them. Goods has the strongest possible version of this, because **the exit and the impact are the same act.**

The containerised, mobile plant (~30 beds/week: collect → shred → press sheets → CNC-cut legs → assemble) is designed to travel to communities and **move into community ownership** — the plant, the jobs and the ~$329/bed margin staying on Country. Stated plainly: *"Our goal is to become unnecessary."* That is a literal, structural crowd-out — the concession is recovered or written down as economic and governance rights transfer to community stewards. Recoverable-grant and first-loss instruments are the natural fit precisely because they tolerate that write-down.

The structure is sequenced honestly (it is the destination, not today's fact): **today**, the Goods operating entity runs the economics; **transition**, a Supply Nation–certified joint venture (Goods + a community CATSI corporation, 50%+ Indigenous board, a documented capability-building plan), financed by SEFA blended capital and IBA equipment finance; **endpoint**, a community CATSI corp (51%+, Supply Nation certified) owning the plant — optionally with a Perpetual Purpose Trust locking the asset to community in perpetuity. Certification is not just governance hygiene: it unlocks the Indigenous Procurement Policy's remote Mandatory Set-Aside, turning a community-owned plant into a *preferred procurement supplier* for government remote-housing demand. The funder ladder writes itself: **QBE catalytic match → First Australians Capital patient debt + SEFA blended + IBA equipment finance → IPP procurement demand.** This is a time-bound capability transfer, not a token stake — and Australia's certification regime actively polices the difference ("black cladding"), so the real-control structure is a credibility asset, not a caveat.

### 5. The match gate is the crowd-in test in miniature — and closing it is the single most catalytic-aligned move

The Catalysing Impact program's defining rule is that Stage-2 funding (up to $400K from a $1M pool) **"must be at least matched to the external investment they have secured. The more external investment the better."** Catalytic capital is designed to *unlock* other money, not be the money. The match gate is the crowd-in mechanism rendered as program design — and it is **open**: 0 signed LOIs as of late May 2026.

This reframes the whole raise. The QBE match is the **last** dollar in, not the first; the work is **conversion** (warm conversations → signed paper), not discovery. What counts toward a match is binding: signed grant agreements, executed concessional-debt term sheets, board-approved philanthropic commitments, **binding offtake/forward-purchase LOIs**. What does not count: letters of support, "in conversation," EOIs, pipeline forecasts. The keystone double-play is **demand-as-capital**: a government/institutional procurement LOI (NT housing, NEMA, PICC) simultaneously satisfies the match (contracted forward revenue de-risks the catalytic dollar) *and* closes the exact "weak paperwork" / commercial-proof gap SIH named. And the leverage is the metric these funders read: across blended deals, **$1 of concessional capital has mobilised ~$4.10 of commercially priced capital** (~$1.80 of it private). A near-term ask of ~$400K signed match-eligible capital → QBE 1:1 → ~$800K catalytic tranche, tied to the Buy-Kit → Factory transition, is the most fundable use-of-funds Goods has, because it is a measurable economic step-change rather than "general scale-up."

## What it de-risks, and what it crowds in

**De-risks:** (a) first-loss/concessional philanthropy sits *below* commercial money in the stack; (b) demand-as-capital LOIs convert market risk into contracted forward revenue; (c) the press economics make break-even arithmetic (own the press → ~$194/bed saved → break-even 1,679 → 338) rather than narrative; (d) independent-director governance + accountant-endorsed, Goods-only financials close the SIH structuring gaps; (e) staged drawdown so capital lands only as milestones are hit.

**Crowds in:** matched committed capital that satisfies the QBE 1:1 gate (FAC patient debt for the press, SEFA blended bridge finance, IBA equipment finance once 50%+ Indigenous-owned), commercial revenue from $750 bed sales, and IPP procurement demand once the community entity is certified — and, ultimately, **community ownership of the asset itself**, the final crowd-out the concession is written down into.

## Why this is one of the most impactful Indigenous-led movements in Australia

Hold the citation discipline first: verified-paid grant funding is real (~$588K+, including PICC $436,700); the Goods revenue carve-out (~$713,827) is citable; **no surplus / no "230% self-reliance" claim** — the connected entity runs an FY26 net loss because it is investing ahead of revenue, which is the correct and fundable story for a scale-up. Xero is the source of truth.

With that floor set, the case is rare because **five forms of impact converge on a single asset**:

- **Health hardware.** A washable bed off the ground is one practical, community-identified link in the scabies → Strep A → ARF → rheumatic heart disease chain — the death-certificate problem the enterprise was founded to answer. Presented as infrastructure communities are *choosing*, never as Goods diagnosing helpless recipients.
- **Employment and a margin that stays home.** The press doesn't just lower cost — it puts ~$329/bed of contribution, plus fair-wage manufacturing jobs, inside the community that owns the plant.
- **Plastic diversion.** 2,660kg of recycled HDPE already diverted (Stretch-only; the over-claim is corrected) — and the feedstock becomes a revenue line, not a cost, where councils pay for community plastic collection.
- **Self-determination, structurally encoded.** Linda Turner (Tennant Creek): *"We've never been asked what sort of house we'd like to live in."* The answer is the opposite of charity — design happens around the fire with Elders; Oonchiumpa (100% Aboriginal-owned) leads design; Ebony and Jahvan Oui (Palm Island) are training as manufacturing leads; Elder Dianne Stokes named the washing machine in Warumungu and asked for 20 beds in two weeks. Norman Frank (Tennant Creek): *"Now we've got our own ways, we can collaborate with our own people… It'll be everywhere."*
- **Community-owned assets at national scale.** The mobile plant travelling community to community and into local ownership is the literal "one bed → a movement" device — agency rendered as business model.

The community is the hero; Goods is the guide; the catalytic funder is the catalyst who joins the movement and is then, by design, no longer needed. That is what makes catalytic capital not merely *a* fit for Goods on Country, but the *right* and *only* instrument for this moment — and it is what makes the moment worth backing.
