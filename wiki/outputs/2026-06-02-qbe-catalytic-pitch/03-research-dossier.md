## 1. Catalytic & blended finance — theory and best practice

*Agent source:* Catalytic capital & blended/impact finance — theory and best practice (MacArthur C3, Convergence, Omidyar, Tideline, GIIN, Rockefeller), digested for a Goods on Country pitch author


**Key points**

- DEFINITION (verified, MacArthur C3): catalytic capital is impact investment that is more PATIENT, RISK-TOLERANT, CONCESSIONARY and FLEXIBLE than conventional finance — it 'accepts disproportionate risk and/or concessionary returns relative to a conventional investment in order to generate positive impact AND enable third-party investment that otherwise would not be possible.' The crowd-in is part of the definition, not a side effect.
- WHERE IT SITS (Omidyar 'Across the Returns Continuum', verified): a spectrum from philanthropic grants (no financial return) -> subcommercial / catalytic (below-market, accepts trade-off intentionally) -> commercial / market-rate impact (no concession). Catalytic is the deliberately concessionary middle, justified only when chasing market-level impact a commercial dollar won't reach. This is the 'capital stack' logic a pitch should make explicit.
- THE WHY (verified, C3 + Convergence): catalytic capital exists to close the PIONEER GAP / 'missing middle' — ventures too proven for a grant but too unproven, sub-scale or thin-margin for commercial capital. Its job is to DE-RISK and demonstrate, so commercial capital can follow. This is the exact gap Goods sits in: real deployment proof, not yet commercially bankable.
- THE THREE ROLES (verified, Tideline/MacArthur): SEEDING (back pioneers with no track record / no comparables), SCALING (proven model, still sub-scale, help it grow or be replicated), SUSTAINING (a permanent need for concessional/risk-tolerant capital to keep serving hard-to-reach beneficiaries where full commercial viability is impossible). Goods' factory build-out = SCALING; serving the most remote communities at the margin = SUSTAINING.
- INSTRUMENTS — the toolkit a pitch can name: RECOVERABLE / RETURNABLE GRANTS (repaid only if the venture succeeds), FIRST-LOSS / subordinated (junior) capital (absorbs initial losses to protect senior investors), GUARANTEES / risk insurance (backstop against capital loss), CONCESSIONARY DEBT (below-market rate, longer tenor, grace periods), QUASI-EQUITY / revenue-based finance (returns flexed to cashflow — well suited to a hardware enterprise without an equity exit), and DESIGN/TECHNICAL-ASSISTANCE grants (fund the work that makes a deal investable).
- BLENDED FINANCE ARCHETYPES (verified, Convergence): four ways to structure a deal — (1) concessional debt/equity in a junior/subordinate position with first-loss; (2) guarantee or risk-insurance; (3) design/preparation funding; (4) technical-assistance facilities. Concessional debt/equity is the most-used archetype.
- LEVERAGE IS THE HEADLINE METRIC (verified, Convergence): on average every $1 of concessional capital has mobilised ~$4.10 of commercially priced capital (~$1.80 of it private). A catalytic funder wants to see its dollar 'crowd in' a multiple — so a pitch should quantify the multiplier its ask unlocks.
- WHAT CATALYTIC FUNDERS LOOK FOR: (a) ADDITIONALITY — proof the capital does something commercial money won't, and isn't just subsidising what markets would fund anyway; (b) a credible CROWD-IN / exit-of-concession path — the concession is time-bound, with a line of sight to commercial or self-sustaining capital; (c) RIGHT-SIZED concessionality — 'minimum concessional capital to open the door' (Convergence); over-subsidy is a flag; (d) clear, measured IMPACT (GIIN/IRIS+ style metrics, impact integrity, no overclaiming); (e) a path to NOT needing them forever.
- GOODS FRAMING — the role: this is a SCALING + SUSTAINING ask, not a seed. The proof exists: 496 beds (363 Basket + 133 Stretch) + 28 washing machines (14 working) across 9 communities served, 2,660kg recycled HDPE diverted (Stretch-only). The catalytic dollar de-risks the unit-economics shift, not the concept.
- GOODS FRAMING — additionality & the asset that closes the pioneer gap: the press is the catalytic thesis. Bought-finished recycled-plastic legs cost $344/bed vs ~$40 of raw shredded plastic (8.6x markup); owning the plastic press saves ~$194/bed and moves marginal cost from ~$685 (Buy-Kit) to ~$426 (Factory) to ~$421 (Community), and break-even from ~1,679 beds/yr to ~338 to ~333 (fixed block ~$109,500/yr). Catalytic capital funds the containerised on-Country plant (~30 beds/wk) that commercial lenders won't underwrite yet — classic additionality.
- GOODS FRAMING — the exit-of-concession story funders reward: 'our goal is to become unnecessary' — the containerised plant, the jobs and the margin are designed to MOVE INTO COMMUNITY OWNERSHIP. That is a literal, structural crowd-out of the concession into community-held commercial value. Pair it with the health-hardware logic (washable bed off the ground -> scabies/Strep A/ARF/RHD pathway) for the impact case.
- GOODS FRAMING — the capital stack & the QBE match gate: structure the ask as a STACK — catalytic/concessionary tranche (QBE-type, first-loss or recoverable, de-risks the plant) + a matched committed tranche (the open match gate; 0 signed LOIs as of late May 2026) + commercial/revenue from $750 bed sales. The QBE 'match' requirement IS the crowd-in test in miniature — closing matched committed capital is the single most catalytic-aligned move. CITE CAREFULLY: ~$588K+ verified-paid grants incl. PICC $436,700 (Xero is source of truth); Goods revenue carve-out ~$713,827 is citable; do NOT cite a surplus or '230% self-reliance' (entity runs an FY26 net loss); GHL pipeline $ is unreliable. ## Catalytic capital & blended finance — a pitch author's frame

### 1. The one-sentence definition (verified, MacArthur C3)
Catalytic capital is impact investment that is **more patient, risk-tolerant, concessionary and flexible** than conventional finance, deployed to achieve impact that *would not otherwise be possible* AND to **enable third-party investment that otherwise would not happen**. The crowd-in is built into the definition.

### 2. Where it sits — the returns continuum (verified, Omidyar Network)
A spectrum: **philanthropy** (grants, no return) → **catalytic / subcommercial** (intentionally below-market, accepts the trade-off) → **commercial / market-rate impact** (no concession). Catalytic is distinct from *pure philanthropy* (it expects recovery/return and aims to mobilise other money) and from *market-rate impact* (which makes no concession). A pitch should locate each tranche of its ask on this continuum.

### 3. Why it exists — the pioneer gap / missing middle (verified, C3 + Convergence)
Catalytic capital fills the gap for ventures **too proven for a grant, too unproven/sub-scale/thin-margin for commercial capital.** Its function is to **de-risk and demonstrate** so commercial capital can follow. Convergence: use the **minimum concessional capital to open the door** to private investment.

### 4. The three roles (verified, Tideline/MacArthur)
- **Seeding** — back pioneers with no track record or comparables.
- **Scaling** — proven model, still sub-scale; help it grow or be replicated.
- **Sustaining** — a *permanent* need for concessional/risk-tolerant capital to keep serving hard-to-reach beneficiaries where full commercial viability isn't achievable.
> Goods reads as **Scaling** (the plant) + a **Sustaining** edge (most-remote communities at the margin) — explicitly *not* a seed.

### 5. Instruments (the toolkit to name in the stack)
Recoverable/returnable grants · first-loss / subordinated (junior) capital · guarantees & risk-insurance · concessionary debt (below-market, long tenor, grace) · quasi-equity / **revenue-based finance** (fits a hardware enterprise with no equity exit) · design & technical-assistance grants. **Convergence's four archetypes** (verified): (1) concessional junior debt/equity w/ first-loss; (2) guarantee/insurance; (3) design/prep funding; (4) TA facilities — concessional debt/equity most common.

### 6. The metric that lands — leverage (verified, Convergence)
On average **$1 concessional → ~$4.10 commercial mobilised (~$1.80 private)**. Quantify the multiple your ask unlocks.

### 7. What catalytic funders look for
**Additionality** (does what commercial money won't) · a **time-bound exit-of-concession / crowd-in path** · **right-sized concessionality** (over-subsidy is a flag) · **measured impact** (GIIN/IRIS+ rigour, no overclaiming) · a credible path to *not needing them forever*.

### 8. Goods on Country — how to frame the ask
- **Role:** Scaling+Sustaining, evidenced (496 beds [363 Basket + 133 Stretch] + 28 washers [14 working], 9 communities, 2,660kg HDPE diverted — Stretch-only).
- **Additionality = the press.** Finished legs $344 vs ~$40 raw shredded plastic (8.6x); the press saves ~$194/bed and shifts marginal cost $685→$426→$421 and break-even ~1,679→338→333 beds/yr (fixed ~$109,500/yr). Commercial lenders won't underwrite the containerised on-Country plant (~30 beds/wk) yet — that's the catalytic dollar's job.
- **Exit of concession = community ownership.** The plant/jobs/margin move into community hands ("our goal is to become unnecessary") — a structural crowd-out. Pair with health-hardware logic (washable bed → scabies/Strep A/ARF/RHD).
- **Capital stack:** catalytic/first-loss tranche (QBE-type, de-risks plant) + **matched committed tranche** (open match gate; 0 signed LOIs late-May 2026 — closing it IS the crowd-in test) + commercial revenue from $750 bed sales.
- **Citation discipline:** ~$588K+ verified-paid grants incl. **PICC $436,700** (Xero = truth); revenue carve-out ~$713,827 citable; **NO surplus / no '230% self-reliance'** (FY26 net loss); GHL $ unreliable; **Mykel & Fred quotes consent-pending — exclude.**

## 2. Structuring the ask — the capital stack & match strategy

What does NOT count: letters of support, 'in conversation,' pipeline forecasts, or unsigned EOIs. - MISSION-LOCK / OWNERSHIP-TRANSFER IS THE PRODUCT, NOT A CAVEAT. 'Our goal is to become unnecessary' — the plant, the jobs and the margin move into community ownership (PICC pathway). Present this as the exit/return mechanism a catalytic funder is buying: their capital is recovered or written down as ownership transfers, and the recoverable-grant structure (Minderoo/PFI repayable) is the natural instrument because it tolerates that transfer.
- TERMS + MILESTONES grid: present each instrument as amount / instrument type / pricing / covenant / mission-lock / trigger-milestone. Critical-path sequence: confirm opening cash -> collect authorised receivables (Snow, Centrecorp) -> sign SEFA term sheet -> reach ~$400K committed match -> QBE match lands -> deploy Plant 2 -> ownership-transfer design. Each layer's drawdown is gated on the milestone below it.
- DE-RISK FOR THE CATALYTIC FUNDER: (a) first-loss philanthropy sits below their money; (b) demand LOIs convert market risk into contracted forward revenue; (c) the unit-economics step-change (own the press -> $194/bed saved, 8.6x markup captured) makes the path to break-even arithmetic, not narrative; (d) independent-director governance + accountant-endorsed financials close the SIH paperwork gaps; (e) staged drawdown so capital lands only as milestones are hit.
- FINANCIAL HONESTY GUARDRAILS for the deck: cite verified-paid grants (~$588K incl. PICC $436,700) and the Goods revenue carve-out (~$713,827) as VERIFIED; do NOT cite a surplus or '230% self-reliance' — the connected entity runs an FY26 net loss (investing ahead of revenue), which is the correct and fundable story for a scale-up. Label everything else modelled/unverified. Xero is source of truth, not GHL.
- VOICE / CONSENT GATE: use display-cleared community voices (Linda Turner TC, Alfred Johnson Palm Island, Norman Frank TC). Do NOT use Mykel's or Fred's quotes externally until consent is captured. Indigenous agency and the ownership-transfer endgame must lead — this is the health-hardware + self-determination story (washable bed off the ground -> scabies/Strep A/RHD chain), not a charity story.

This funds the Factory transition that drops break-even from 1,679 to 338 beds/yr and moves the press toward community ownership.' Lead with the trigger math; the stack table is the appendix.

## 3. Pitch-deck craft — structure & narrative arc

*Agent source:* Web research synthesis (Jun 2026): Sequoia/Kawasaki frameworks (inknarrates, storydoc, winningpresentations), social-enterprise + catalytic-capital deck structure (bertzman, convergence.finance, sustainableatlas blended-finance explainer), heart-vs-head storytelling craft (viktori, qubit.capital, financialmodelslab), failure modes (waveup, qubit, decktopus, fundtq), and exemplar breakdowns (Airbnb/Uber/Dropbox via inknarrates, livingslide, spectup). All claims web-sourced; Goods-specific applications flagged inline.


**Key points**

- The proven arc is a chain of resolved questions, not sections: Why this matters -> Why now -> Why this approach -> Proof it works -> Why this team -> Impact thesis -> The ask -> The future. Investors move through it in the order they price risk (believe the problem -> understand the solution -> see the market -> see proof -> trust the team -> see the ask).
- Length: 12-16 core slides for a funded modern deck (Kawasaki's 10 is the floor); push everything else to a due-diligence appendix. Decks over ~20 slides lose attention. Each slide = one idea, headline states the takeaway (not a label).
- Open with TENSION, not your product. Lead with one concrete human moment (a named person, a single scene), then widen to the systemic problem. Facts inform, emotion is what gets remembered. For Goods: open on a community voice (Linda Turner: 'We've never been asked what sort of house we'd like to live in') NOT a product photo.
- Insight slide is the hinge: state the non-obvious truth only you see. For Goods this is the unit-economics insight - recycled-plastic legs cost $344 bought-finished vs ~$40 of raw shredded plastic (8.6x markup); owning the press saves ~$194/bed. That one number reframes the whole business.
- Proof beats promises (the Airbnb lesson): show real traction with evidence, not projected demand. For Goods, the traction slide is deployment, not forecasts: 496 beds + 28 washers (14 working) across 9 communities; ~$588K-plus verified-paid grant funding (cite Xero, NOT GHL); 2,660kg plastic diverted (Stretch-only). Every claim carries a source.
- Model + unit-economics slide must be brutally simple (one sentence + one chart). Show the cost-down curve as the story: Buy-Kit ~$685 -> Factory ~$426 -> Community ~$421 marginal cost/bed; contribution $65 -> $324 -> $329; break-even 1,679 -> 338 -> 333 beds/yr against a ~$109,500/yr fixed block. The arc IS 'owning the press moves us from underwater to viable.'
- Catalytic/blended-capital ask is structurally different from a VC ask: name the market failure first (why commercial capital won't flow alone), then show the capital stack by tier and the mobilization multiplier. Catalytic capital is patient/risk-tolerant/concessional/flexible and exists to crowd IN commercial money (median blended-finance mobilization ~4:1).
- Make the QBE 'match' gate the spine of the ask slide: catalytic dollars must be matched by committed/signed capital. Show the stack visually - catalytic/first-loss layer (QBE), matched commercial/philanthropic layer, and the use-of-funds tied to the press (the asset that shifts unit economics). Current reality: 0 signed LOIs - so the ask should be framed as 'unlock the match,' and the deck's job is to convert.
- Impact thesis = theory of change shown as a results chain, not adjectives: input (recycled plastic + fair-wage labour) -> output (beds off the ground) -> outcome (health hardware: scabies -> Strep A -> ARF/RHD pathway) -> systemic shift (plant + jobs + margin move into community ownership; 'our goal is to become unnecessary'). Label every metric verified/modelled/target.
- Balance heart and head by alternating, never blending into mush: human story slides carry emotion; metric slides carry stabilization ('is this real, working, moving the right way?'). Numbers support the narrative, never lead it. Research cited: a tight narrative + one clean data point lifts persuasiveness ~43%.
- Keep solution PROPORTIONAL to the tension - if a focused problem meets a 'changes everything' solution, credibility collapses. Goods' containerised on-Country plant (~30 beds/week, travels, moves to community ownership) is the right-sized vision: ambitious but mechanically concrete.
- Top failure modes to avoid: vague/missing ask (the single most common own-goal), weak or projection-only traction, information overload (charts with no story), too many slides, over-claiming. Goods-specific traps: do NOT cite a surplus or '230% self-reliance' (entity runs an FY26 net loss); revenue carve-out ~$713,827 is citable; do NOT use Mykel's/Fred's consent-pending quotes; cite Xero not GHL for money.
- The close should clarify the future, not hype it: restate the ONE thing, the specific ask with use-of-funds and milestones, and a single forward image. Mirror the opening human story to close the emotional loop (return to the community, now changed).

## Recommended slide architecture (catalytic-capital impact deck, 14 core slides + appendix)

13. **Milestones / roadmap.** Specific, dated, tied to the funds.
14. **The close.** Restate the ONE thing + specific ask; return to the opening person, now changed.

## Craft principles (do / don't)
- **Do** alternate heart and head — never blend into mush. Story slides carry emotion; metric slides answer "real, working, moving right?" One clean data point per story beat (~43% persuasion lift).
- **Do** make every claim carry a source; tag verified/modelled/target.
- **Do** keep the solution proportional to the tension; "changes everything" kills credibility.
- **Do** one idea per slide; headline = the takeaway.
- **Don't** ship a vague/missing ask (the #1 own-goal), projection-only traction, chart-dumps, or >~20 slides.
- **Goods guardrails:** cite Xero not GHL; revenue carve-out ~$713,827 is citable; do NOT claim a surplus or "230% self-reliance" (FY26 net loss); exclude consent-pending quotes.

## Exemplars & why they landed

- **Dropbox:** sold the vision/feeling before the feature set.
Common thread: structure, clarity, and an unshakeable "why" in 10–15 slides.

## 4. Human impact narrative — real people, told ethically

*Agent source:* Web research synthesis: ethical, evidence-paired, community-centred impact storytelling for the Goods on Country pitch deck (nonprofit/philanthropy storytelling literature + Indigenous data-sovereignty CARE principles + identifiable-victim-effect research). **Key points**

- Make community the hero, the org the guide, the funder the catalyst. Never let Goods (or QBE) be the protagonist who 'saves' anyone. Frame: the people closest to the problem are leading the change; capital/tools are the vehicle. This is the 'donor is the hero, org is the guide' rule and the heart of avoiding saviour framing.
- Lead with one real, named person and their aspiration, NOT their deficit. Asset-based/strengths-based framing means open on agency and contribution ('we've never been asked what sort of house we'd like to live in' is a self-determination claim, not a pity hook), then let data follow as proof. Never define a person by their problem first.
- Beware the 'drop in the bucket' effect (Save the Children study): a single story raised 2x more than facts alone, but story+data raised LESS than story alone because big numbers make people feel their contribution is futile. Devices the deck must use: (a) keep the story and the big number on SEPARATE beats, (b) re-anchor every stat to one concrete unit of personal impact ('your match = N beds off the floor in community X'), (c) frame scale as momentum/movement the funder JOINS, not a chasm they can't fill.
- Sequence: human FIRST, then zoom out to scale. One person -> reveal they're one of many -> the system. Mirror the narrative arc with a numeric arc (before/after, year-over-year growth) so the data feels like the same story at a larger scale, not a cold pivot.
- 'Nothing about us without us' = narrative sovereignty. The storyteller controls their own story start to finish: what's told, how, where it's used, and the final say. Practically for Goods: community voices are co-authors, not subjects; quotes are used verbatim and attributed; the plant 'moving into community ownership' and 'our goal is to become unnecessary' ARE the agency frame in structural form.
- Hard consent gate is non-negotiable: ONLY use display-cleared voices (Linda Turner, Alfred Johnson, Norman Frank). Mykel and Fred are CONSENT-PENDING and must NOT appear in any external/QBE deck until consent is captured. Treat this as a release-blocker, not a nicety.
- Apply CARE principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) + FPIC (Free, Prior, Informed Consent) on top of FAIR. Community has the right to control how it is represented in data and images; data and imagery must never portray communities in deficit terms; benefit must flow back to community.
- Convey scale as a MOVEMENT, not a monolith: ensemble/multi-voice narrative (several named voices across communities) builds attachment to many people without anchoring everything to one 'poster child'. Collective narrative sustains momentum where a single story only ignites it. The containerised plant travelling community-to-community and into local ownership is the literal 'one bed -> a movement' device.
- Pair every claim with provenance and a single source of truth. Cite only verified numbers: 496 beds (363 Basket + 133 Stretch), 28 washers (14 working), 9 communities served, 2,660kg plastic diverted (STRETCH-ONLY), ~$588K+ verified-paid grants incl. PICC $436,700. Use Goods revenue carve-out (~$713,827) but do NOT claim a surplus or '230% self-reliance' — the entity runs an FY26 net loss. Wrong/inflated numbers destroy the dignity case and the funder case simultaneously.
- Give the funder a single, concrete, proximate call to action tied to a meaningful result. QBE's match gate is the CTA: 'sign/commit $X of match-eligible capital so the on-Country plant gets built and the margin stays in community.' One ask, one outcome, named — not a vague 'support us.'
- Turn unit economics into a dignity-and-ownership story, not a spreadsheet. The 8.6x markup ($344 bought legs vs ~$40 raw plastic) and ~$194/bed saved by pressing in-house IS the argument that owning the press = community owning the margin. Lead the capital case with WHO captures the value, then show the cost-down curve ($685 Buy-Kit -> $426 Factory -> $421 Community) as proof.
- Frame the bed inside the systems story without medicalising people: a washable bed off the ground is ONE practical piece of the health-hardware chain (scabies -> Strep A -> ARF/RHD). Present it as community-identified infrastructure that communities are choosing, not as Goods diagnosing a problem and fixing helpless recipients.

## Narrative principles (the spine)

1. **Recast the hero.** Across the nonprofit literature the single biggest anti-saviour move is who you make the protagonist: the community is the hero, Goods is the guide, the funder (QBE/match partners) is the catalyst who joins the story. Saviourism = the org takes credit for the person's success and implicitly makes the person "the problem to be fixed." Avoid it structurally, not just in tone. ([Meyer Partners](https://www.meyerpartners.com/fundraising-blog/nonprofit-storytelling), [Virtuous](https://virtuous.org/blog/ethical-nonprofit-storytelling/), [GoFundMe Pro](https://pro.gofundme.com/c/blog/nonprofit-storytelling/))

2. **Asset-based open.** Start with aspiration, strengths, agency — never the deficit. "No one wants to be defined by their problems first." Linda Turner's line ("we've never been asked what sort of house we'd like to live in") is a self-determination statement; use it as a thesis, not a tragedy. Strengths-based framing is also the Indigenous-specific best practice (move beyond trauma; never deficit-frame). ([Blue Avocado](https://blueavocado.org/community-and-culture/ethical-storytelling-for-nonprofits/), [Strengths-based Indigenous resource, NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8022673/), [RedPath Talent](https://www.redpathtalent.com/blog/beyondthemonolith))

3. **Narrative sovereignty = "nothing about us without us."** Authentic representation requires the people closest to the issue to lead/control the telling — a "declaration of narrative sovereignty." Skoll's philanthropy practice: treat storytelling as a *collaborative editorial project where the community controls how the work is represented*; funders trust those closest to the challenge to lead. ([UCalgary](https://ucalgary.ca/news/nothing-about-us-without-us-1), [Skoll](https://skoll.org/2021/09/24/towards-more-proximate-storytelling-in-philanthropy/))

## Pairing one story with rigorous evidence + scale

4. **Human first, data second — on separate beats.** Emotion drives the decision; data justifies it. Open on one named person, THEN zoom out to the number. ([Clairification](https://clairification.com/2022/08/25/), [Elevation](https://www.elevationweb.org/blog/data-impact-stories/))

5. **The "drop in the bucket" trap (load-bearing).** Save the Children study: story alone $2.38 vs facts alone $1.14 — but story+data only $1.43, because big numbers make people feel futile. Counter-devices: (a) keep the emotional story and the macro stat on different slides; (b) re-anchor scale to ONE personal unit ("your match puts N beds off the floor in [community]"); (c) use 1-in-6 style framing, before/after, year-over-year momentum, and maps; (d) frame the number as a movement to join, not a gap to fill. ([GoFundMe Pro – Identifiable Victim](https://pro.gofundme.com/c/blog/identifiable-victim-effect/), [Sway](https://www.swaystorytelling.com/blog/2020/8/12/the-identifiable-victim-effect))

6. **Ensemble, not poster child.** Multiple named voices across communities (Linda/Alfred/Norman) build a collective narrative that sustains momentum where one story only ignites it — and conveys "one bed -> a movement" without flattening anyone into a symbol. ([Creative Praxis](https://www.creativepraxis.org/post/story-as-strategy-how-collective-narratives-drive-community-organizing-and-liberation))

## Ethics (release-blockers)

7. **Consent gate.** Use only display-cleared voices. Mykel + Fred are consent-pending — exclude from any external/QBE deck until consent is captured. Storyteller gets final say and scoped permission for each use. ([Blue Avocado](https://blueavocado.org/community-and-culture/ethical-storytelling-for-nonprofits/))

8. **CARE + FPIC for Indigenous data/images.** Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics — community controls how it's represented; benefit flows back; never deficit-frame. Layer CARE on top of FAIR. ([CODATA](https://datascience.codata.org/articles/dsj-2020-043), [ARDC](https://ardc.edu.au/resource/the-care-principles/))

## Moving funders to act
9. **One proximate CTA tied to a result.** Name the single action (commit match-eligible capital) and the outcome (plant built, margin stays in community). Funders respond to proximity and solutions-framing, not crisis. Provenance every dollar; cite the carve-out (~$713,827) but never a surplus. ([fundsforNGOs](https://www2.fundsforngos.org/articles/storytelling-for-impact-how-to-move-grant-funders-to-action/), [Skoll](https://skoll.org/2021/09/24/towards-more-proximate-storytelling-in-philanthropy/))

## 5. Indigenous-led & community-owned funding + governance

*Agent source:* Web research (June 2026): IBA, First Australians Capital, Supply Nation/IPP, NIAA, ORIC/CATSI Act, native-title benefit structures, Sefa/blended finance, steward-ownership & exit-to-community, Indigenous data sovereignty (CARE/OCAP/Maiam nayri Wingara). Citations inline in detail.


**Key points**

- The structural question for Goods is sequence, not just form: it is currently a non-Indigenous-founded social enterprise. Genuine Indigenous ownership of the on-Country plant is the destination, and the credible path is a TIME-BOUND, capability-building transfer — exactly the 'become unnecessary' thesis — not a token equity stake (which Supply Nation explicitly polices as 'black cladding').
- Two ownership thresholds drive everything downstream: 50%+ Indigenous = Supply Nation REGISTERED; 51%+ owned, managed AND controlled = CERTIFIED, which is the gate for the Indigenous Procurement Policy (IPP). Certified status unlocks Commonwealth Mandatory Set-Aside (MSA): ALL contracts delivered in REMOTE Australia, regardless of value, plus $80k-$200k contracts anywhere, must first be offered to Indigenous businesses. Goods' remote-community footprint makes this lane uniquely valuable.
- IPP buy-target rose to 3% of Commonwealth spend from 1 Jul 2025, climbing 0.25%/yr to 4% by 2030; Mandatory Minimum Requirements (MMR) hit contracts >=$7.5M in 19 industries. This is demand-side capital: a community-owned plant that is Supply-Nation-certified can sell beds INTO government remote-housing procurement as the preferred supplier — turning the 'demand-as-capital' LOIs (NT housing, NEMA) into a procurement moat, not just match evidence.
- Governance vehicle for the COMMUNITY-OWNED endpoint is most likely a CATSI Act corporation (regulated by ORIC), which requires 51%+ Indigenous members and a majority-Indigenous board, but allows a customised Rule Book that can encode traditional law/custom and a minority of non-Indigenous directors during transition. CATSI corps get lighter-touch, free regulation and built-in cultural governance — fit-for-purpose for a community manufacturing co.
- For the TRANSITION (Goods still operating today), the cleanest instrument is a Supply Nation CERTIFIED JOINT VENTURE between Goods (or its operating entity) and a community/Indigenous corporation, with a mandatory, documented capability-building strategy and 50%+ Indigenous board control. JV certification explicitly forbids shareholder agreements that let the non-Indigenous partner control the board via supermajority — so the legal docs must give real, not nominal, control.
- Steward-ownership / Perpetual Purpose Trust (PPT) and 'exit to community' are the global frame that names what Goods is already trying to do: lock mission into the legal DNA and transfer economic + governance rights to community stewards (Patagonia 2022, Organically Grown Co are reference points). A PPT or community trust can hold the plant's shares so the asset can never be sold away from community — a powerful pitch-deck framing alongside the CATSI vehicle.
- FUNDER FIT (the warm exemplar): First Australians Capital is Indigenous-LED and is QBE's own portfolio exemplar (QBE put $500k into FAC's seed fund; FAC banked a $20M Catalytic Impact Fund). FAC offers patient debt $100k-$2M (CIF, for capital purchases like the press/plant) and blended grant+low-interest seed capital up to $100k. BUT eligibility is First-Nations-LED businesses — so FAC fits the community-owned plant entity, and a warm intro via Jay is the move once the JV/CATSI structure exists.
- Indigenous Business Australia (IBA) finances businesses that are 50%+ Indigenous-owned: Start-Up package (up to 30% of a loan as a grant, loans to $150k), invoice finance (to 80% of invoices), and chattel mortgage / finance leases for plant & equipment (directly relevant to a containerised press). As of Feb 2025 IBA has new powers to raise private/market capital, and a CommBank co-development partnership — a growing balance-sheet lender for the community entity once it crosses 50% Indigenous ownership.
- BLENDED-FINANCE bridge for the transition (when Goods is not yet 51% Indigenous): Sefa (Social Enterprise Finance Australia) structures loans+grants+guarantees for impact enterprises incl. Indigenous orgs, has deployed $160M+, and offers investment-readiness advisory. Sefa is the natural blended-finance partner to fund the plant build BEFORE the community entity can borrow on its own balance sheet, and to de-risk the QBE match.
- INDIGENOUS DATA SOVEREIGNTY is non-negotiable and a differentiator: apply CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) + OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) + Maiam nayri Wingara principles to ALL community data Goods holds (asset register locations, community voices, EL stories, impact metrics). Concretely: community controls its own bed-register and story data; the existing consent gate (Mykel/Fred consent-pending) is the right instinct — codify it as policy, not ad hoc.
- Native-title / community-benefit structures (PBCs, charitable trusts) are the RIGHT REFERENCE but likely the WRONG primary vehicle for Goods: they're built to hold land payments, can cost up to ~$1M/yr to run with independent trustees, and attach to native-title holder groups, not a manufacturing co. Borrow the benefit-distribution and charitable-trust mechanics where useful (e.g. a community-benefit trust over plant margin), but anchor operations in a CATSI corp + certified JV.
- RECOMMENDED STRUCTURE FOR THE DECK: (1) Today - Goods operating entity runs Buy-Kit/Factory economics; (2) Transition - certified JV (Goods + community CATSI corp), 50%+ Indigenous board, documented capability plan, financed by Sefa blended + IBA plant finance; (3) Endpoint - community CATSI corp (51%+, Supply-Nation certified) owns the containerised plant + jobs + ~$329/bed contribution margin, optionally with a PPT/community-benefit trust locking the asset to community in perpetuity. Funder ladder: QBE catalytic match -> matched by FAC patient debt + Sefa blended + IBA equipment finance + IPP procurement demand.

## How Goods should frame Indigenous ownership + governance of the production assets

**The core framing.** Goods is a non-Indigenous-founded social enterprise whose stated goal is to "become unnecessary" — the plant, jobs and margin moving into community ownership. 1. **CATSI Act corporation (ORIC-regulated)** — the endpoint vehicle.
