## 1. The model — draft definition for sign-off

**The line (Ben's, from the 9 July workshop):**
> Goods turns community knowledge into health hardware, local work, and production that communities can own.

**The definition, plainly:**

Communities design the products with Goods — beds and washing machines that survive remote
conditions because the people who live there specified them. The making happens in community
hands, and *whose* hands depends on the place: Elders designing and naming around the fire
(Dianne Stokes — both products), families building (the Bloomfield family built the five
current Pakkimjalki Kari machines with Nic, Jan 2026), young people learning through a trusted
local partner (Mykel and Xavier with Oonchiumpa — one example of how it can work, not the whole
model), and local organisations deploying, reordering, and asking to own (Anyinginyi, PICC,
Oonchiumpa). Every product into a home feeds back into the next design. And the plant itself —
workflow, training, quality practice, supply links, repair knowledge, sales learning — is
built to be handed over, place by place, with Goods staying on as ongoing enterprise support
rather than owner. The purpose the whole loop serves: the best products, made and designed by
community, supporting the home conditions that reduce the scabies→RHD pathway and ongoing
health issues — said always as the why, never as a claimed clinical outcome until evaluated.

**The five stages of the loop (the thing to diagram):**
1. **Listen / be asked properly** — community names the need (Dianne: design; Linda: "never been asked")
2. **Designed in community + build the best product** — specs from lived conditions (washable, low for Elders, no tools). Language rule: it is *designed in community / designed with community* — never "co-design"
3. **Make on Country** — the containerised plant + young makers via the trusted partner (Oonchiumpa pattern)
4. **Deliver + feedback** — beds into homes, product feedback loops straight back into design
5. **Transfer + support** — plant and enterprise move toward community hands; Goods stays as support layer

**Ownership language (locked by Ben 2026-07-10):** a pathway moving closer to community
ownership — never claimed as complete. No Goods entity meets an Indigenous-ownership tier today.

**The loop has already run twice — Pakkimjalki Kari is the second proof, not a side product:**

- **Designed and named by an Elder.** Dianne Stokes designed it, refines designs "around the
  fire" with her family, and named it Pakkimjalki Kari in Warumungu. She was the first to have
  a Goods bed and a Pakkimjalki Kari in her home. Her line on it: *"It means something that
  really makes me happy. Every time I go away, it's like it's calling me. Come back home."*
  (partner-dashboards.ts — strongest product quote in the corpus.)
- **Built by a family.** Nic built the five current machines with the Bloomfield family,
  Tennant Creek/Alice, late Jan 2026 — adults and family, not a youth program. The design:
  commercial-grade Speed Queen base, one-button operation, remote-serviceable. Prototype
  status, register-interest only; the goal is price-down without losing reliability.
- **Bought and reordered by a health service.** Anyinginyi Health deployed 5 and quoted for 4
  more (Feb 2026) — institutional demand for the second product. 16 in community (curated,
  Ben-confirmed). Standing demand beyond: Groote Archipelago ~300 washers; Miwatj exploring an
  8-clinic fleet.
- **It answers the model's founding failure story.** One Alice Springs supplier sells ~$3M/yr
  of washing machines into remote communities; most reach the dump within months. Pakkimjalki
  Kari is designed in community and repairable — same loop as the bed, different product.
- **It completes the health-purpose logic.** Bed off the ground + washable bedding + a working
  washing machine at home = the home-conditions system. Carriers: Patricia Frank ("They truly
  wanna a washing machine to wash their blanket… it's right there at home" — she also built the
  language-group relationships that made the machine possible), Dr Boe Remenyi (practitioner:
  "It's great to say you should wash your sheets every week. But if you don't have a washing
  machine, that's not going to work."), Jessica Allardyce (strongest RHD/washing line — consent
  not yet cleared, decision §6.5).

Two products through one loop, different hands each time — that is the transferability
argument. The young-maker build day is one instance; the fire, the family build, and the
health-service reorder are others.

## 2. The narrative arc — six belief turns, each carried by a voice

Principles applied (Ben's, 9 July): community voice before Goods claims · one belief turn at a
time · proof beats polish · pathway language · quotes carry decisions, not decoration.

**Turn 1 — The need is real, and people name it themselves.**
Anonymous Arlparra household: *"We've been sleeping on a door."* · Arlparra Elder: *"Off the
ground. That's the main thing."* · Ivy (Palm Island): *"Hardly any people around the community
have got beds. When they got family members over, there's not enough for everyone."* ·
Katrina Bloomfield: *"Most of our people in community are just on a blanket on the ground…
mainly for the old elders."*

**Turn 2 — The current supply fails these places.**
Alfred Johnson (Palm Island): *"You have to bring them on the barge. You can't just take them
on the boat. You have to pay for freight. It all adds up."* · Daniel Patrick Noble: *"Sometimes
people would rather go without."*

**Turn 3 — The products work because community designed them. Both of them.**
Dianne Stokes designed the bed and the washing machine, refining "around the fire" with
family; she named Pakkimjalki Kari in Warumungu, and says of it: *"Every time I go away, it's
like it's calling me. Come back home."* · Dorrie Jones (Arlparra): *"Good for me and comfy,
easy to put together."* · Melissa Jackson: *"They like to have lower beds, especially for our
older people."* · Heather Mundo: *"These two boys just picked it up straight away."* · Patricia
Frank carries the washing/health thread: *"They truly wanna a washing machine to wash their
blanket… it's right there at home."* Specs carry quietly underneath (26kg, no tools, washable,
200kg, recycled HDPE; Speed Queen base, one-button).

**Turn 4 — The making belongs in community hands. Young people are one proof; families are
another.**
The Bloomfield family built the five current Pakkimjalki Kari machines with Nic (Jan 2026).
Mykel (Utopia): *"Never would have thought it would have come out like that."* → *"Yeah, I'll
be rocking up every day to make them."* · Fred Campbell narrating Xavier: *"He had the pattern
of how everything was all coming together… so proud showing them that he can build it."* ·
Karen Liddle (Oonchiumpa): *"To see kids' faces with joy after making a bed, it just really
hits you."* · Kristy Bloomfield: the safe-space-for-young-people line. Oonchiumpa is proof,
not a side story: same partner held the young people, the build, the households, and the
question of who owns the work next. Ebony & Jahvan (Palm Island) training with Defy Design as
future manufacturing leads extend the same pattern.

**Turn 5 — The plant makes the pattern transferable; ownership is the promise.**
The containerised plant (visible, teachable, movable: Alice → Tennant Creek → Katherine →
Darwin circuit). Karen Liddle: *"I had a yarn with the girls one day. Said you got to get out
and start your own business. That's how we started Oonchiumpa."* · Shayne Bloomfield: *"This
partnership could go a long way. I feel it's got a long, long path ahead."* · Norman Frank:
*"I want to see a better future for our kids and better housing for our people."* PICC's
"we'll buy the production facility itself" and Dianne's self-funded 20 sit here as demand for
the MODEL, not just the bed.

**Turn 6 — What the capital does (once, near the end).**
Funds the bridge: the 50-bed in-source run (modelled → measured), the first place-based
ownership pathway with Oonchiumpa, the enterprise-support layer (GM + BD), plant capex.
AU$400K recoverable, ≥1:1 match-eligible, QBE Catalysing Impact. Dianne carries scale
("each place needs its own version of the pattern"); Shayne carries the ask.
