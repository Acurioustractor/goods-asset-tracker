# Goods on Country: the impact model (single source of truth)

*Canonical as of 2026-06-18. This is THE model. It supersedes the eleven scattered impact framings found across the repo (website dimensions, the eight Snow principles, the public theory-of-change diagram, the three shifts, the ALMA lens, LCAA, the four pillars, the metric tiers, and the community snapshots). Every surface, public site, funder dashboard, deck, wiki article, social post, conforms to this one model or it gets brought to it. Numbers are canon (`v2/src/lib/data/asset-canonical.ts`). Voices are consent-cleared and verbatim. No figure or voice enters an external cut unless it clears both gates. Brand voice applies (no em dashes, straight quotes, On Country capitalised, no charity framing).*

---

## 1. The one rule (the spine)

Every impact claim travels three steps, on the same beat:

1. A community **voice** carries the felt change, the why, in their own words.
2. A canon **number** carries the scale, what is actually deployed and counted.
3. An honest **label** says how sure we are: verified, modelled, or future.

Voice plus face plus number, together. Never a statistic alone, never a quote alone. This is the whole model in one line. Everything below serves it.

## 2. The top line: three shifts (the elevator version)

When we have one breath to explain the change, it is three shifts. Each shift contains outcome domains from section 3.

- **Material shift.** Waste plastic becomes a durable, washable, repairable good, made On Country. (Domains: circular and local economy; rest and health.)
- **Economic shift.** This is the economics story. The freight tax puts a basic good out of reach, so the bed is built to beat the true remote cost, not the sticker price. Value, jobs and the making stay local, and the in-house cost-down moves Goods from grant-funded toward an enterprise that communities can own and run. (Domains: dignity and safety; jobs, On Country work and the path to ownership.)
- **Story shift.** This is Indigenous self-determination. Communities name it (Pakkimjalki Kari, named in Warumungu), design it, and own the record of it, on their terms. (Domain: Indigenous self-determination and community-led design.)

The three shifts are the headline. The five domains are how we carry evidence underneath them.

## 3. The five outcome domains (the backbone)

Each domain is carried by cleared voices and anchored to a canon number. The label says what we can honestly claim.

### 2. Dignity and safety
A bed is safety and belonging, and the freight tax is why a basic good is out of reach. A need, not charity.

### 3. Indigenous self-determination and community-led design
Designed in community, with the people who will use it, and owned by them. "Never been asked" becomes "named it, tested it, we built what they asked for." Self-determination runs from the design through to who holds the data and the decisions.

- Anchor: the design-in-community practice. The washing machine name is the proof object.

### 4. Jobs, On Country work and the path to ownership
The making moves On Country. Real jobs are created where the plastic, the people and the need are. Young people build the product and, over time, hold the work and the ownership.

### 5. Circular and local economy
Plastic becomes beds On Country. Value and capability stay local instead of leaving the community, which is the economic core of the story: a local enterprise that, over time, funds itself rather than the grant cycle.
- Voice: Mykel (cleared by Ben 2026-06-18) carries the circular story directly, alongside the number.

## 4. The honest-label ladder (and the public phrasing)

Three internal labels, with the plain words we use when a community or funder reads them:

- **Verified** = checkable today against the asset register or a primary source. Public phrasing: "what we know" or "what we have counted".
- **Modelled** = computed from a stated assumption (for example 20kg per bed). Public phrasing: "our best estimate" or "what we are testing".
- **Future** = not yet collected, needs a partner method, never dressed up as live. Public phrasing: "what we do not claim yet".

**The claim ceiling (absolute).** The scabies to rheumatic heart disease pathway is our **why**. It is the reason a bed and a washing machine are health hardware. It is never a claimed outcome. We do not say a bed prevented heart disease, we do not badge a health cascade as interrupted, we do not publish a government-savings number, and we do not claim a bed-to-courtroom justice outcome. A health or justice outcome only leaves the future column when a partner clinical or evaluation method (Miwatj and equivalent ACCHOs) produces it, attributed to that partner.

## 6. The arc and the two through-lines (the philosophy)

Three lines hold the whole story:

- Demand follows use. People ask for more after they have used the product, not before.
- The making belongs On Country. Press the bed where the plastic, the people, and the need are.
- Our goal is to become unnecessary. Success is community owning the making, not Goods growing.

Two through-lines run across all five domains. They are not separate domains, they are the spine that connects them.

- **The economics story.** A basic good is out of reach in remote communities because of the freight tax and goods that fail in months. Goods answers that with a durable, washable, repairable bed built to beat the true remote cost, made On Country so the value and the jobs stay local, with an in-house cost-down (toward about AU$421 to AU$425 a bed, modelled) that moves the work from grant-funded toward a community-owned enterprise that can stand on its own. The economics are the impact, not a footnote to it. Detail lives in the cost model (QBE Area 11) and the business model (QBE Area 03).
- **Indigenous sovereignty.** Self-determination runs through every layer: cultural (the washing machine named Pakkimjalki Kari in Warumungu), design (named and tested in community), data (consent travels with the story, in the Mukurtu and Warumungu lineage), and economic (jobs, then ownership of the making). Sovereignty is what "become unnecessary" means in practice.

Mykel's arc is the single human spine: waste, to product, to skill, to work, to ownership. It is the model in one person.

## 7. Method layers (how we listen and select)

These sit underneath the model. They are how a story is gathered and earns its place. They are not competing domain sets and they never replace the five domains.

- **The story-selection lens.** The six signals currently documented in `wiki/articles/impact/alma-framework.md` decide whether a story is ready and true to carry weight. **Retire the "ALMA" name:** that acronym already means Alternative Local Models Australia in our work, so the lens keeps the thinking under a plain name (story-selection lens) and the old label is removed everywhere to stop the collision.
- **The engagement-stage model (Listen, Curiosity, Action, Advocacy).** How we move with a community over time, from first listening to a community advocating for itself. A relationship arc, not an outcome measure.

## 10. Conformance checklist (every surface, public and internal)

A surface is on-model when all of these are true.

Must do:
- Pairs a cleared voice with a canon number on the same beat (the spine).
- Uses the five domains, or the three shifts, as its structure. No other pillar set.
- Carries the two through-lines where relevant: the economics story and Indigenous sovereignty, not just the product.
- Pulls every asset number from `asset-canonical.ts`.
- Labels each claim verified, modelled, or future (or the public phrasing).
- Filters voices to the cleared-for-external set.

Must not do:
- Claim any health or justice outcome (the claim ceiling).
- Use charity framing ("sponsor a bed", "donate", "beneficiary", "every bed changes a family's life", "empower").
- Show a non-canon or stale number.
- Introduce a competing dimension, pillar, principle or horizon set.
- Carry a voice or face that is not cleared for external use.
