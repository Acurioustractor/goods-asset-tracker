# Goods on Country: the impact framework

*Draft for Ben, 2026-06-18. The reference model every impact cut draws from. Built from the consent-cleared storyteller corpus, the canon assets, and the 2026-06-05 living-ledger decision. Numbers are canon (`v2/src/lib/data/asset-canonical.ts`). Voices are consent-cleared and verbatim. No figure or voice enters an external cut unless it clears both gates.*

## The spine: from voice to verified

One rule holds the whole framework together. Every impact claim travels three steps:

1. A community voice carries the felt change, the why, in their own words.
2. A canon number carries the scale, what is actually deployed and counted.
3. An honest label says how sure we are: verified (checkable today), modelled (computed from a stated assumption), or future (not yet collected, never dressed up as live).

Voice plus face plus number on the same beat. Never a statistic alone, never a quote alone. This is the discipline the website and QBE Area 02 already run on. The framework makes it the organising idea.

## Five outcome domains

Each domain is carried by cleared voices and anchored to a canon number. The label says what we can honestly claim.

### 1. Rest and health (the core why)
Off-the-ground, washable sleep that supports the conditions needed to interrupt the scabies to rheumatic heart disease pathway. This is the rationale for treating a bed and a washing machine as health hardware, not furniture.
- Voices: Ray Nelson (Plenty Highway, bed GB0-156-96), Brian Russell (Tennant Creek), Gloria Turner (Kalgoorlie). Practitioner frame: Dr Boe Remenyi (cardiologist), labelled as a practitioner, not a recipient.
- Anchor: 496 beds, 9 communities (verified). A reduction in rheumatic heart disease is never claimed without partner clinical evidence (Miwatj and equivalent ACCHOs). It stays in the future column.

### 2. Dignity and safety
A bed is safety and belonging, and the freight tax is why a basic good is out of reach. A need, not charity.
- Voices: Alfred Johnson (Palm Island), Ivy (Palm Island). Practitioner context: Chloe (Kalgoorlie).
- Anchor: 9 communities served. The freight story sits in the cost model (QBE Area 11).

### 3. Community-led design and self-determination
Designed in community, with the people who will use it. "Never been asked" becomes "named it, tested it, we built what they asked for."
- Voices: Linda Turner (Tennant Creek), Dianne Stokes (Tennant Creek, named the washing machine Pakkimjalki Kari in Warumungu), Patricia Frank (Tennant Creek).
- Anchor: the design-in-community practice. The washing machine name is the proof object.

### 4. Youth, jobs and the path to ownership
The making moves on Country. Young people build the product and, over time, hold the work and the ownership.
- Voices: Mykel (cleared by Ben 2026-06-18, canon and the Snow dashboard; a young person, handled with extra care), Fred Campbell and the Xavier arc (Oonchiumpa, Alice Springs), Kristy Bloomfield (Oonchiumpa lead), Gary. Mykel is the human spine here.
- Anchor: 16 washers in community; the 50-bed in-source run (target, labelled, not yet run). Jobs and ownership are labelled future or target, never as achieved.

### 5. Circular and local economy
Plastic becomes beds on Country. Value and capability stay local.
- Voice: Mykel (cleared by Ben 2026-06-18) carries the circular story directly, alongside the number.
- Anchor: 2,660kg plastic diverted, 20kg per bed (modelled).

## The arc that ties it together (the Goods philosophy)

Three lines hold the whole story:

- Demand follows use. People ask for more after they have used the product, not before.
- The making belongs on Country. Press the bed where the plastic, the people, and the need are.
- Our goal is to become unnecessary. Success is community owning the making, not Goods growing.

Mykel's arc is the single human spine: waste, to product, to skill, to work, to ownership. It is the framework in one person.
