# Borroloola/Ngardara: what to take, what to leave, what to act on

## TL;DR

Ben's analysis of the Borroloola/Ngardara ARENA microgrid (~$8.35M public catalytic capital toward a $19.62M total project) is genuinely useful for Goods, but only if applied at the right altitude. Used well, it sharpens our **capital-sequencing pattern** and unlocks two existing assets we have under-used:

1. **Ngardara Project (Latrell, Luke, Scott) is in our QBE cohort.** They are literally doing the model the analysis describes. We can be in conversation with them inside two weeks, not as imitators but as cohort peers learning sideways. The wiki at `cohort-peers.md` already flags this as "worth a direct conversation"; Ben's analysis confirms that conversation is now overdue.

2. **The First Nations Clean Energy Advice Grants Round 1 (open to 3 September 2026, $5K-$80K)** is a real, time-bounded readiness funding line for the Goods on-Country production plant if we frame it correctly. Goods plants need power. Powering a plant with solar + battery is not a stretch. It is a credible scope of work for a $40K-$80K advice grant.

Used badly, the analysis pushes us to rebrand Goods as a "Community-Owned Clean Energy and Circular Economy Hub". That is scope creep. Goods is furniture and washable household goods first. A clean-energy nexus is a sub-thread, not a new identity. **The fundability story we already have (durable goods, community ownership, blended capital, Beautiful Obsolescence handover) does not need a Borroloola repaint.** It needs catalytic-capital sequencing applied with discipline.

This artefact separates the three things to do with the analysis: **adopt as pattern, explore as thread, push back on as scope.**

## What is the Borroloola/Ngardara project actually

(Reading the user-supplied facts and the linked sources together.)

- **Total project cost:** ~$19.62M.
- **ARENA grant component:** ~$8.35M (~43% of total).
- **Technical scope:** 2.1 MW solar array, 1.8 MW / 6.6 MWh battery, 2 MVA substation, integrated into existing Borroloola diesel network.
- **Lead structure:** Ngardara Co-operative leads / majority-owns; Original Power is co-development partner.
- **Outcomes claimed:** community ownership, bill relief through prepaid meter credits, training and employment, reduced emissions, replicable model for other communities.
- **Capital sequencing:** community entity establishes legitimacy, early money pays for readiness (governance, land access, technical design, utility interface, community support, workforce planning, financial model), government grant de-risks the rest of the stack, household benefit is concrete (not vague community uplift).
- **ARENA's stated rationale:** these projects de-risk future community microgrids by generating learnings other communities can use.

This is a solid example of catalytic-capital structuring in a remote First Nations context. The capital scale is ~10x larger than Goods' immediate raise and the technology is grid infrastructure, not durable goods. So the value is in the **shape**, not the project itself.

## What to ADOPT (patterns we already share or should sharpen)

These are principles the Borroloola model demonstrates that are already in our QBE strategy. Ben's analysis lets us sharpen our language and sequencing.

### 1. Catalytic capital de-risks the rest of the stack

We have this in our blended finance design (`enterprise/10-investors-capital-raising.md`). QBE Foundation grant up to $400K is match-contingent and explicitly catalytic. Snow's letter of support sits in the guarantees layer. Mindaroo recoverable and PFI repayable grant are catalytic by design.

**What Borroloola adds:** the discipline that catalytic capital should be sized large enough relative to total round to actually move private/philanthropic money. ARENA at 43% of project cost is doing real signalling work. If our QBE grant lands at $66K against a $5M target, the catalytic ratio is ~1.3% and the signal is weak. **Implication:** push to size the QBE grant toward the upper bound ($400K) precisely because the catalytic-ratio matters for what it unlocks downstream, not just the dollars in hand.

### 2. Community entity leads or majority-owns

We have this articulated in topics 01 (community ownership BHAG), 03 (40% profit share non-negotiable), 09 (51% Indigenous structure work in flight, end-Q3 2026 target). Ngardara is leading via co-op + Original Power.

**What Borroloola adds:** confirms ARENA, government and likely SEFA / IBA all reward majority-Indigenous ownership at the operating-entity level, not just advisory. Reinforces urgency on the 1 July 2026 IPP cutoff. This is already top of the meta-review action stack.

### 3. Replicability framing

We have this in Beautiful Obsolescence (topic 01 line 28, ACT wiki concept). Goods plants are designed to be transferable to community ownership; PICC is the first signalled buyer.

**What Borroloola adds:** ARENA's explicit reasoning around "learnings other communities can use" is exactly the language we should adopt for the QBE Stage 2 application and for any state/federal procurement pitch. Reframe Goods plants not as "facilities we operate" but as "first instances of a model designed to be replicated by community owners". This is already implicit; Borroloola gives us the public-funder vocabulary to say it.

### 4. Concrete household benefit, not vague community uplift

Borroloola promises specific bill relief through prepaid meter credits + workforce training + apprenticeships. We have specific Stretch Bed cost savings (10x lifespan vs $200 disposable beds), measurable HDPE diversion (9,225 kg), 33 storyteller voices, scabies-to-RHD KPMG $1:$6 ROI clinical wedge.

**What Borroloola adds:** discipline to lead with quantified household-level benefit, not abstract impact. We have the numbers. Lead with them in the pack page 1 and the diagnostic opening line, not as a buried table.

### 5. Staged funding case (readiness → demonstrator → blended → replicable)

The user's proposed stage model is a clean restatement of what we already do, with sharper labels. Worth adopting verbatim as the four-stage frame in the QBE Stage 2 application:

- **Stage 1 (readiness):** small grants for advice, planning, governance, technical scoping. Goods has $445K received philanthropic + the $40K-$80K FNCE Advice Grant on the table.
- **Stage 2 (demonstrator):** the Alice Springs / Oonchiumpa first plant is the demonstrator. Already in design.
- **Stage 3 (blended capital stack):** the $2.5M-$5M round is exactly this. Already designed in topic 10.
- **Stage 4 (replicable model):** Tennant Creek, Katherine, Darwin circuit. PICC plant transition. Already in horizon 2-3.

So adopting Borroloola's vocabulary tightens our story without changing our scope.

### B. Apply for the First Nations Clean Energy Advice Grant Round 1

**Why:** Open to **3 September 2026**, $5K-$80K, eligible for First Nations communities/groups to access advice, planning and support for clean energy development. Eligible uses include technical advice, legal advice, financial advice, renewable resource mapping, community engagement, training, monitoring/reporting and community benefit agreements.

The Goods Alice Springs plant (Oonchiumpa partnership) is genuinely energy-intensive. Powering it with solar + battery instead of grid diesel is a real engineering question with real climate, cost-of-living, and reliability implications. The advice grant is **explicitly designed for the readiness work this question requires**.

**This is not a stretch fit.** Goods qualifies because:
- Oonchiumpa is 100% Aboriginal-staffed and the operating entity for the plant.
- The energy question is real (containerised plant, plastic shredding and moulding are energy-loads).
- Solar + battery is the obvious answer in Alice Springs latitude.
- Community benefit agreement is part of the eligible scope (40% profit share is already non-negotiable).

**What the grant would fund:**
- Technical scoping: solar/battery sizing for plant load profile.
- Legal advice on Oonchiumpa-as-energy-asset-owner.
- Financial model: capex, opex, payback vs grid diesel.
- Community engagement: co-design of the energy benefit (offset to plant power bills, surplus to community uses).
- Documentation: replicability for Tennant Creek, Katherine, Darwin plants.

**Estimated ask:** $40K-$60K. **Owner:** Ben writes the EOI, Nick provides plant load assumptions, Sally reviews the funder framing. **Deadline:** EOI drafted within 30 days, lodged within 60.

**Benefit beyond the cash:** receiving an FNCE Advice Grant is itself a credibility signal for the QBE Stage 2 application. It demonstrates ability to assemble blended grant funding across multiple federal programs.

## What to PUSH BACK ON (scope creep risk)

These are parts of Ben's analysis that are well-intended but, if adopted, would dilute the QBE story we have built.

### 1. Do NOT rebrand Goods as "Community-Owned Clean Energy and Circular Economy Hubs"

The user's suggested working title is "GOODS: Community-Owned Clean Energy and Circular Economy Hubs" with a pitch:

> GOODS will develop a community-owned clean-energy-enabled hub model that lowers household costs, strengthens local access to essential goods, creates training and employment pathways, and demonstrates how First Nations/community-led circular economy infrastructure can deliver climate, economic and social benefits.

This is a **clean-energy hub** pitch. Goods is a **durable household goods + on-Country production** company. The two stories overlap but they are not the same. If we lead with the energy-hub framing in front of QBE, SEFA, IBA and Snow, we contradict three years of positioning, three years of product evidence, and the $445K already raised. The mentor will reasonably ask "wait, are you a furniture company that wants to do energy, or an energy company that does furniture?" That confusion is the cost.

**Stick with the existing positioning** (durable goods for remote communities, on-Country production, community ownership endgame) and **add energy as a sub-thread** under business model and process maturity. The story stays coherent. The energy nexus appears where it belongs: as one input to the plant, not the headline.

### 2. Do NOT chase ARENA's Advancing Renewables Program or Future Made in Australia Innovation Fund

Ben's analysis lists these as "possibly relevant if". The "if" is doing a lot of work. ARENA Advancing Renewables requires "renewable energy technology or enabling technologies with a clear renewable-energy nexus". Future Made in Australia is for "green metals, renewable energy technology manufacturing and low-carbon liquid fuels". Goods' core product (Stretch Bed, washing machine) is neither. We could contort to fit, but we would burn calendar time we do not have before 31 August 2026.

**Stick with the FNCE Advice Grant** (genuine fit) and **leave the larger ARENA streams for a later round** when the energy story is genuinely mature.

### 3. Do NOT confuse Borroloola's $19.62M scale with our $400K-$5M scale

Borroloola is a grid-infrastructure project. Our raise is a small social enterprise blended round. Mentors and funders will spot the scale mismatch instantly if we use Borroloola comparisons too directly. Use it to **inform pattern**, not to **claim equivalence**. Better framing in the pack: "We are designing the same capital sequencing pattern proven at scale by Ngardara Co-operative's Borroloola microgrid (ARENA-backed, $19.62M). Our scale is appropriate to our stage."

That is humble and credible. "We are doing what Borroloola is doing" is overreach.

### 4. Do NOT pitch Ngardara as a syndicate partner in the QBE pack page 1

The Ngardara conversation is exploratory. Until we have actually talked to Latrell, Luke or Scott, naming them as a syndicate partner in mentor or funder materials would be premature and potentially damaging to a cohort relationship we have not yet activated. **Have the conversation first. Update positioning after.**
