# How the cost model builds the DEWR / community model

**Date:** 2026-07-22
**Status:** Canonical forward-look. The strategic read of the cost numbers.
**Why:** The cost work landed three hard numbers: a community facility costs ~$340,000/yr to run properly, needs ~1,000 beds/yr to cover itself on bed sales, and no remote site will hit that from product revenue alone in its early years. Those are not a problem to hide. They point straight at the operating model Oonchiumpa and the DEWR REAL Innovation Fund application already describe. This document makes that case.

## 1 · The core truth: two pots of money, never mixed

A community facility that runs properly costs **~$340,000/yr** and needs **~1,000 beds/yr** to carry itself on bed sales. Handing a community a plant without the operating money attached would be handing them a $340,000/yr liability. So the model has to separate two things that must never be funded from the same pot:

- **Production pays for itself.** A bed makes $324 to $329 contribution ($750 price less ~$421 to $426 marginal cost). At volume, product revenue carries the materials, the labour, the machine upkeep. This is the **investment** case: a plant that pays back.
- **The wraparound never pays back, and should not.** The manager, the trainer, the WHS officer, and above all the youth-employment and training program ($300,000/yr of brokerage in the DEWR budget) are carried by grant and government money. This is the **jobs-are-the-dividend** case. It is philanthropically and government funded by design, and that is a feature, not a weakness.

## 2 · How to run it with staff, in a supported way

The DEWR application already prices the supported version: a full-time project manager, a trainer / WHS officer, insurance, admin, and cultural governance. That is what "run by community, supported" actually costs, and it is why the program money exists.

The honest line for a partner:

> The *production* can stand on its own at volume. The *employment and training* is grant and government funded, by design. We are not pretending bed sales will pay a youth worker's wage. That is what the DEWR money is for.

This is also the honest correction to the old "75 to 100 beds a year and a site stands on its own" claim. That was only ever true of a facility with no manager, no insurance, no admin and no WHS. A properly staffed site needs 460 to 1,040 beds a year to cover itself on bed sales alone. Say the staffed number, and say what carries it.

## 3 · Link with other projects: spread the fixed block

A single remote site carrying a $340,000/yr block alone is fragile. The ACT consortium shape fixes that:

- **Oonchiumpa leads.** ACT holds the machinery. The trainer/WHS, admin, insurance and cultural governance are **shared across projects** rather than each site standing up its own.
- That is how a bare facility (~$152,000/yr) becomes viable long before a site can hit 1,000 beds a year on its own: the heavy fixed costs are **consortium-carried, not site-carried**, until the community is ready to take them on.

The break-even math, for reference:

| Yearly facility bill | Beds/yr to break even |
|---|---|
| $24,000 (old rent-only figure) | 73 |
| $151,666 (bare facility, no staff) | 461 |
| $341,666 (facility + manager + trainer) | 1,038 |

Consortium-carrying the staff block is what moves a community site from the impossible bottom row toward the achievable middle one while it grows.

## 4 · Build the community model in partnership, honestly

- **ACT holds the plant until the community is ready.** Ownership is a pathway, not a handover of a liability. Selling or leasing a plant without the operating money attached would set a community up to fail.
- **Grant money carries the program; product revenue carries production.** Keep them in separate pots on every budget, every pitch, every report.
- **The 50-bed run is the missing evidence.** It settles capacity (250 versus 1,500 beds/yr), and capacity decides whether community ownership is viable at all. Zero beds have been pressed in-house yet, so every Factory and Community figure is modelled. It is the single most valuable next thing to do.
- **The DEWR budget has an internal reconciliation issue.** Budget table total $1,995,000, but a stated "$578k average" and a $647k to $525k profile that cannot sum to $1,995,000. If it is not submitted, fix it. If it is, expect the question.

## 5 · Why this is a stronger pitch, not a weaker one

The instinct is to hide that a community site cannot pay a youth worker from bed sales. The opposite is true. A funder who is handed "this plant pays for itself and also runs a youth program" will not believe it, and should not. A funder who is handed "the plant pays for itself, here is the evidence, and the youth program is grant-funded by design, here is why that is the right money for it" is being told the truth, and it is a truth they can fund with confidence. The separation is the credibility.
