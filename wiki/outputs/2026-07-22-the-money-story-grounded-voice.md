# What the money is for

> The money story in the ACT voice, tuned to funder-simple (the dial of the 12 blessed lines). Grounded and honest, but short: a funder gets each part in one pass. The Goods Cost Lines database and the connected books are the receipts underneath. Every figure traces. No em dashes, no grant-speak. Claims kept honest: the DEWR figures price an application, not secured money; ownership is a pathway; scabies to rheumatic heart disease is the why, never a claimed outcome.

A Stretch Bed leaves the farm for $750.

Today, about $680 of that is gone before it reaches a family: the plastic kit cut down south, the steel, the canvas, the long freight north. $65 stays with the work.

Press the legs ourselves, from plastic collected on Country, and the bed costs a little over $400 to make. $324 stays. Same bed, same price to the family. The only thing that moves is where it is made, and who holds it after.

That is the capital case in two numbers. Not a margin. A supply chain to bring home.

## Two pots. We never mix them.

One pot builds beds. Bed sales pay for the plastic, the labour, the machines. At volume it stands on its own, and it can go to a community.

The other pot pays the people: the manager, the trainer, the young worker being paid to learn. A bed will never cover a wage. So grant and government money carries the people. The jobs are not a cost. They are the point.

Mix the pots and you hand a community a plant and a wages bill in the same breath. The gift becomes a debt with a roof. So we keep them apart, on every budget.

## The plant is already real

It is standing at the farm: two containers, a generator, a shredder, presses, a router. It presses beds now.

About $110,000 has gone into it, probably more, bought mostly second hand. Around $84,000 of that is cleanly in the connected books; the rest is gear we own but whose paperwork is still catching up: the shredder is running but its invoice is not in those books yet, and a bigger router arrived with only its install recorded. That is a filing job, not a fiction.

A fresh site, at honest market rates, costs about $90,000 to $120,000 to put in the ground. Most of that is the plant itself.

## What a site costs to run

A community site that runs properly, with a manager, trainer, safety officer, insurance and cultural governance, costs about $340,000 a year. To cover that from bed sales alone, a site needs close to 1,000 beds a year. No remote site hits that early.

This is not a problem to hide. It is why we never sell a plant and walk away. A plant without the operating money attached is a liability dressed as a gift. So the plant stays with us until a community is ready, and the money that pays the people is found first, not after.

## One plant, many shapes

Because the pots are separate, the plant takes whatever shape a community needs. It depends on two things: what they can run, and what they can link to.

A justice program links the plant to justice money, and the young people who build beds are paid from money that already exists. A health service links it to health. A school links it to attendance. One place runs it as an enterprise, another as a program, most as a blend.

The plant does not change. The stream that fills the second pot does. Goods is not one plan we carry from town to town. It is a set of parts a community builds from.

## Oonchiumpa is the one we can show

In Alice Springs, this is already real. Young people build beds. Some chose to be paid with a bed instead of cash. The people side is priced in a DEWR application, about $300,000 a year for the wages and training that justice and employment money is meant to carry. Bed sales carry the beds. Neither pays for the other.

Dianne Stokes received one bed, came back asking for twenty, and offered to pay herself. Shayne Bloomfield drove our biggest delivery and said the model in one line: "A lot of people say community is community and they need what we've got. When you flip it, we need what they've got."

## The one number we do not have yet

We have made beds in-house. Our first forty, pressed and assembled at our own plant, are in homes in Maningrida. What we have not done yet is run that plant at sustained volume, or run one on-country. So the in-house cost per bed is still modelled, not measured.

A measured run, with a stopwatch on it, settles whether a site makes 250 beds a year or 1,500. That one number decides whether a community can carry a plant at all. It is the first thing this raise buys, and the most honest thing we can show a funder: here is what we have proven, forty beds made ourselves, and here is the one thing we have not, the rate at volume.

## Where this leaves us

The bed is the evidence. The plant is real. The pots stay separate. The plant goes home when a community is ready, and the money that pays the people is found first.

This is not a pitch to be funded forever. It is a design that ends the asking.

Nobody funds Goods forever. That is the design.
