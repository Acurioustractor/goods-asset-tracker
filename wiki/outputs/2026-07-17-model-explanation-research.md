## 2. The techniques that make a model feel inevitable (ranked)

1. **Single killer napkin-math number.** The model reduces to one per-unit figure the investor multiplies live. If it survives the quick math, the slide is won. *(Airbnb's 10%.)*
2. **The secret / why-now.** A specific, recent, undeniable shift, or a counterintuitive truth few agree with, that makes the model viable *now*. Creates urgency and inevitability. *(Thiel: "what important truth do few people agree with you on?")*
3. **The flywheel, with the mechanism shown.** Each unit makes the next cheaper or better. The most over-claimed slide, so *draw the loop and prove it*, never assert it. *(Amazon.)*
4. **The one-line analogy "X for Y."** Borrows one attribute of a famous winner. Powerful memory device, but you can't control which attribute they anchor on, so add a clarifier. *(Coinbase, Uber.)*
5. **The wedge.** Dominate one narrow place, then expand outward *with the same model*. *(Uber-SF.)*
6. **Narrative written before slides.** Story discipline reads as business discipline. *(Front's three beats.)*
7. **Traction-first single metric.** If you have real current numbers, nothing moves a room faster. *(Buffer.)*
8. **One revenue stream on the slide.** Even if you have several, lead with the one that drives the business. *(Hoffman.)*
9. **Honest before/after or 2×2.** Pick two axes customers care about; claim an *underserved* quadrant, not "best" (VCs see through the top-right trick).
10. **Bottoms-up market size.** Build TAM from real units, not "1% of a huge market."

## 3. The Goods model, explained to push investors (the applied framing)

Goods' model is unusually strong on exactly the moves that win: it has a **single napkin-math number**, a **counterintuitive secret**, and a **real flywheel**. Use all three.

### The three beats (write these before any slide, per Front)
1. **Why we started:** remote families pay double for goods that fail in months, and the making, the wage and the machinery always begin somewhere else.
2. **Why it's working:** 496 beds delivered, every one on a QR register; the product is proven and demand keeps coming back after use.
3. **Why it could be 100x bigger:** the making moves on Country, and the economics compound: one container funds the next, toward community-owned production.

### The model in one breath (the line to lead with)
> We pay an 8.6x markup on the plastic legs. Bring that inside and the bed's cost roughly halves. Here is the part nobody expects: a fair community wage lands *below* a factory, so dignity is free. Each container makes about $140,000 surplus a year and repays its build cost in under a year, so one container funds the next. The return we are building is the transfer itself.

That single paragraph carries four proven moves at once: the napkin-math number (8.6x), the secret (fair wages cost nothing), the flywheel (container funds container), and the mission-lock (the return is the transfer).

### The napkin-math number (the engine, per Airbnb + a16z "show the engine behind the revenue")
- A finished HDPE leg kit costs **$344.05** (verified invoices); the shredded plastic inside it costs about **$40**. That **8.6x** gap is the engine.
- In-sourcing captures about **$260 a bed**, moving contribution from about **$65 to about $324 a bed** at the $750 price [modelled].
- One line: *"Every bed we make ourselves captures about $260 the market currently loses to markup."* Vertical integration is a recognised thesis: internalising the margin third parties extract can fund the business by itself.

### The secret (per Thiel's "what do few people agree with you on")
> **Fair wages cost the model nothing.** Community production at a fair wage lands about $5 a bed *below* a factory. There is no trade-off between viable economics and paying people properly.

This is the line that disarms the reflexive impact-investor worry (that impact and returns trade off). It is Goods' contrarian truth, and it is the anchor for every impact-capital conversation.

### The flywheel (per Amazon, shown not asserted), named in investor language
> One container (~$125K) makes ~$140K surplus a year and repays its build cost in under a year. That surplus funds the next container. By year five: 5,000+ beds a year, 100+ tonnes of plastic diverted, and about $1M a year of surplus compounding, and then each container transfers to community.

Label it with the term investors already price: a **self-liquidating / revolving model** — repaid principal recycles into the next unit. Pair it with the **QBE Catalysing Impact** match (the only place "catalytic" is allowed) as the concessional layer that brings the first containers forward.

### The wedge (per Uber-SF)
> One plant, one community, the same repeatable model transferring outward, place by place. Ownership is a pathway, moving closer to community-owned production, never claimed complete.

### The blended-capital view (per the impact-investor playbook)
Put **unit economics and impact-per-dollar in the same view**, with a path to a targeted return, never in separate sections. "Investors respect impact, but they fund businesses." Impact-per-bed (20kg plastic, one family off the floor, $130 fair wage, a consented story) sits beside the contribution math on one slide.

## 4. The two slides that decide it (applied to Goods)

### The model slide (the "quick-math" test)
It must answer in under five seconds: how does this make money and compound? Winning model slides name **one model, 1–3 streams, a unit-economics signal, and a scaling mechanic**, and quote *today's* economics, not projected ("$65 today, $324 in-sourced" beats "great margins at scale"). For Goods: the bed sale at $750, the captured margin (napkin math), and the self-liquidating flywheel, on one screen. The hero "Machine" slide in §6 is built to pass this test.

### The why-now slide (the urgency engine)
Weak = "the market is growing." Strong = a **trigger that did not exist or did not matter before**, ideally a convergence. Goods' why-now:
- **Micro-manufacturing** makes an On-Country container plant viable now.
- **Freight and HDPE economics** make local production beat imported goods now.
- **Procurement policy** (Indigenous Procurement Policy thresholds tightening) opens institutional demand now.
- **The market failure is live:** $3M of washing machines dumped near Alice Springs every year.
- **The stakes (the why, not an outcome):** floor-sleeping and dirty bedding sit upstream of the highest rheumatic-heart-disease rates in the world.
