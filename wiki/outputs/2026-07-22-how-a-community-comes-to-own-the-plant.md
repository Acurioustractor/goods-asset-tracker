# How a community comes to own the plant

**Date:** 2026-07-22
**Status:** Draft for Ben and Nic. Nothing here is decided.
**Why this exists:** The cost model proves a community site works once it exists. Nothing anywhere says how a community actually comes to hold it. This is the question every funder asks straight after the handover slide, and today there is no answer on paper.

---

## 1 · What is already settled, and what is not

Settled (`wiki/articles/enterprise/03-business-model.md:283`):

> The principle is settled. The legal and financial mechanism is not.

Also settled:

- The direction is transfer, not just sales. Local people collect, sort, shred, press, cut, assemble, deliver and sell. Goods keeps R&D, design, quality, training and back office only where useful.
- The old **40 percent community profit share is a placeholder, not a committed structure.** It should not be revived in the deck or in conversation.
- Community ownership is a **pathway**, always. Never claimed as complete.

Not settled, and each one blocks something real:

- Legal form for community ownership or plant transfer.
- How licence fees, shared services or equipment rental work without hollowing out the ownership.
- The 51 percent First Nations ownership decision. This is the keystone. It gates IBA and First Australians Capital eligibility and the Supply Nation certification, and the IPP threshold tightened around 1 July 2026.
- Entity structure below A Curious Tractor. Expected to be worked through after the Butterfly charity handover lands, which was tracking to about end July 2026.

## 2 · The numbers a community site actually runs on

All modelled, from the current cost model. The process is proven (40 beds pressed and assembled in-house at the farm, Maningrida INV-0303); what is not yet measured is the per-bed cost/time at a sustained rate.

| Figure | Value |
|---|---|
| Bed price | $750 |
| Cost to build, legs pressed on site | about $426 |
| Stays with the site, per bed | about $329 |
| Yearly bill to run a community site | about $24,000 |
| Beds a year to cover that bill | about 73 |
| Working target | 75 to 100 beds a year |
| Site capital, IBA shaped | $100,000 to $150,000 a site |

Read the surplus carefully, because it is the whole story:

- At **75 beds**: $24,675 contribution, minus the $24,000 bill, leaves about **$675 a year**.
- At **100 beds**: $32,900 contribution, minus the $24,000 bill, leaves about **$8,900 a year**.

**This is the finding that matters.** At 75 to 100 beds a year a site pays its bills and pays the people working in it. It does not throw off enough to buy itself. Retiring $125,000 of plant out of $8,900 a year takes about fourteen years.

So "the site stands on its own" and "the site can buy itself" are two different claims, and only the first one is true at the volumes currently modelled.

**The volume where buying itself becomes real:** about **110 to 130 beds a year**. At 127 beds a year the site clears roughly $17,850 above its bill, which retires $125,000 in about seven years. At 111 beds it does it in about ten. That is arithmetic off the figures above, not a new model, and it needs checking against the sheet before anyone says it out loud.

## 3 · The four routes

### Route A. It is gifted

Grant or philanthropic capital buys the plant. The community entity owns it outright from day one.

- **Costs the community:** nothing up front. They carry the $24,000 a year running bill from bed sales.
- **Goods gives up:** the asset, and any claim on it.
- **Needs:** a funder willing to capitalise an asset they will never see again, and a community entity that can legally hold it.
- **Risk:** an asset with no debt against it and no earn-in has no forcing function. Plants that arrive free can sit idle. Every remote community has a story about equipment that turned up and died.
- **Honest read:** cleanest ownership, weakest accountability. Works where the community organisation is already strong.

### Route B. Earn-in, or lease to own

Goods holds the plant. The community runs it. A fixed slice of each bed retires the capital until title transfers.

- **Costs the community:** a cut of the $329 a bed, for as long as it takes.
- **Goods gives up:** the asset eventually, and carries the capital in the meantime.
- **Needs:** volume above roughly 110 beds a year, or the earn-in runs longer than the equipment lasts.
- **Risk:** **at 75 to 100 beds a year this route does not work.** See section 2. Offering it at those volumes is offering a fourteen year mortgage on a press, and it would be a bad-faith deal dressed as an ownership pathway.
- **Honest read:** the most intuitive route and the one most likely to be proposed in the room. The arithmetic does not currently support it. Do not offer it until a site is demonstrably running above 110 beds a year.

### Route C. The community buys it with its own capital

A community-controlled entity borrows site capital, IBA or First Australians Capital shaped, at $100,000 to $150,000, and buys the plant.

- **Costs the community:** a real debt, serviced from bed sales.
- **Goods gives up:** the asset, at a fair price, and receives capital back to fund the next site.
- **Needs:** the 51 percent First Nations ownership decision made first. This route is locked shut until then. It also needs a community entity with the balance sheet and governance to carry debt.
- **Risk:** debt service competes with wages. At $8,900 a year of surplus, servicing $125,000 is not possible without either higher volume or concessional, patient terms.
- **Honest read:** structurally the strongest, because the community holds real title bought with its own capital. Blocked today by the entity decision, and only viable with genuinely soft terms.

### Route D. Joint venture, community majority

A new entity holds the plant. The community organisation holds the majority, Goods holds a minority stake plus a services agreement for design, quality and training.

- **Costs the community:** governance time, and a share of the surplus going to services.
- **Goods gives up:** control, and takes a minority position plus fee income.
- **Needs:** a designed fee structure. This is exactly the open question at `03-business-model.md:411`. Get the fees wrong and it is ownership on paper only.
- **Risk:** the failure mode is a community that owns 51 percent of something whose margin is all consumed by the service agreement. That is the social-washing pattern, and it is worse than not doing it.
- **Honest read:** the most flexible, and the easiest to get quietly wrong. If this is the route, the fee structure has to be designed in the open with the community, not retrofitted.

## 4 · What I would pick

**Route D as the structure, Route A for the first one.**

Reasoning:

- The first community site should be **gifted**, funded from the raise, and treated as the proof. Not as generosity, but because there is no honest way to sell or lease a plant whose in-house economics have never been measured. Nobody has pressed a bed in-house yet. Asking a community to take on debt against a modelled number would be selling them a forecast.
- Once one site has a **real year of numbers**, the choice between C and D can be made on evidence rather than argument, and by then the entity decision should have landed.
- Route D becomes the template for sites two and onward, with the fee structure designed once, in the open, and published.
- **Route B should come off the table** until a site is provably running above 110 beds a year. It is the route people will reach for instinctively and the arithmetic does not support it.

---

## 5 · What has to happen before any of this is real

1. **Make the 51 percent First Nations ownership decision.** It gates IBA, First Australians Capital and Supply Nation. Routes C and D are both locked until it is made. This is the keystone and it is a legal and human decision, not a documentation gap.
2. **Run a measured in-house proof.** The process is already proven (40 beds made in-house at the farm); the figures above are modelled on per-bed cost, not yet measured at a sustained rate. The whole ownership case rests on the $329 being real, so capture the actuals.
3. **Check the 110 to 130 bed threshold against the playable sheet.** If it moves, Route B either opens or stays shut.
4. **Design the service fee structure** before offering Route D to anyone. Open question at `03-business-model.md:411`.
5. **Decide which of the four communities goes first.** Palm Island, Alice Springs, Utopia and Tennant Creek have all asked. They will not all suit the same route.

---

## 6 · How to say it in the room

> A community site needs about 75 to 100 beds a year to pay its bills and pay its people. That much we have modelled. We have made 40 beds in-house at our own farm plant already, so the process is proven; what no community has done yet is run a plant on-country for a full year. So the first site we intend to fund outright and hand over, because we are not going to ask a community to take on debt against a rate we have not yet run at volume on-country. Once that site has a real year behind it, the communities coming after it can buy in properly, and we are working through what that ownership structure looks like now.

That is honest, it is specific, and it does not claim a mechanism that does not exist yet.
