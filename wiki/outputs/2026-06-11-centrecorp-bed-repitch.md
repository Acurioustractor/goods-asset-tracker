# Centrecorp Bed Repitch Pack

Prepared: 11 June 2026  
Meeting target: Centrecorp Foundation Board, 26 June 2026  
Primary ask: support for 130 Stretch Beds for Aboriginal families and young people in Central Australia

## Quick Read

Randle's feedback is useful and should be treated as the brief for the next version.

The Board did not reject the bed request. It deferred it and asked for clearer evidence. The revised pitch should drop the production plant and washing-machine asks for this meeting, correct the product language to **Stretch Bed**, and answer four Board questions directly:

1. Why these places and people?
2. What is the confirmed Central Australian Aboriginal benefit?
3. Who is helping deliver, prioritise and install the beds?
4. What has community feedback shown so far?

The core message should be:

> We are asking Centrecorp Foundation to fund 130 Stretch Beds for identified Aboriginal families and young people in Central Australia, delivered through local partners, with transparent prioritisation, community involvement in assembly and installation, and public reporting back to Centrecorp after delivery.

## Review Of The Original Applications

### Application 1: Production facility, $97,900

**What worked**

- Practical, enterprise-building idea.
- Strong connection to waste, local skills and future production.
- Long-term strategic fit with Goods on Country.

**Why it was weak for Centrecorp**

- The direct beneficiary was not tight enough. A production plant could benefit communities beyond Central Australia and non-Aboriginal customers over time.
- The benefit was conditional on future steps: product demand, further funding, local operator readiness and eventual production.
- It asked the Board to fund infrastructure before proving the direct Central Australian household outcome.

**Recommendation**

Do not lead with this at the 26 June meeting. Mention it only as the longer-term pathway after the bed delivery succeeds:

> The 130-bed delivery is the immediate household benefit. The production facility is the future employment and ownership pathway, and we will bring that back only once the direct Central Australian delivery evidence is complete.

### Application 2: Washing machine v2, $61,050

**What worked**

- Strong health and household need.
- Builds on Centrecorp's history with washing-machine access.
- Clear practical problem: remote homes need durable machines.

**Why it was weak for Centrecorp**

- The design and engineering phase creates potential benefit, not guaranteed benefit.
- If the product succeeds, the benefit could spread beyond Centrecorp's Trust Deed geography and beneficiary class.
- It needs a stronger Central Australia pilot, named local beneficiaries, and clearer product readiness before it is a clean Foundation ask.

**Recommendation**

Park it for now. Bring it back later as a Central Australia pilot with named delivery sites, confirmed local partners, and a direct household distribution plan.

### Application 3: 130 beds, $106,150

**What worked**

- It is the clearest direct benefit.
- Beds can be delivered now.
- The household outcome is immediate: people are sleeping off the ground on washable, durable beds.
- It can be restricted to Aboriginal peoples of Central Australia.

**What must be fixed**

- It called the product "Goods Weave Bed v2.3"; this must become **Stretch Bed** everywhere.
- It did not explain why Tennant Creek and Mparntwe were prioritised over other recent-need areas such as upper Barkly flooding.
- It did not name the partner roles clearly enough.
- It did not put all previous evidence, photos, community feedback and delivery learning into one package.
- It blended "workshops" with delivery without showing what employment, training or community involvement actually means.

**Recommendation**

Make the 26 June meeting a single-purpose bed repitch. No side asks. No prototype asks. No production-plant ask.

## Revised Ask

### Title

130 Stretch Beds for Central Australian Aboriginal Families and Young People

### Amount

$106,150 incl. GST, subject to final quote reconciliation.

### What Centrecorp Funds

- 130 Stretch Beds.
- Freight and practical delivery support to agreed Central Australian locations.
- Two local assembly and installation days with community partners.
- A simple delivery report back to Centrecorp with photos, counts, partner notes and community feedback where consent permits.

### What Centrecorp Does Not Fund

- Product research and development.
- Washing-machine design.
- Production plant infrastructure.
- Non-Central Australian distribution.
- General Goods on Country operating costs.

This distinction matters because it answers the Trust Deed issue directly.

## Eligibility Frame

Use this language early:

> This request is restricted to the benefit of Aboriginal peoples of Central Australia. The funded beds will be allocated only to Aboriginal families, Elders, young people or households in the agreed Central Australian delivery areas. No Centrecorp funding will be used for product development, national expansion, or non-Central Australian delivery.

Then repeat the control mechanism:

> We will provide Centrecorp with a pre-delivery allocation plan and a post-delivery report showing where the beds went, who the delivery partners were, how prioritisation happened, and what feedback was received.

## Answering Randle's Board Questions

### 1. How are areas, communities or towns being prioritised?

Recommended answer:

> We will prioritise by confirmed local need, partner readiness, and ability to deliver safely with community involvement. The first priority is not where Goods wants to go; it is where Aboriginal community partners can identify households or young people who need beds now, help allocate them fairly, and support installation.

Use a simple scoring table in the Board pack:

| Criterion | What The Board Should See |
| --- | --- |
| Confirmed need | Written request, partner referral, household or youth need list |
| Central Australia eligibility | Aboriginal beneficiaries in Central Australia only |
| Partner readiness | Named local organisation or community contact |
| Delivery practicality | Transport, storage, install support and timing are realistic |
| Community involvement | Local assembly, youth involvement, feedback capture, maintenance pathway |
| Urgency | Flooding, overcrowding, floor sleeping, health risk, family movement |

For the upper Barkly question, do not be defensive. Say:

> If Centrecorp's Board wants the 130 beds redirected to upper Barkly or flood-affected families, we are open to that. Our request is for beds for Aboriginal peoples of Central Australia, not for Goods to choose locations without the Board's guidance. We can bring a recommended allocation and adjust it with Centrecorp and local partners.

### 2. What community employment and training is planned?

Keep this modest and credible:

> This bed round will not overclaim full-time employment. It will create practical paid or supported participation in unloading, assembly, installation, feedback and basic maintenance. The training outcome is that local people understand how to assemble, move, clean and repair the Stretch Bed, and that young people can see the pathway from waste plastic to useful household infrastructure.

If funding allows, budget a local participation line:

- Local delivery coordination.
- Paid assembly helpers.
- Youth participation through partner organisation.
- Maintenance and part-replacement briefing.

Do not call it a production facility outcome unless that is actually funded.

## Meeting Script

### Opening

Thank you for the feedback. We heard the Board's concern clearly. The production plant and washing-machine proposals were too broad and too conditional for this meeting.

Today we are here for one ask only: 130 Stretch Beds for Aboriginal families and young people in Central Australia.

### Trust Deed Alignment

This request is restricted to Aboriginal peoples of Central Australia. The funded beds will not go to other regions or non-Aboriginal beneficiaries. The benefit is actual, not speculative: a bed is delivered, assembled and used by a household or young person.

### Why Beds

People are still sleeping on floors, couches and shared bedding. A Stretch Bed is not a research project. It is available now, washable, movable, repairable, and designed from community feedback.

### Prioritisation

We will prioritise by confirmed need, local partner readiness, urgency, and Central Australian eligibility. If the Board believes flood-affected upper Barkly families should be first, we will work with Centrecorp and local partners to adjust the allocation.

### Community Involvement

This round includes practical local involvement: unloading, assembly, installation, feedback, maintenance knowledge and youth participation where local partners support that. We will not overclaim employment. We will report exactly who was involved and what happened.

### Reporting

Centrecorp will receive a pre-delivery allocation plan and a post-delivery report with final counts, partner notes, photos and feedback where consent permits.

### Close

We are asking Centrecorp to fund the direct household outcome first. If this is delivered well, we can then come back with the larger production and training pathway on stronger evidence.

## Revised Application Summary

Goods on Country seeks $106,150 incl. GST from Centrecorp Foundation to deliver 130 Stretch Beds to Aboriginal families and young people in Central Australia.

The Stretch Bed is a flat-packable, washable bed made from recycled HDPE plastic legs, galvanised steel poles and heavy-duty Australian canvas. It weighs 26kg, supports 200kg, assembles in about five minutes without tools, and is designed for remote conditions.

This application is restricted to the benefit of Aboriginal peoples of Central Australia. Centrecorp funding will be used only for the agreed bed delivery, freight, practical delivery support, local assembly / installation activity, and reporting back to Centrecorp. It will not be used for product development, washing-machine design, production-plant infrastructure, national expansion, or non-Central Australian delivery.

The allocation will be finalised with Centrecorp and local partners using confirmed need, Central Australian eligibility, partner readiness, urgency and safe delivery capacity. Proposed locations include Tennant Creek and Mparntwe / Alice Springs region communities, with flexibility to redirect beds to upper Barkly or other priority Central Australian communities if Centrecorp identifies greater urgency.

Local partners will assist with beneficiary identification, delivery coordination, assembly, installation and feedback. Goods on Country will provide the beds, logistics, assembly support and post-delivery reporting. Centrecorp will receive a clear report showing where beds went, which partners were involved, how prioritisation occurred, and what community feedback was received.
