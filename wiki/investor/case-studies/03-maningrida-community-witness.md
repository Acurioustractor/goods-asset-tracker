# Case study 3 — Maningrida: the community witness account

> Pattern: the delivery team's own community members are the most credible witnesses.
> Cleared quotes marked; others internal pending Ben's pass. Figures register-verified.

## What happened

The largest single Goods deployment: 40 beds and a laundromat refit in Maningrida, with 8
washing machines now in community. Shayne Bloomfield, oldest of the six Bloomfield siblings,
drove the delivery: the Darwin road, floodwater crossings, gutting and scrubbing the laundromat,
then building about 20 beds with young people from the aged-care service for the Elders they
care for.

Weeks later the feedback was still arriving, unprompted (cleared):

"Today my mum still gets text messages from her family saying, I can't believe how good this
bed's going. I don't want to get outta bed in the morning 'cause it's just so comfortable."

And his summary of the whole approach (cleared):

"A lot of people say community is community and they need what we've got. When you flip it, we
need what they've got."

## Why it matters as a model

The biggest deployment had no written story until Shayne's transcript surfaced; his firsthand
account is now the narrative source, and it is better than anything the enterprise could write,
because he is accountable to the community he describes. His protocol line (internal, pending
clearance) describes entering through Elders and traditional owners before government business
managers: the sequence, from the person who did it.

## The replicable pattern

1. Employ and record community members on the delivery itself; their account is the story.
2. Let the witness set the frame (reciprocity, protocol), not the marketing team.
3. Unprompted post-delivery feedback (the text messages) is the durability evidence.
4. Write the public story FROM the transcript, never beyond it.
