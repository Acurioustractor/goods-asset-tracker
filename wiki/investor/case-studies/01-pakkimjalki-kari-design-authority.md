# Case study 1 — Pakkimjalki Kari: design authority lives in community

> Pattern: product legitimacy flows from named Elder authorship, on the record, consented.
> All quotes registry-verbatim and cleared (Ben pass 2026-07-20). Figures register-verified.

## What happened

Dianne Stokes, a Warumungu and Warlmanpa Elder in Tennant Creek, designed and named the Goods
washing machine: Pakkimjalki Kari, named in Warumungu. She refined the designs with family,
around the fire. When the machine reached her homeland she told us what it changed:

"I will know where to come. If I need to wash my blanket, I don't have to go do it in town.
Laundry, do it here."

And how it felt: "I'm happy. I, I can't tell you how my heart is, uh, hopping and skipping
inside me."

## Why it matters as a model

Most products arrive in remote communities designed elsewhere, priced elsewhere, and owned
elsewhere. One Alice Springs provider sells $3M a year of washing machines into remote
communities; most fail within months. Pakkimjalki Kari inverts the chain at its first link:
the design authority. The person who named the product is the person who uses it, and her
authorship is recorded, consented and credited.

Dianne also gave the operating doctrine for anyone entering a community:

"If you have an idea what place you'd go to, first go and talk to the elders. Sit down and talk
to them." And: "They need to hear from the ground, who wants it, who's gonna do it, who's out
there, who needs help?"

## The replicable pattern

1. Design happens in community, led by the people who will use the product.
2. Authorship is named and credited, in language where the Elder chooses.
3. The authority trail is recorded in the storyteller's own words, consented line by line.
4. The enterprise quotes the doctrine back as its operating manual, not as decoration.
