# Voice-Led Impact Measurement vs Traditional Frameworks: Research Alignment

> Research note for the Goods on Country voice-led, transcript-grounded impact model.
> Compiled 2026-07-20 from web research. Citation status: "verified" = page fetched and read;
> "search-result only" = cited from search snippet, page not directly fetched.
> No dollar or asset figures in this document; canon numbers live in `wiki/investor/` and `canon.ts`.

## 1. Traditional impact-measurement frameworks and their weaknesses

### Theory of Change (ToC)
A causal map from activities to outputs to outcomes to long-term impact, used to make a program's logic explicit and testable. Its known weakness in community contexts is that the logic is usually authored by the organisation or funder, not the community; linear causal chains fit poorly where change is relational, slow, and multi-causal; and the framework tends to lock in outcomes defined before the community has spoken. SROI critics note the same underlying problem: models fail where there is "complex or unobservable causality" and "lack of legitimacy among stakeholders" (Nielsen et al. 2021, search-result only: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/nml.21439).

### SROI (Social Return on Investment)
Monetises social outcomes into a ratio (e.g. $X of social value per $1 invested) using financial proxies. The literature's most recurrent criticism is lack of standardisation: proxy choice for "soft" outcomes (wellbeing, self-esteem) is highly subjective, and deadweight/attribution/drop-off assumptions make ratios non-comparable; different analysts on the same data produce different ratios (Emerald systematic review, search-result only: https://www.emerald.com/medar/article/30/7/49/282355/The-social-return-on-investment-model-a-systematic; Wiley: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/nml.21439). A deeper community-context failure: SROI in principle requires affected people to define and validate value, but in practice most organisations survey, then calculate their own version of what the community experienced (https://www.sopact.com/use-case/social-return-on-investment-sroi, search-result only). "Wide scope with thin evidence" is the most common reason SROIs fail external review.

### Impact Management Project (IMP) five dimensions
A shared vocabulary for impact: What, Who, How Much, Contribution, Risk (GIIN alignment doc, search-result only: https://iris.thegiin.org/document/iris-and-the-five-dimensions/). Weaknesses for small community enterprises: the full five-dimension profile per outcome is heavy for small teams; clean attribution ("Contribution") is rare in multi-actor community systems; and most organisations referencing the framework lack the data infrastructure to score all five dimensions consistently (https://umbrex.com/resources/frameworks/strategy-frameworks/impact-management-project-dimensions/, search-result only).

### IRIS+ (GIIN metrics catalogue)
A standardised catalogue of impact metrics for investors, mapped onto the IMP dimensions (https://iris.thegiin.org/document/iris-and-impact-management-project/, search-result only). Weakness: metrics are investor-defined and standardised for portfolio comparability, which is exactly the property that makes them poor at capturing community-defined outcomes; the demanding data collection sits on the investee and community, a structurally extractive arrangement when the community neither chose the metrics nor owns the data.

### Outcome harvesting
Wilson-Grau's method works backwards from observed outcomes (intended or not) to how the intervention contributed, suited to complex non-linear settings (https://www.betterevaluation.org/methods-approaches/approaches/outcome-harvesting, search-result only; INTRAC summary: https://www.intrac.org/app/uploads/2017/01/Outcome-harvesting.pdf, search-result only). Limitations: substantiating outcomes to a high credibility standard is resource-intensive, and practitioners struggle to surface negative outcomes (https://practicalmel.com/outcomes-harvesting-methodology-and-applications-in-me/, search-result only).

### Cross-cutting weaknesses for community contexts
- **Attribution**: all five frameworks strain to isolate one actor's contribution in multi-causal community change.
- **Proxy metrics**: SROI proxies and IRIS+ standard metrics substitute a fundable number for the thing that actually changed.
- **Extractive data collection**: outcomes are defined upstream (funder/investor), data is collected from the community, analysed elsewhere, owned elsewhere, and reported to audiences the community never meets.

## 2. Qualitative and participatory alternatives, and where they fall short

### Most Significant Change (MSC)
Davies and Dart's technique collects stories of the most significant change from participants, then uses structured panel selection and feedback loops to surface what stakeholders value (Dart & Davies 2003, search-result only: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/109821400302400202; overview verified: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Most_significant_change_technique). Criticisms: it tends to produce more positive evaluations because critics' voices may not be prioritised; selection is subjective; it is often incompletely implemented (stories collected without the verification and feedback steps); and it is time- and skill-intensive. Davies and Dart themselves recommend pairing it with conventional methods because it carries its own distinct biases (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/261287934_Most_significant_change_technique_a_supplementary_evaluation_tool, search-result only).

### Participatory evaluation and narrative-based evaluation
Participatory approaches hand evaluation questions and interpretation to affected people; narrative evaluation treats stories as primary evidence. Shortfalls raised in the literature: cherry-picking risk (the best stories get told), unclear sampling logic, weak verification steps, and difficulty aggregating stories into decisions funders can act on. MSC's own mitigations (transparent selection criteria, a documented verification step) are the field's answer to the rigour critique (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Most_significant_change_technique, verified).

The pattern: qualitative methods solved the "whose outcomes count" problem but left the rigour and ownership problems partly open. That is the gap Indigenous data sovereignty closes.

## 3. Indigenous evaluation and data sovereignty: what they REQUIRE

### Maiam nayri Wingara principles (Australia, 2018)
The Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Data Sovereignty Collective's five principles, from the 2018 Indigenous Data Sovereignty Summit (verified: https://www.maiamnayriwingara.org/mnw-principles):
1. **Control** of the data ecosystem: creation, development, stewardship, analysis, dissemination, infrastructure.
2. **Contextual** and disaggregated data access (individual, community, First Nations levels).
3. **Relevant**: data must empower sustainable self-determination and effective self-governance.
4. **Accountable**: data structures accountable to First Nations people and communities.
5. **Protective** of individual and collective interests.
Indigenous data is defined broadly: "information or knowledge, in any format or medium, which is about and may affect Indigenous peoples both collectively and individually."

### CARE principles (Global Indigenous Data Alliance, 2019)
Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics: people- and purpose-oriented principles that complement the technical FAIR principles (verified landing page: https://www.gida-global.org/careprinciples; full paper: https://datascience.codata.org/articles/10.5334/dsj-2020-043, search-result only). Authority to Control includes free, prior and informed consent in collection AND use, treated as ongoing, not a one-time signature.

### First Nations-led evaluation in Australia
- **AES First Nations Cultural Safety Framework** (commissioned 2020, launched Sept 2021; developed by Sharon Gollan and Kathleen Stacey): principles of culturally safe evaluation across all phases from design to reporting and translation, binding on commissioners, evaluators, and program staff, with critical self-reflection and cultural accountability required of non-Indigenous evaluators (verified: https://www.aes.asn.au/evaluation-learning/first-nations-cultural-safety-framework-workshops; framework PDF: https://www.aes.asn.au/images/AES_FirstNations_Cultural_Framework_finalWEB_final.pdf, search-result only).
- **Ninti One**: Indigenous-owned firm that has employed 250+ Aboriginal Community Researchers across remote Australia since 2011; community researchers participate in project design, execution AND analysis, on the argument that local knowledge, language and culture produce "more authentic, accurate and honest engagement" (https://www.nintione.com.au/about-us/aboriginal-community-researchers/, search-result only).
- Treasury's Indigenous evaluation work notes that when commissioners prioritise relationships, cultural safety and reciprocity, evaluations better support two-way learning and accountability to community (https://evaluation.treasury.gov.au/about/indigenous-evaluation, search-result only).

### What these require that mainstream impact models ignore
- **Ownership of the data asset** by the community, not the enterprise or funder (Maiam nayri Wingara Control; CARE Authority to Control).
- **Consent as ongoing and scoped to use**, not a one-time release (CARE Responsibility/Ethics; FPIC in collection and use).
- **Benefit-sharing**: the community must derive benefit from its own data (CARE Collective Benefit).
- **Community-defined outcomes**: relevance is judged by whether data empowers self-determination, not by comparability across an investor portfolio (Maiam nayri Wingara Relevant).
- **Accountability structures that face the community**, not only the funder (Maiam nayri Wingara Accountable; AES cultural accountability).

## 4. Precedents: storytelling as evidence in funding

- **Dusseldorp Forum** runs Narrative Practice workshops with place-based community partners (Kempsey, Bourke, Moree, Mt Druitt) and publishes "Storytelling for Systems Change: A Story-Kit for Changemakers"; community storytelling (e.g. Mounty Yarns, Aboriginal youth documenting justice-system experience) sits alongside data-sharing agreements (Maranguka) as evidence of change (verified: https://dusseldorp.org.au/resources/).
- **PLACE partnership** (Paul Ramsay Foundation, Minderoo, Ian Potter, Bryan Foundation, Dusseldorp with government) funds community-led, place-based change where communities articulate their own priorities and evidence (https://www.philanthropy.org.au/news-and-stories/place-government-and-philanthropy-partner-to-empower-communities/, search-result only).
- **Participatory grantmaking evidence review** (CEI, 2023) documents the philanthropic shift toward communities defining evidence and value (https://www.ceiglobal.org/sites/default/files/uploads/files/Participatory%20grantmaking%20-%20Building%20the%20evidence_finalreport.pdf, search-result only).
- Internal precedent (not web-sourced): Snow Foundation's funding relationship with Goods already operates on narrative-plus-numbers reporting; treat as internal knowledge, not a citation.

## 5. The synthesis argument: why the Goods voice-led model is defensible rigour

Why per-quote-consented, verbatim, theme-coded transcript analysis, with voices as first-class evidence alongside canonical numbers and community ownership of the narrative asset, stands up:

- **Triangulation with canonical outputs.** Every claim in a transcript can be read against the asset register's independent output numbers (beds, washers, communities). Triangulation across data sources is a core credibility strategy in the Lincoln and Guba trustworthiness canon (https://studyhealthcaretheory.com/pages/blog/57-trustworthiness-in-qualitative-research-lincoln-and-gubas-5.html, search-result only). This is exactly the pairing Davies and Dart prescribe for MSC's bias problem: stories plus conventional measures, not stories instead of them.
- **Verbatim traceability = audit trail.** Quotes are never paraphrased; each one traces to a named transcript, speaker, place and date. An auditable trail from finding back to source is the qualitative-rigour standard for dependability and confirmability (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2949916X24000045, search-result only). This directly answers the cherry-picking critique: any reader can walk from the theme code back to the full transcript context.
- **Consent provenance beats mainstream practice.** Per-quote, per-audience, revocable consent (registry-tracked, holds enforced in code) operationalises CARE's Authority to Control and FPIC-in-use, a standard ToC/SROI/IRIS+ pipelines do not even attempt.
- **Saturation across independent voices.** Themes are only asserted when they recur across multiple storytellers in multiple communities who did not hear each other. Recurrence across independent sources is the qualitative analogue of statistical power, and guards against the single-good-story failure mode of narrative evaluation.
- **Community-defined outcomes, not proxy metrics.** Theme codes emerge from what people actually said, so the outcome taxonomy is community-authored, satisfying Maiam nayri Wingara "Relevant" where SROI proxies and IRIS+ standard metrics substitute investor-defined stand-ins.
- **The narrative asset is community-owned.** Storytellers hold ongoing rights over their words (withdrawal honoured, holds respected); the transcript corpus functions as a community data asset under Maiam nayri Wingara Control/Protective, not an extraction.
- **Attribution honesty.** Voices describe contribution in the speakers' own causal terms rather than a claimed attribution coefficient; paired with claim-ceiling discipline (the scabies-to-RHD chain stays a "why", never a claimed health outcome), this is more honest than SROI's synthetic attribution percentages.
- **It composes with, not against, funder frameworks.** The model can populate IMP's Who/What/How Much from register numbers and its Contribution/Risk narrative from consented voices, so funders using mainstream frameworks lose nothing and gain evidence provenance they can audit.
