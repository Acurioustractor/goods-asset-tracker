## Part 3 — Understanding Catalytic Capital and Finding Aligned Investors

### Identifying the Right Type of Capital (13 instruments across 3 tiers)

**GRANT & GRANT-LIKE**
| Instrument | Definition |
|---|---|
| Grant | Gift of cash. Restricted (milestones/deliverables) or unrestricted. No repayment. |
| Crowdfunding Donations | Contributions from large number of people via platform |
| Pay For Success | Contract between government, service provider, investor to achieve a social outcome |
| Loan Guarantee | Third-party capital to protect investor if investment doesn't come back in full |
| Recoverable Grant | High-risk loan that will either be paid back or written off by a certain date |

**DEBT & DEBT-LIKE**
| Instrument | Definition |
|---|---|
| PRI Debt | Charitable investment by private foundations as a loan |
| Variable Repayment Debt | Loan with customised repayment terms tied to company operations |
| Debt | Standard loan that must be repaid with interest |
| Crowdfunding Debt | Borrowing from many people via platform, repaid in cash or goods/services |
| Convertible Debt | Loan that can convert to equity under certain conditions |

**EQUITY & EQUITY-LIKE**
| Instrument | Definition |
|---|---|
| PRI Equity | Charitable investment by private foundations for ownership shares |
| Equity | Capital in exchange for ownership shares |
| Crowdfunding Equity / DPO | Publicly advertising an equity opportunity to a group of investors |

### The Big Questions to Decide
- **Making Debt Repayments:** Can you make regular cash payments to investors (e.g. quarterly)?
- **Type of Ownership:** Are you planning for an exit eventually? (IPO, acquisition, buyout)
- **Valuation:** Are you willing to let an investor set a valuation?
- **Growth Rate:** Are you targeting 3-10x growth within 5 years?
- **Customisation Level:** Willing to negotiate customised terms with each investor?

### Who Are Impact Investors?

**Individuals** — (1) High Net Worth Individuals & Angels, (2) Friends & Family

**Small & Medium Institutions** — (3) Small & Large Family Offices, (4) Community & Family Foundations, (5) CDFIs & DFIs

**Individuals + Small, Medium & Large Institutions** — (6) Private Foundations, (7) Government Investment Funds, (8) International NGOs, (9) Corporates, (10) Investment Funds, (11) Institutional Investors & Banks

### Blended Finance

> "A strategy that combines the right mix of capital from multiple stakeholders who have different risk-return requirements on their capital but common impact objectives."
> — adapted from The GIIN, Big Society Capital, World Bank

**How it works:** philanthropic funding "catalyses" private capital into impact investments. Concessional (de-risking) capital from philanthropy sits alongside market-rate capital from private investors, together financing the social enterprise.

**Blended capital stack (what it could look like):**
Standard Debt (most senior) → Subordinated Debt → Common Equity → Junior Equity Position → **Grants** → **Guarantees** (least senior). Grants and guarantees provide greater protection to senior capital. If grants aren't absorbed through losses, the asset value accrues as equity.

**4 Types of Blended Finance:**
1. **Fund-level** — concessional + full-return private capital in companies' regular equity or bonds
2. **Project-level** — public/philanthropic partial funding or guarantees for large infrastructure or SDG-aligned projects ← **relevant for Goods manufacturing capex**
3. **Company-level** — public/philanthropic credit enhancement through guarantees or subsidised concessional loans ← **relevant for Goods working capital**
4. **Outcome-based** — investors in bonds/loans tied to SDG impact

**3 Benefits:**
1. **Increases availability of capital** — attracts conventional investors to impactful investments
2. **Meets needs of social enterprises** — creates more flexible products (unsecured, affordable, longer terms)
3. **Improves investment economics** — can subsidise operating costs, reduce ultimate cost

### Also Referred to as Catalytic Capital
> "Going where no one else will for the purpose of releasing other capital."

**Many possible ways:**
- **Fund earlier** — feasibility studies, seed stage
- **Fund around the edges** — learning or evaluation
- **Fund higher-risk piece on concessional terms** — first loss, guarantees, longer term / lower cost

> In chemistry, a catalyst is a material that enables a reaction, but is lost in the process.

### How Philanthropy Can Participate (Australian Tax Guidelines)
- **PAF / PuAF → DGR** — where rates charged are below market rates, PAF can be credited with a distribution for the difference
- Example: PAF charging 5% pa vs market rate of 12% pa → PAF deemed to have distributed 7% pa, required distribution reduced by that amount
- Losses on loans to DGRs are credited as distributions
- References: s.15 Public Ancillary Fund Guidelines 2022, s.15 Private Ancillary Fund Guidelines 2019

## Part 5 — Investor Alignment Tool (CASE Smart Impact Capital)

### Knockout Criteria
- **Investment Geography** — invests in places we operate or headquartered
- **Legal Structure** — invests in our legal structure (for-profit, NFP, hybrid)
- **Investment Size** — generally invests the amount we seek
- **Return Expectations** — aligns on financial/social return expectations (timeframe, exit)
- **Capital Type** — offers the type of capital (debt, equity, recoverable grant) we seek
- **Size of Growth** — invests in ventures at our stage

### Fit Criteria

**Mission and Vision**
- Social Mission — understands and supports our social mission
- Company Mission — understands and supports our vision

**Expected From Us**
- Governance — expectations match ours
- Restrictions — any restrictions on use of funds work for us
- Impact Evidence — we can provide the level of impact evidence expected

**Expected From Them**
- Connections — able and willing to make useful introductions
- Industry Experience — has experience in our industry/market segment
- Business Challenge — has experience with the specific issues we're facing
- Impact on Future Investments — any positive/negative impact on future capital raises works for us

### Debt Myth Buster
**Myth:** Debt is risky and only for struggling orgs.
**Truth:** Debt is a tool for organisations with a specific need for a loan — not a last resort.

Used appropriately, debt can:
- **Unlock growth** without giving up any ownership stake or control
- **Smooth cash flow** for seasonal or contract-based income
- **Finance assets** that generate future income (equipment, property)
- **Strengthen credibility** with other funders and banks — shows financial discipline and business maturity

> Debt doesn't make an organisation weaker — poorly managed debt does. With a clear purpose, plan, and repayment strategy, debt can help amplify impact sustainably.

### Top 3 Questions Borrowers Ask

**1. When is debt a good option?**
- Funding will help grow revenue or income
- Pty Ltds wanting to avoid giving up ownership
- Have an operating track record and demonstrated social impact

**2. Besides banks, who provides debt funding?**
- Impact investors
- Private trust funds
- Philanthropic loans
- Government (sometimes)
- Small business lenders (e.g. Prospa)
- Startup lenders (e.g. Lighter Capital, Tractor Ventures)

**3. What key documents do I need to prepare?**
- Historical financials
- Forecast financials
- Constitution
- Organisation chart
- Business plan / pitch deck
- Theory of change
- Evidence of impact measurement

> Tip: Preparing early saves time and shows lenders you're ready and serious.

### Risks of Taking On Debt (and how to manage them)

| Risk | How to Manage |
|---|---|
| Unable to service loan | Forecast cash flows conservatively; match repayments to income timing; build cash buffer |
| Restricts future flexibility | Borrow only what's needed; negotiate early repayment without penalties |
| High interest costs | Compare cost vs benefits (revenue growth, asset purchase) |
| Pressure on cash flow | Align debt to activities that generate income; avoid debt for day-to-day ops |
| Impact on org risk profile | Discuss pros/cons with board; integrate into financial governance |

> Debt isn't just for when cash runs low — it's a strategic tool to fund growth, avoid dilution, and amplify impact.

### Typical Sefa Loan Investment Process

**Initial Phase (2-4 weeks):**
- Pipeline: enquiry/referral, informal introduction call
- Initial Assessment: initial conversation, mutual understanding

**Analysis Phase (2-3 months):**
- Due Diligence & Structuring: documentation review, information gathering, scenario analysis
- Investment Committee: investment proposal, investment decision

**Execution Phase (2-4 weeks):**
- Legal Documentation: Letter of Offer, Legal Documents (Facility Deed, Mortgage, General Security Deed)
- Disbursement: drawdown conditions, tranches
- Ongoing Reporting & Management: social impact & financial, periodic investment reviews
