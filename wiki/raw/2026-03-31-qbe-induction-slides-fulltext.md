**EQUITY & EQUITY-LIKE**
- PRI Equity
- Equity
- Crowdfunding Equity / DPO

### Types of capital — definitions (adapted from CASE Smart Impact Capital)

**Grant or grant-like:**
- **Grant or Grant-Like:** Gift of cash that can be restricted (milestones/deliverables) or unrestricted; doesn't need to be repaid.
- **Crowdfunding Donations:** Raising from many people through a platform.
- **Pay For Success:** Contract between govt agency, service-providing org, and investor to accomplish a social outcome.
- **Loan Guarantee:** Third-party capital that protects an investor if their investment in you doesn't come back in full.
- **Recoverable Grant:** High-risk loan that will either be paid back or written off by a certain date.

**Debt or debt-like:**
- **PRI Debt:** Charitable investment made by private foundations as a loan.
- **Variable Repayment Debt:** Loan with customised repayment terms linked to company operations.
- **Debt:** A loan repaid, usually with interest.
- **Crowdfunding Debt:** Borrowing from many people through a platform, repayable in cash or in goods/services.
- **Convertible Debt:** Loan that can be (and is intended to be) turned into equity under certain conditions, usually at future investment rounds.

**Equity or equity-like:**
- **PRI Equity:** Charitable investment by private foundations in exchange for shares.
- **Equity:** Capital in exchange for ownership shares.
- **Crowdfunding Equity / DPO:** Advertising an equity opportunity publicly.

### The Big Questions to Decide

- **Making Debt Repayments:** Can you make regular cash payments (e.g. quarterly)?
- **Type of Ownership:** Planning for an exit (IPO, acquisition, buyout)?
- **Valuation:** Willing to let an investor set a valuation?
- **Growth Rate:** Targeting 3-10x or more in 5 years?
- **Customisation Level:** Willing to negotiate customised terms per investor?

## BLENDED FINANCE

**Definition (GIIN, Big Society Capital, World Bank):**
> A strategy that combines the right mix of capital from multiple stakeholders who have different risk-return requirements on their capital but common impact objectives.

Often framed as the strategic use of philanthropic funding to "catalyse" private capital into impact investments.

### Capital stack visualisation (regular vs blended)

**Regular finance stack:**
- Standard Debt (most seniority, most protection)
- Subordinated Debt
- Common Equity (least seniority)

**Blended finance stack (same base plus):**
- Standard Debt
- Subordinated Debt
- Common Equity
- Junior Equity Position
- Grants
- Guarantees (least seniority, most risk, protects everyone below)

Grants and guarantees provide "greater protection" to the more senior layers, allowing commercial investors to participate at their required risk-return profile.

### 4 types of blended finance

1. **Fund-level** — concessional + commercial capital combined in one fund.
2. **Project-level** — partial funding / guarantees for large infrastructure or energy projects contributing to SDGs (most relevant to Goods' manufacturing plant).
3. **Company-level** — credit enhancement (guarantees, subsidised concessional loans) on below-market terms.
4. **Outcome-based** — corporate bonds / loans tied to SDG impact in use-of-proceeds or outcome achievement.

### 3 benefits of blended finance

1. **Increases availability of capital** — attracts conventional investors into impactful investments.
2. **Meets the needs of social enterprises** — more flexible products (unsecured, affordable, longer terms).
3. **Improves investment economics** — can subsidise fund operating costs so economics stack up.

### Catalytic capital

> "Going where no one else will, for the purpose of releasing other capital."

Possible forms:
- **Fund earlier** — feasibility studies, seed stage.
- **Fund around the edges** — learning, evaluation.
- **Fund higher-risk piece on concessional terms** — first loss, guarantees, longer-term lower-cost.

Chemistry analogy: a catalyst enables a reaction but is lost in the process.

### How philanthropy can participate (PAF / PuAF to DGR)

- Where rates charged are below market rates, the PAF can be credited with a distribution for the difference.
- Example: PAF charging 5% p.a., market 12% p.a., PAF deemed to have distributed 7% p.a., required distribution reduced accordingly.
- Losses on loans to DGRs also credited as distributions.
- **References:** PuAF — Section 15 of the Public Ancillary Fund Guidelines 2022; PAF — Section 15 of the Private Ancillary Fund Guidelines 2019.

## AUSTRALIAN EXAMPLES — Blended finance in practice

### Dismantle / WV Tech (illustrative)
- $1m Debt Facility (First Australians Capital)
- $750K Equity Investments
- $400K Catalysing Impact Grant
- **Total: $2.25m**

### Victoria Theatre Revival (illustrative)
- $3.03m Century Group
- $4.1m NSW Government Grant
- $1m Activating State Heritage Grant
- $10m Donations (depending on investment)
- $6m Equity Investment in Operating Company (depending on donations)
- **Total: $24.13m**

### Jigsaw
- Social enterprise providing pathway to open employment for people with disability.
- Total raise: $3M (70% debt $2.1m, 30% grants $0.9m).
- Syndicate agent: SEFA.
- Investors: Westpac Foundation, Paul Ramsay Foundation, Scalzo Foundation, Snow Foundation, Impact Generation Partners, The RW Fenton Family Trust, The Bryan Foundation, Lord Mayor's Charitable Foundation, Ian Potter Foundation.
- **Snow Foundation is already a Goods funder** → potential co-investment connector.

## Additional context (from session 1 notes)

- SEFA has $9m undeployed capital.
- SEFA's track record: 75-80 loans, only 3 with financial difficulty (2 were fraud, 1 was ignorance).
- 40%+ of SEFA clients repaid early (no penalty).
- SEFA requires independent board directors (majority) or at minimum an advisory group.
- SEFA process: Initial 2-4 weeks → Analysis 2-3 months (DD, structuring, Investment Committee) → Execution 2-4 weeks (legal, disbursement, reporting).
